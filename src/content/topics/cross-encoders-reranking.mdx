## Overview & motivation

[Dense retrieval](/topics/dense-retrieval-dual-encoders) ended on a debt. Its whole architecture rested on *separability* — a dual encoder scores $s(q,d) = \langle E_Q(q), E_P(d)\rangle$, query and document meeting in a single inner product — and three times over the topic named the thing separability forbids. It promised that "the **cross-encoder** that this topic kept invoking as the rank-free, un-precomputable counterpoint becomes the reranker in a retrieve-then-rerank cascade," and deferred *how that extra power is spent* to "a later topic." The [embedding-dimension lower bound](/topics/embedding-dimension-lower-bounds) sharpened the debt into an impossibility — the single-vector wall it proved is one "cross-encoders … pay a quadratic cost to fuse query and document and so are **not factorizations at all**" route around. And [late interaction](/topics/late-interaction-learned-sparse) set the cross-encoder as the far pole of the expressivity axis: where ColBERT keeps matching cheap with a sum of token dot products, "a cross-encoder pays a full joint forward pass for the **most expressive score of all**." This is that topic. We pay the debt, and we charge it.

A **cross-encoder** scores a *fused* pair, $s(q,d) = h([q;d])$, letting query and document attend to each other at every layer rather than meeting once at the end. The first result is the one that explains why this matters at all, and it is more subtle than "more layers, more power." A *learned bilinear* scorer $q^\top W d$ — a dual encoder with a trained similarity metric bolted on — does **not** escape the rank ceiling, because $S = QWG^\top = (QW)G^\top$ is still a product through a $d$-dimensional bottleneck. The escape is not the interaction matrix. It is the *nonlinearity*. Only a genuinely nonlinear joint scorer has no rank to bound, and that is exactly what lifts the cross-encoder out of the sign-rank wall.

That expressivity is bought with a forward pass per pair, which forbids precomputation and rules the cross-encoder out as a first stage. So it becomes a **reranker**: a cheap dual encoder retrieves a candidate pool of $K$ documents, the cross-encoder rescores only those $K$. Two facts govern the cascade. The **recall pinch** says an oracle rerank of the top-$K$ makes recall@1 equal the pool's recall@$K$ — it can lift the first stage, but never past what the first stage retrieved. And **monotonicity**: an oracle reranker only improves as $K$ grows, but a *lossy* one can dip below the first stage by being confidently wrong, and more over-fetch can make the dip worse.

## The cross-encoder and the cascade

Fix a query $q$ and a corpus $\mathcal{C} = \{d_1, \dots, d_n\}$. A dual encoder, as the previous topic established, scores by a separable inner product and so realizes only relevance matrices of bounded rank. We define its expressive opposite and the structure that makes it deployable.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Cross-encoder and reranking cascade">

A **cross-encoder** is a map $h : \mathcal{Q} \times \mathcal{C} \to \mathbb{R}$ that scores a *fused* pair, $s(q,d) = h([q;d])$, with no requirement that the score factor through separate query and document representations. A **retrieve-then-rerank cascade** is the two-stage pipeline: a cheap first stage (here a dual encoder) returns the top-$K$ documents $\mathcal{T}_K(q) = \operatorname{top\text{-}}K_j\, s_1(q, d_j)$ by its own score $s_1$, and the cross-encoder rescores only that pool, returning $\operatorname{argmax}_{d \in \mathcal{T}_K(q)} h([q;d])$. The first stage's job is **recall** (keep the gold document in the pool); the reranker's is **precision** (surface it within the pool).

</TheoremBlock>

The two stages are not interchangeable. The first stage must be precomputable to run over the whole corpus, so it must be a factorization; the reranker need only run over $K$ documents, so it can afford to fuse. We take $s_1$ to be the dual encoder of the previous topic and ask, in order: how much more can a fused scorer express, what does the cascade buy, and how can it fail.

## Movement 1 — joint encoding breaks the rank ceiling, and the nonlinearity is why

The previous topic proved that a dual encoder's score matrix $S = QG^\top$ has $\operatorname{rank}(S) \le d$, and that a target relevance matrix is realizable exactly if and only if its rank is at most $d$. The natural first guess at escaping this ceiling is to keep the two towers but let them interact through a learned matrix — a bilinear score $q^\top W d$, a trained Mahalanobis similarity. The first result is that this guess fails completely.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="A learned bilinear stays rank-bounded">

Let $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$ and $G \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ stack query and document embeddings, and let $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ be any learned interaction matrix. The bilinear score matrix $S = Q W G^\top$ satisfies $\operatorname{rank}(S) \le d$ — the same ceiling as the plain dual encoder. Moreover the best bilinear realization of any target equals the best plain dual encoder's: both are the truncated SVD.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

Absorb $W$ into the query tower: $S = QWG^\top = (QW)G^\top$, a product of an $m \times d$ matrix with an $n \times d$ matrix, so $\operatorname{rank}(S) \le \min(\operatorname{rank}(QW), \operatorname{rank}(G^\top)) \le d$. The learned bilinear is *literally* a dual encoder with reparametrized query embeddings $Q' = QW$ — the interaction matrix changes the embeddings, not the realizable set. For the second claim, the set of matrices expressible as $QWG^\top$ over all $(Q, W, G)$ is exactly $\{\,M : \operatorname{rank}(M) \le d\,\}$: taking $Q = U_d$, $G = V_d$ the top-$d$ singular vectors of a target $M$ and $W = \Sigma_d$ its top-$d$ singular values gives $QWG^\top = U_d \Sigma_d V_d^\top$, the truncated SVD, which Eckart–Young makes the optimal rank-$d$ approximation. No bilinear can do better, and no plain dual encoder does worse. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is the pedagogical hinge of the topic. Adding a learned similarity metric to a pair of frozen encoders feels like buying expressivity; it buys none. Whatever escapes the rank ceiling, it is not the interaction term — it is leaving the bilinear family altogether. A fused, *nonlinear* scorer does exactly that.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="A joint scorer has no rank ceiling">

For any target relevance matrix $M \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ there exists a cross-encoder $h$ with $h([q_i; d_j]) = M_{ij}$ for all $i, j$, regardless of $\operatorname{rank}(M)$. The realizable set of a fused scorer is *every* score table, unbounded in rank.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

A cross-encoder reads the pair $[q_i; d_j]$ jointly, so it can in principle assign each pair an independent value; the function $h$ that maps the pair $(q_i, d_j)$ to $M_{ij}$ is well-defined for any $M$. There is no factorization to bottleneck the rank, because there is no factorization at all — this is the sense in which, as the embedding-dimension topic put it, a cross-encoder is *not a factorization*. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The theorem is, by itself, almost trivial: a lookup table realizes any matrix. Its force is the contrast with Proposition 1 and the *constructive* demonstration the laboratory makes. We take the [signed-identity target](/topics/embedding-dimension-lower-bounds) — full rank $n$, $+1$ on the diagonal and $-1$ off it, the cleanest full-rank pattern that topic studied — and reconstruct it three ways as $d$ grows. The rank-$d$ ceiling (the best dual encoder, which is also the best learned bilinear) has relative reconstruction error that descends from $0.745$ at $d=1$ only to $0$ at $d = n = 6$, plateauing strictly above zero for every $d < n$. A *finite* nonlinear cross-encoder — a random-ReLU-feature map on the fused pair with a closed-form ridge readout, our deterministic stand-in for $h$ — reconstructs the target to machine precision at *every* $d$, including $d=1$, and its realized score matrix has full rank $6$. The cross-encoder has no embedding budget to spend; it does not factorize, so there is nothing to bound. (That this finite net realizes the pattern is *demonstrated*: universal approximation guarantees a realizing network exists only asymptotically, and our scorer is a seeded surrogate, not a trained transformer — the load-bearing caveat below.)

The same expressivity shows on the finance geometry. Fit the cross-encoder on the corpus's query–document pairs and it realizes the relevance **exactly** — recall@1 $= 1.0$ — separating the same-sector companies a rank-3 dual encoder ($0.781$ recall@1) confuses. The cross-encoder can resolve hard negatives a single embedding cannot. The catch is that it cannot do so cheaply.

## Movement 2 — the retrieve-then-rerank cascade and the recall pinch

A cross-encoder must run a joint forward pass for every pair it scores, and it cannot precompute anything, so scoring the whole corpus costs $|\mathcal{C}|$ forward passes per query — exactly the cost the dual encoder was built to avoid. The cascade resolves the tension by spending that cost only on a small pool: retrieve $K$ candidates cheaply, rescore $K$ expensively.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The cascade cost law">

Let the first stage cost $c_\text{ret}$ per query (one encode plus a MIPS lookup) and let each cross-encoder forward pass cost $c_\text{ce}$. Reranking the top-$K$ pool costs

$$
\text{cost}_\text{cascade} = c_\text{ret} + K\,c_\text{ce}, \qquad \text{against} \qquad \text{cost}_\text{brute} = |\mathcal{C}|\,c_\text{ce},
$$

so the cross-encoder's per-query work is constant in the corpus size, set by the over-fetch depth $K$ rather than $|\mathcal{C}|$. The over-fetch needed to keep the gold in the pool with retention $r = $ recall@$K$ is the [imported](/topics/capstone-multimodal-financial-rag) factor $1/r$.

</TheoremBlock>

The cost is exact arithmetic; the question is how large $K$ must be. That is where the cascade's defining identity lives. Because the reranker only ever sees the pool, its ceiling is set entirely by what the first stage retrieved.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="The recall pinch">

Under a known-item qrel — one gold document $g_q$ per query — let an **oracle** reranker (one that ranks the gold first whenever it is present) rescore the first stage's top-$K$ pool. Then

$$
\text{recall@1}_{\text{after}} \;=\; \text{recall@}K_{\text{stage-1}}.
$$

The oracle lifts recall@1 from the first stage's own recall@1 up to its recall@$K$, and caps it there exactly.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

The oracle ranks $g_q$ first among any pool that contains it, by definition. So the post-rerank top-1 is $g_q$ if and only if $g_q \in \mathcal{T}_K(q)$. Averaging the indicator over queries,

$$
\text{recall@1}_\text{after} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{Q}|}\sum_q \mathbf{1}[g_q \in \mathcal{T}_K(q)] = \text{recall@}K_\text{stage-1},
$$

the middle expression being precisely [recall@$K$](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr) with $|R| = 1$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The pinch is the whole logic of over-fetch in one line. The reranker's reach is the pool's recall@$K$, so you fetch deep enough that recall@$K$ saturates, then rerank. On the finance toy the rank-3 first stage has recall@1 $= 0.781$ but recall@3 $= 1.0$ — it confuses companies at the very top but keeps the gold within the top three — so reranking a pool of $K = 3$ lifts recall@1 all the way to $1.0$. The laboratory's second panel draws the oracle curve tracing the pool's recall@$K$ exactly, with the cost climbing as $c_\text{ret} + K c_\text{ce}$ against the brute corpus-scan line orders of magnitude above: the knee where recall@$K$ saturates is where you stop. One corollary is worth stating, because it is the reason the first stage's recall is the cascade's true bottleneck.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="The cascade ceiling">

For *any* reranker — oracle or lossy — $\text{recall@1}_\text{after} \le \text{recall@}K_\text{stage-1}$. A reranker can only reorder the pool; it can never recover a gold document the first stage dropped.

</TheoremBlock>

Under the known-item qrel the recall pinch is an exact identity; with several relevant documents per query the oracle can surface only one at rank 1, and the identity weakens to the inequality of the corollary. We ship the topic on the known-item qrel, where the equality holds and recall@1 is the natural reranking accuracy.

## Movement 3 — monotonicity, and when reranking hurts

The recall pinch is about a *perfect* reranker. Two facts separate the perfect case from the real one. The first is reassuring.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="Oracle rerank is recall-monotone in K">

An oracle reranker's recall@1 is non-decreasing in the over-fetch depth $K$: deepening the pool never lowers accuracy.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

The top-$(K+1)$ pool is a superset of the top-$K$ pool, $\mathcal{T}_K(q) \subseteq \mathcal{T}_{K+1}(q)$. If the gold $g_q$ was in $\mathcal{T}_K(q)$ it is still in $\mathcal{T}_{K+1}(q)$, and the oracle still ranks it first; the only new event a larger pool admits is $g_q$ entering the pool, which can only turn a miss into a hit. So $\text{recall@1}_\text{after}(K+1) \ge \text{recall@1}_\text{after}(K)$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is the same superset argument the [multi-vector ANN](/topics/multi-vector-ann-retrieval) topic used for an exact rerank over a pruned survivor set, and it holds for exactly the same reason — but, exactly as there, only for an *exact* reranker. A real cross-encoder is not exact, and the second fact is the warning.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="A lossy cross-encoder can dip below the first stage">

A reranker that is confidently wrong — that assigns a same-sector distractor a higher score than the true document — *breaks* queries the first stage got right, dropping recall@1 below the first stage's own. And because more over-fetch hands such a reranker more chances to err, the dip can *deepen* as $K$ grows: the anti-monotone direction, the sharpest possible contrast to the oracle's guarantee.

</TheoremBlock>

The laboratory's third panel makes this a slider. Model the reranker quality by a corruption level $\sigma$ added to the oracle's scores; at $\sigma = 0$ it is the oracle and fixes all seven same-sector hard negatives the first stage missed, a net recall@1 lift of $+0.219$. As $\sigma$ grows the buckets shift — *fixed* falls, *broke* rises — and the net lift turns negative; a constructed worst-case promoter that always elevates a same-sector distractor breaks every recoverable query. The dip is not a bug in the model. It is the score-versus-ranking gap made operational: a high cross-encoder *score* on the wrong document is a wrong *ranking*, and no amount of over-fetch protects against it. This is why a production stack keeps an exact scorer as ground truth and why the monotonicity guarantee is the oracle's alone.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="Expressivity is paid for in samples">

The capacity that lets the cross-encoder realize any relevance table is exactly the capacity that overfits a small training set. A fused scorer is a high-complexity hypothesis class; by the [VC-dimension](/topics/embedding-dimension-lower-bounds) and generalization-bound machinery of learning theory, more expressive scorers need more labeled pairs to close the generalization gap. The dual encoder's separable inner product is a strong *inductive bias* — a low-complexity restriction that generalizes from fewer examples and wins below a crossover sample size. We state this rather than simulate it: the expressivity our laboratory measures is realized on the full relevance, where train and test coincide, and the sample-complexity story belongs to a learning-curve protocol this topic does not run.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="What the laboratory measures">

Every number in the three panels is owned by a tested notebook and asserted by the harness: the rank-ceiling reconstruction errors (the ceiling positive for $d <$ rank, the cross-encoder near zero at every $d$); the recall pinch (oracle rerank recall@1 equal to the first stage's recall@$K$ to floating point at every $K$); oracle monotonicity in $K$; the lossy dip below the first stage and its deepening with $K$; and the collapse anchors — reranking the whole corpus ($K = |\mathcal{C}|$) equals the brute-force argmax, and the interpolating cross-encoder matches the imported oracle. The laboratory recomputes only closed forms in the browser: the cost arithmetic $c_\text{ret} + K c_\text{ce}$ and its speedup, and the net lift from the bucket counts.

</TheoremBlock>

<FinanceCaseStudy title="The reranker that pages the desk" modality={["text", "pdf", "news"]}>

A production retrieval system over financial filings, earnings calls, and analyst notes runs a dual encoder first: it encodes the whole document corpus once, offline, and answers a query with one MIPS lookup. That stage is fast and high-recall, but it confuses *same-sector hard negatives* — two refiners, two regional banks, two payment networks whose filings share boilerplate and sit a fraction of a radian apart on the sphere. A single embedding cannot resolve them; the [rank ceiling](/topics/embedding-dimension-lower-bounds) says so. So a **cross-encoder reranker** sits behind the first stage, rescoring the top few hundred hits where its quadratic cost is affordable — and, as the dual-encoder topic put it, *it can never be the first stage*. The recall pinch is the operational rule the desk lives by: the reranker can only surface what the retriever fetched, so the over-fetch depth is tuned until recall@$K$ saturates on held-out queries, and no deeper — every extra candidate is a cross-encoder forward pass. And the confident-wrong dip is the failure the desk fears most: a reranker that scores the wrong refiner's filing highest does not merely miss, it *replaces* a correct retrieval with a wrong one, which is why the pipeline keeps an exact scorer as ground truth and audits the reranker against it.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Honest accounting

<RigorFlag>

The architectural results are exact; their reach is narrower than the demonstrations suggest. That a learned bilinear scorer $q^\top W d$ cannot escape the rank ceiling is a theorem — $W$ absorbs into one tower, leaving a rank-$\le d$ dual encoder — and the recall pinch is an exact identity, but only under a known-item qrel with one gold document per query; with several relevant documents it weakens to the corollary's inequality. That a joint scorer has *no* rank ceiling is exact for the oracle (any score table is a function of the fused pair), but the claim that a *trained* nonlinear model realizes a given pattern is **demonstrated, not proved**: the cross-encoder in the laboratory is a finite random-ReLU-feature scorer with a closed-form ridge head, a deterministic seeded stand-in, not a trained transformer, and universal approximation guarantees a realizing network exists only asymptotically. Monotonicity of recall in $K$ is a theorem for the *exact* (oracle) reranker only; the lossy reranker is modeled as the oracle's scores plus seeded Gaussian corruption (and a constructed confident-wrong promoter), so its dip is a demonstration, not a bound. The cascade's cost advantage is exact arithmetic, but its accuracy gain is measured on the synthetic von Mises–Fisher finance cloud reused from the InfoNCE and DPR topics, restricted to a rank-3 first stage so the first stage is imperfect-but-recoverable — at full embedding width the toy is already perfect at rank 1 and the cascade has nothing to lift. The expressivity that breaks the rank ceiling is paid for in sample complexity; the inner-product restriction is a strong inductive bias, a claim we state and up-link to learning theory rather than prove.

</RigorFlag>

## Where this sits

The cross-encoder closes the expressivity ladder the dense track climbed — single vector, multi-vector late interaction, fused joint scorer — and opens the reranking sub-track. Its cost is the lever the next topics pull. **Knowledge distillation for retrieval** uses the cross-encoder as a *teacher*: its margins supervise a cheap student dual encoder, so the expressive scorer's judgments are compressed back into a precomputable first stage — the MarginMSE recipe, the practical answer to "the reranker is too slow to run everywhere." **Listwise LLM rerankers** push the joint-scoring idea further, scoring a whole candidate list in one context rather than one pair at a time, trading the cross-encoder's pairwise forward passes for a single sequence model that reasons over the pool. And **hard-negative mining** is the training-side counterpart of the hard negatives this topic's reranker exists to resolve: the same same-sector confusions that the cross-encoder fixes at inference are the negatives a first stage should be trained against. Each is named here and developed in its own topic; the cross-encoder is the architecture they all take as given.
