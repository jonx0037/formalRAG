## Overview & motivation

The inverted file cut the space into Voronoi cells and searched the few nearest. This topic replaces the flat partition with a *graph*: each vector is a node connected to some of its neighbors, and search is a *walk* — start somewhere, and repeatedly step to whichever neighbor is closest to the query. The question that makes this mathematics rather than engineering is sharp: when is such a graph *searchable* by greedy hops at all?

The answer comes in two movements. The first is a theorem of Kleinberg's about *navigability* — the surprising fact that a graph's searchability by a greedy, decentralized walk depends entirely on how its long-range links are distributed, and that there is exactly one distribution that works. The second turns that idea into a working index, the navigable small-world graph, built not on a lattice but on real embedding vectors. The laboratory below lets you sweep the link distribution and watch greedy routing collapse, then walk a small graph and watch a greedy search get stuck.

The first panel is Kleinberg's U-curve: greedy routing is fast only when the long-range exponent matches the dimension. The second is the honest catch of graph search — a greedy walk halting at a local minimum that a wider beam escapes. The third is the recall-versus-work frontier the index trades along.

## Movement 1 — the mathematics of greedy routing

Kleinberg's setting is deliberately idealized. Take a $d$-dimensional lattice — nodes on a grid, each joined to its immediate lattice neighbors — and give every node *one* additional long-range link, whose endpoint is chosen at random with probability proportional to $r^{-\alpha}$, where $r$ is the lattice distance and $\alpha \ge 0$ is a parameter. *Greedy routing* delivers a message from a source to a target using only local information: at each node, forward to whichever neighbor (local or long-range) is closest to the target in lattice distance. The question is how the expected number of hops scales with the number of nodes $n$, as a function of $\alpha$.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Kleinberg's navigability theorem">

On the $d$-dimensional lattice augmented with one long-range link per node drawn $\propto r^{-\alpha}$, decentralized greedy routing has expected delivery time

$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{hops}] =
\begin{cases}
O\!\left(\log^2 n\right), & \alpha = d, \\[4pt]
\Omega\!\left(n^{\beta(\alpha,d)}\right), & \alpha \ne d,
\end{cases}
$$

for a positive exponent $\beta(\alpha, d) > 0$. The graph is *navigable* — routable in polylogarithmic hops by a purely local algorithm — if and only if the long-range link exponent equals the lattice dimension, $\alpha = d$. No decentralized algorithm achieves polylogarithmic delivery for any $\alpha \ne d$.

</TheoremBlock>

The condition $\alpha = d$ is not arbitrary; it is the unique exponent that makes the long-range links *scale-free*. Partition the distances to the target into geometric scales — nodes at distance between $2^j$ and $2^{j+1}$. Under $\alpha = d$, the probability that a node's long-range link lands in a given scale is roughly the same for *every* scale, because the number of nodes in a scale (growing like $r^d$) exactly cancels the $r^{-d}$ weight. A message can therefore expect, at every scale, a link that halves its remaining distance, and there are only $\log n$ scales to descend, each costing $O(\log n)$ — hence $O(\log^2 n)$. If $\alpha > d$ the links are too short to cross scales quickly; if $\alpha < d$ they are too uniform to home in. Either way, decentralized routing stalls.

We demonstrate the theorem on the one-dimensional ring, where $d = 1$ and the optimum is $\alpha = 1$. The companion code routes between random pairs and traces the U-shaped delivery-time curve:

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The navigability U-curve, on the ring">

On a ring of $n = 20{,}000$ nodes, mean greedy delivery time is minimized near $\alpha = 1$ (about $55$ hops) and rises sharply away from it — to about $123$ hops at $\alpha = 0$ (links too uniform) and over $1{,}500$ hops at $\alpha = 2$ (links too local), a roughly twenty-eight-fold penalty. The trough sits at the dimension.

</TheoremBlock>

One honesty note belongs here: at finite $n$ the empirical trough is very *near* $\alpha = 1$, not pinned exactly to it — the exact-dimension optimum is an asymptotic statement, and the simulated minimum can fall a little to either side. The shape, and the dramatic degradation away from the dimension, are the robust facts.

## Movement 2 — the navigable small-world graph

Kleinberg's lattice explains *why* a graph can be navigable, but real data is not a lattice. The navigable small-world graph (NSW) is the construction that reproduces the small-world property — short paths, local clustering — over arbitrary embedding vectors, with no coordinates and no explicit link law.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="NSW construction and greedy search">

Insert the database vectors one at a time in random order. When inserting $x$, run a greedy search of the graph built so far to find its $\mathrm{ef}_{\text{construction}}$ approximate nearest neighbors, and connect $x$ bidirectionally to the $M$ nearest of them. Because the earliest insertions are linked when the graph is sparse, their edges span large distances and become the long-range hubs; later insertions add local, short-range edges — so the small-world structure emerges from the *insertion order*, not from a lattice.

To search for a query $q$, perform greedy beam descent from an entry node: keep the $\mathrm{ef}$ best candidates found so far, repeatedly expand the nearest unexplored one, and stop when the nearest candidate is farther than the worst kept result. $\mathrm{ef} = 1$ is pure greedy hill-climbing.

</TheoremBlock>

The construction makes the graph a small world in the measured sense: on a cloud of $500$ vectors the mean shortest-path length between random nodes is about $2.9$ hops — close to $\log n \approx 6.2$ and far below any linear scaling. The long-range hubs are what collapse the diameter.

Greedy search inherits the navigability of that structure, but with a genuine catch.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Greedy search reaches a local minimum; the beam escapes it">

Pure greedy hill-climbing ($\mathrm{ef} = 1$) moves to the neighbor nearest the query while one exists, and stops at the first node whose every neighbor is farther from the query — a *local minimum* of the distance-to-query function over the graph. That node need not be the true nearest neighbor, so greedy recall is strictly below $1$. Widening the beam to $\mathrm{ef} > 1$ retains enough alternative candidates to step around local minima, and recall is non-decreasing in $\mathrm{ef}$, reaching $1$ as the beam grows.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

A greedy step strictly decreases the distance to the query, so the walk is monotone and terminates at a node $u$ with $\lVert u - q \rVert \le \lVert v - q \rVert$ for every neighbor $v$ of $u$ — a local minimum by definition. Whether $u$ is the global minimum (the true nearest neighbor) depends on the graph's connectivity near $q$; when it is not, greedy returns a wrong answer, so recall $< 1$. The beam search keeps the $\mathrm{ef}$ best candidates and expands each, so its visited set is a superset of the pure-greedy path; enlarging $\mathrm{ef}$ only enlarges that set, and a candidate that reaches the true neighbor's basin is never discarded while a worse one is kept. Hence recall cannot decrease in $\mathrm{ef}$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

On the synthetic cloud the frontier is steep: recall@10 climbs from $10\%$ at $\mathrm{ef} = 1$ to $99.8\%$ at $\mathrm{ef} = 16$, while the number of distance computations grows from about $72$ to $134$ — well under a third of the $500$ vectors. The search is sublinear in the database, and the beam width is the knob that buys recall with work, exactly as $n_{\text{probe}}$ did for the inverted file.

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text"]}>

A graph index is what most production vector databases actually run. For a financial-retrieval corpus of millions of embeddings, the navigable small-world graph reaches a query's neighbors in a number of hops that grows like the logarithm of the corpus, touching a few hundred vectors rather than millions — and the beam width $\mathrm{ef}$ is the latency-versus-recall dial an operator turns, the graph-search counterpart of the inverted file's $n_{\text{probe}}$. The honest operational facts are the ones the mathematics names: greedy search can stall at a local minimum, so recall is never guaranteed and is monitored, not assumed; the graph's navigability is an empirical property of the construction, not a theorem about the data; and the entry point and degree $M$ are tuning choices. On the synthetic cloud the same trade is visible in miniature — a beam of sixteen recovers nearly all of the recall at a quarter of the work — and it is the structure the next topic, the hierarchical navigable small-world graph, layers for a logarithmic entry point and a bounded degree.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Honest accounting

<RigorFlag>

Graph search rests on one theorem and several measured directions. Kleinberg's theorem is exact, but it is about the idealized lattice with the precise $r^{-d}$ link law, and its polylogarithmic guarantee holds asymptotically; at finite $n$ the delivery-time trough sits very near, not exactly at, $\alpha = d$, and the hop counts reported are for a specific ring size. The practical navigable small-world graph is *not* that lattice: it is built heuristically by incremental insertion over real vectors, so its navigability is empirical and inherits no guarantee from the theorem — the theorem is the intuition for why the construction can work, not a proof that it does. Greedy search returns a local minimum of the distance-to-query function, so its recall is below one and depends on the beam width $\mathrm{ef}$, the degree $M$, and the entry node; we assert the direction of each effect on a synthetic low-rank cloud and never promise a universal recall. The small-world path-length and sublinear-work figures are measured properties of the constructed graph at one size, not asymptotic claims.

</RigorFlag>

The inverted file and the navigable small-world graph are the two great families of sublinear vector search — partition the space, or walk a graph. Both leave the same opening: a single flat structure, whether one layer of cells or one layer of graph, with a fixed entry. The hierarchical navigable small-world graph closes it, stacking the graph into layers so that search enters at a coarse level and refines — the index the next topic builds.
