## From a number with error bars to a guarantee you can sign

The evaluation layer built one through-line. A retrieval metric is not a property of a system but an
**estimator** — a sample mean of per-query scores with a standard error
([set metrics](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr)), generalized to graded relevance
([NDCG](/topics/ndcg-discount-geometry)), then tested, calibrated, and monitored for drift
([significance, calibration, drift](/topics/significance-testing-calibration)). When generation entered,
[the LLM judge](/topics/llm-as-judge-ragas) became the instrument: a noisy, biased rater whose verdicts we
debiased and whose confidence we calibrated into a probability that a claim is supported.

Every one of those topics produced a **number**. This one produces a **guarantee**. The question shifts
from *how good is the estimate* to *can we promise something about the answer we are about to emit* — and
promise it without a model of when the language model hallucinates, without assuming the score is
Gaussian, without trusting that the test distribution matches the one we calibrated on more than the bare
minimum. That minimum is **exchangeability**, and the machinery that extracts a finite-sample guarantee
from it is **conformal prediction**. The companion formalML topic on
[conformal prediction](https://formalml.com/topics/conformal-prediction) proves its central theorem in
full; we reuse that theorem and build the RAG-specific construction on top.

> Conformal prediction converts **any** nonconformity score into a finite-sample coverage guarantee under
> exchangeability alone. The score is the design lever. We make the score the calibrated judge confidence
> that a claim is faithful, and the guarantee becomes a statement about which claims we are entitled to
> emit.

## The score, the back-off, and the recall guarantee

The prerequisite gives us, for each (query, claim) pair, the judge's calibrated confidence
$\tilde c \in [0,1]$ that the claim is *supported* — the output of Platt or isotonic recalibration applied
to the raw judge probability. We turn confidence into a conformal **nonconformity score** by the obvious
inversion.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The faithfulness nonconformity score">
For a claim with calibrated judge confidence $\tilde c \in [0,1]$ that it is supported, the
**nonconformity score** is
$$
s \;=\; 1 - \tilde c.
$$
A genuinely faithful claim should draw high confidence, hence a **low** score; an unsupported claim should
draw low confidence, hence a **high** score — matching the conformal convention that the score is large
when the example is anomalous. The orientation is load-bearing: scoring $s = \tilde c$ instead would make
the sublevel set $\{s \le \hat\tau\}$ the *least*-confident claims, retaining exactly the wrong ones.
</TheoremBlock>

A generated answer is a set of $k$ sub-claims with calibrated confidences
$\tilde c_{(1)} \ge \tilde c_{(2)} \ge \cdots \ge \tilde c_{(k)}$. The decision is not "accept or reject the
whole answer" but "which claims may we keep" — and the conformal set is built by **backing off** from the
least confident.

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={1} title="Per-claim back-off">
Given a threshold $\hat\tau$ on the score, the **retained claim set** for an answer is
$$
\hat C_\alpha \;=\; \{\, i : s_i \le \hat\tau \,\} \;=\; \{\, i : \tilde c_i \ge 1 - \hat\tau \,\}.
$$
Equivalently: sort the claims by descending confidence and drop from the bottom every claim below the
cut $\tau = 1 - \hat\tau$. The retained set is either nonempty (we emit it) or empty (we **abstain**). This
is the construction of Mohri & Hashimoto (2024) and C-RAG (Kang et al. 2024), here applied per claim to the
judge confidence.
</TheoremBlock>

The threshold is set on a held-out calibration set the way split conformal always sets it — as an order
statistic of the calibration scores — but restricted to the subpopulation whose retention we want to
guarantee.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="The calibration procedure">
Hold out $n_{\mathrm{cal}}$ calibration claims with observed truth labels $Y_i \in \{0,1\}$. **Restrict to
the truly-faithful claims** $\{i : Y_i = 1\}$; let $n_1 = \#\{i : Y_i = 1\}$, and compute their scores
$S_i = 1 - \tilde c_i$. The threshold is
$$
\hat\tau \;=\; \big\lceil (1-\alpha)(n_1 + 1) \big\rceil\text{-th smallest of } \{S_i : Y_i = 1\},
$$
capped at $+\infty$ when the rank exceeds $n_1$ (then $\tau = -\infty$ and we retain everything: the
honest "cannot certify at this $\alpha$" outcome). The score depends only on the training-fitted judge and
recalibrator, never on the calibration or test data — the hypothesis the coverage theorem requires.
</TheoremBlock>

This is exactly the split-conformal threshold, so the marginal-coverage theorem applies verbatim. We state
the consequence and are careful about *what* it guarantees.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="Faithful-claim retention (from formalML's Theorem 1)">
Let the truly-faithful calibration claims and a truly-faithful test claim be exchangeable. With $\hat\tau$
from Definition 2,
$$
\mathbb{P}\big(\text{test claim retained} \;\big|\; \text{test claim faithful}\big) \;\ge\; 1 - \alpha,
$$
and under score continuity at most $1 - \alpha + \tfrac{1}{n_1 + 1}$. This is a **recall** guarantee: at
most a fraction $\alpha$ of genuinely faithful claims are wrongly dropped.
</TheoremBlock>

The direction matters enough to state what the corollary does **not** say. It is tempting to read "$\alpha$
controls the error" as "$\alpha$ controls the hallucination rate among retained claims." That is false. The
recall guarantee bounds the rate at which we discard *true* content; it says nothing about how many
*unsupported* claims slip through. A judge that endorses everything achieves perfect recall while retaining
every hallucination. Panel A of the lab makes the recall side concrete — drag $\alpha$ and the realized
coverage tracks $1 - \alpha$ to within Monte-Carlo error (at $\alpha = 0.10$, realized coverage $0.902$
against the target $0.900$) — but the leakage it ignores is the subject of the next section.

## Recall is not precision: conformal risk control

Panel C runs the back-off across the whole $\alpha$ grid and measures both error directions. Under our
deliberately **lenient** judge — informative but over-endorsing, so its faithful and unsupported
confidences overlap — the recall guarantee leaves the false-claim rate badly **uncontrolled**: at
$\alpha = 0.02$, nearly a quarter of retained slots are hallucinations ($0.23$), and even at $\alpha = 0.10$
the rate is $0.115$. Recall and precision have parted ways, and controlling the false-claim rate needs a
guarantee on a different quantity — one that decreases as we tighten the cut. That is **conformal risk
control**, the generalization of split conformal from the $0/1$ miscoverage loss to any bounded, monotone
loss.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Conformal risk control (Angelopoulos et al. 2024)">
Let $L_1(\lambda), \dots, L_{n+1}(\lambda)$ be exchangeable loss functions, each **non-increasing** in the
threshold $\lambda$, bounded above by $B$. With the empirical risk
$\hat R_n(\lambda) = \tfrac1n \sum_{i=1}^n L_i(\lambda)$, choose
$$
\hat\lambda \;=\; \inf\Big\{\, \lambda : \frac{n}{n+1}\,\hat R_n(\lambda) + \frac{B}{n+1} \;\le\; \alpha \,\Big\}.
$$
Then $\mathbb{E}\big[L_{n+1}(\hat\lambda)\big] \le \alpha$. Split conformal is the special case
$L_i(\lambda) = \mathbf{1}\{\text{miscovered}\}$ with $B = 1$; the $\tfrac{n}{n+1}$ shrinkage and the
$\tfrac{B}{n+1}$ correction are the finite-sample slack, the role the "$+1$" plays in the coverage theorem.
</TheoremBlock>

Everything now hinges on choosing a loss that is genuinely **monotone** in the threshold. The natural
candidate — the *fraction* of retained claims that are unfaithful — is the wrong one, and the reason is a
trap worth exhibiting.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The false-claim loss must have a fixed denominator">
Parametrize the back-off by a confidence cut $\lambda$, retaining $\{i : \tilde c_i \ge \lambda\}$, so a
larger $\lambda$ retains fewer claims. The **fraction-of-retained** loss
$$
L^{\mathrm{frac}}(\lambda) = \frac{\#\{\text{retained and unfaithful}\}}{\#\{\text{retained}\}}
$$
is **not** monotone in $\lambda$: raising the cut shrinks the denominator faster than the numerator can
fall. (Retain one false and nine true claims at fraction $0.10$; raise the cut to drop eight of the true
claims while the one false claim survives, and the fraction *rises* to $1.0$.) Applying conformal risk
control to $L^{\mathrm{frac}}$ is therefore unsound. The fix is the **fixed-denominator** loss
$$
L_i(\lambda) \;=\; \frac{1}{k}\,\#\{\, j : \tilde c_j \ge \lambda \text{ and } Y_j = 0 \,\},
$$
the count of retained hallucinations per answer normalized by the *constant* slot count $k$. Each indicator
$\mathbf{1}\{\tilde c_j \ge \lambda\}\mathbf{1}\{Y_j = 0\}$ can only switch **off** as $\lambda$ rises, so
$L_i$ is non-increasing and bounded in $[0,1]$ ($B = 1$) — exactly the hypothesis the theorem needs.
Conformal risk control thus bounds the **per-slot** false-claim rate $\mathbb{E}[L_{n+1}(\hat\lambda)] \le
\alpha$; the rate *conditional on retention* is not directly controllable, because conditioning
reintroduces the shrinking denominator.
</TheoremBlock>

The notebook reproduces the non-monotonicity numerically before adopting the fixed-denominator loss — the
discipline of building a counterexample before trusting the construction. With the monotone loss in hand,
Panel C's green curve holds the false-claim rate at or below the $y = \alpha$ guarantee line while the red
recall curve runs above it. The one small overshoot, $0.205$ against a target of $0.20$, is not a bug: it
is the honest face of the theorem, which controls the **expected** loss over calibration draws, not a single
realization. The cost is retention — to certify a $2\%$ false-claim rate the controller abstains entirely,
and only as $\alpha$ loosens does it begin to emit claims.

## The collapse anchor: a perfect judge recovers precision at k

Every topic in this layer collapses, under a degenerate setting, onto an imported quantity to machine
precision. Here the degenerate setting is a **perfect judge**, and the imported quantity is the metric the
layer began with.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Perfect-judge collapse onto precision at k">
Suppose the judge is perfect: sensitivity and specificity both $1$, with $\tilde c_i = Y_i$. Then the score
$s_i = 1 - Y_i$ separates the classes exactly (faithful claims score $0$, unsupported claims score $1$), so
for any cut strictly inside the gap the retained set is precisely the faithful claims. Consequently the
per-slot false-claim loss is identically zero at **every** $\alpha$, and the per-query retained fraction
equals the imported $\mathrm{precision}@k$ of the leg against the relevant set — the same
$\mathrm{oracle\_faithfulness}$ the prerequisite collapsed its mean verdict onto, now lifted to the
conformal retained set, matching to $< 10^{-12}$ in the notebook.
</TheoremBlock>

This is the prerequisite's perfect-judge anchor raised one level: there a perfect judge's *mean verdict*
equaled precision at $k$; here a perfect judge's *conformal back-off* recovers exactly the relevant
documents. The distribution-free machinery, stripped of judge noise, is the set metric the evaluation layer
was built on.

## When the guarantee breaks: covariate shift and the weighted repair

Coverage is **marginal**, and it rests on **exchangeability**. The companion formalML topic proves that
distribution-free *conditional* coverage is impossible at finite informative-set size, so no per-query
promise is available. Worse, the marginal guarantee itself fails the moment the test distribution stops
matching the calibration distribution.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" number={1} title="Why drift breaks the guarantee">
Under **covariate shift** the deployment draws claims from a different distribution over the observable
covariate — here document verbosity, the feature the judge's length bias keys on — while the
score-given-covariate relationship is unchanged. The augmented sequence of calibration scores and the test
score is then no longer exchangeable: the test index is distinguishable because its marginal differs, so the
rank-uniformity fact underpinning the coverage theorem fails, and the threshold calibrated on the old mix
under-covers the new one. Panel D makes this visible — as the shift strength $\beta$ grows, the unweighted
split coverage collapses from $0.95$ toward $0.27$, far below the target $0.90$.
</TheoremBlock>

The repair reweights the calibration scores by how much the test distribution favors each one. When the
likelihood ratio is known, it restores marginal coverage exactly.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Weighted split-conformal (Tibshirani et al. 2019)">
With likelihood ratio $w(x) = \mathrm{d}P_{\mathrm{test}}/\mathrm{d}P_{\mathrm{calib}}(x)$, give each
calibration score $S_i$ the normalized weight
$$
p_i^w \;=\; \frac{w(x_i)}{\sum_{j} w(x_j) + w(x_{n+1})}, \qquad
p_{n+1}^w \;=\; \frac{w(x_{n+1})}{\sum_{j} w(x_j) + w(x_{n+1})},
$$
the test point's own weight entering the denominator, and take $\hat\tau^w$ to be the weighted
$(1-\alpha)$-quantile $\inf\{\, s : \sum_i p_i^w\,\mathbf{1}\{S_i \le s\} \ge 1-\alpha \,\}$. Coverage at the
**shifted** test distribution is restored to at least $1-\alpha$. With uniform weights and
$w(x_{n+1}) = \overline{w}$ this is exactly the unweighted threshold $S_{(\lceil(1-\alpha)(n+1)\rceil)}$ —
the weighted machinery collapses onto split conformal when there is no shift, a twin verified to $< 10^{-12}$.
</TheoremBlock>

Panel D's green curve is this repair: using the known $w$, weighted conformal holds coverage at or above
the target across the whole shift range (and over-covers when the weights are skewed, the conservative
price of reweighting). The repair is only as good as the likelihood ratio is known — in production $w$ must
itself be estimated, and a misestimated $w$ voids the guarantee, the load-bearing caveat. This is the same
distribution comparison the prerequisite monitored with KS and PSI: there the job was to *detect* the
shift; here it is to *correct* coverage under it.

<RigorFlag>
The guarantees stack four honesty flags. **First**, every guarantee is marginal — averaged over the claim
and query draw — with no per-query or per-domain promise; distribution-free *conditional* coverage is
provably impossible at finite informative-set size. **Second**, conformal risk control bounds the
**expected** loss over calibration draws, not with high probability: a single calibration set can realize a
false-claim rate above $\alpha$ (here $0.205$ at $\alpha = 0.20$), and the high-probability, $\delta$-level
alternative is the risk-controlling prediction set of Bates et al. (2021), which replaces the plug-in
expectation with an upper-confidence bound on the risk. **Third**, exchangeability is fragile: claims within
one answer share a context and a generation, so they are not independent, and the weighted repair assumes a
*known* likelihood ratio. **Fourth**, "faithful" means faithful under the **synthetic judge oracle** — the
construction inherits the prerequisite's entire synthetic-oracle caveat, and the judge's own bias and
irreducible variance floor lower-bound the attainable error. "Distribution-free correctness" is
distribution-free with respect to the data, conditional on the judge.
</RigorFlag>

<FinanceCaseStudy title="A filings assistant that abstains rather than hallucinate a figure" modality={["text", "pdf"]}>
A retrieval-augmented assistant answers analyst questions over 10-K filings and earnings-call transcripts.
Each answer decomposes into claims — a revenue figure, a guidance change, a segment margin — and the judge
scores each claim's support against the retrieved passages. Without a guarantee, the system is a liability:
a single confidently-stated but unsupported number is the failure an auditor cannot accept. Conformal
factuality converts the judge confidence into a contract. At a chosen level — say $\alpha = 0.10$ — the
per-claim back-off emits only the claims that clear the calibrated cut, and conformal risk control
guarantees that the expected false-claim rate among emitted claims is at most $10\%$, with the system
abstaining on the rest rather than guessing. The price is retention: the stricter the guarantee, the more
the system declines to answer, exactly the precision–coverage tradeoff Panel C plots. And the guarantee is
conditional on the filing distribution holding steady — when a new sector or a new disclosure regime shifts
the query mix, coverage degrades silently until the weighted repair, or a recalibration on fresh labeled
audits, restores it. The honest version of "our RAG system is accurate" is a number with an $\alpha$ next to
it and a drift monitor watching the exchangeability it rests on.
</FinanceCaseStudy>

## What the guarantee is, in one line

A retrieval metric was an estimator; a calibrated judge confidence was an estimator with a second,
instrument-level source of noise. Conformal prediction closes the layer by turning that confidence into a
**finite-sample guarantee**: the per-claim back-off rarely drops a faithful claim (split conformal), the
risk controller holds the false-claim rate at or below $\alpha$ (conformal risk control), and the whole
contract rests on exchangeability — marginal, not conditional; in expectation, not with certainty; valid
under the judge, not above it; and repairable, with a known likelihood ratio, when the distribution drifts.
The notation, gathered: $\tilde c$ is the calibrated confidence, $s = 1 - \tilde c$ the nonconformity
score, $\hat\tau$ the split threshold and $\tau = 1 - \hat\tau$ the confidence cut, $\alpha$ the level, $k$
the claims per answer, $L_i(\lambda)$ the monotone per-slot false-claim loss, $\hat\lambda$ the
risk-control threshold, and $w(x)$ the covariate-shift likelihood ratio. A tested notebook owns every
number the lab displays.
