## Overview & motivation

Every prior topic in this series built one organ of a retriever. The [probabilistic-IR track](/topics/bm25-binary-independence-model) built the lexical leg; the [dense dual encoder](/topics/dense-retrieval-dual-encoders) and [late interaction](/topics/late-interaction-learned-sparse) built the neural legs; [IVF](/topics/ivf-voronoi-partitioning), [product quantization](/topics/product-quantization), and [HNSW](/topics/hnsw) built the index; [PLAID](/topics/multi-vector-ann-retrieval) served late interaction at scale; [reciprocal-rank fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) combined heterogeneous rankings; the [over-fetch laws](/topics/filtered-incremental-ann) priced the cost of recall. A production multimodal financial RAG system is what you get when these organs are wired into one body, and the wiring has mathematics of its own — not a new primitive, but the *composition* of the ones already built.

This capstone proves the three laws that govern the composed pipeline. They are deliberately honest about their status: one is an exact theorem, the rest are measured propositions, and the difference is the whole point. We have built the retrieval half of the stack; the generation, grounding, and evaluation layer above it — pointwise mutual information between query and answer, conformal factuality, determinantal context selection, GraphRAG, retrieval distillation — is the unbuilt upper curriculum, named here but not yet derived. What we can compose now is everything from ingest through rerank, and that is enough to see the system as a single object.

## Notation: the served pipeline and one shared truth

A query $q$ enters a pipeline of stages $S_1, \dots, S_L$ — candidate generation, fusion and pruning, rerank — each a lossy map that keeps some documents and discards others. The exact end-scorer is brute-force MaxSim, $S(q, d) = \sum_i \max_j \langle q_i, d_j\rangle$, and its top-$k$ defines the *ground truth*: the true neighbors of $q$ are the $k$ documents that the full, uncompressed scorer would return. This choice matters. Because the truth is the exact scorer and not any one cheap leg, no candidate-generation leg is privileged — the oracle is the act of reranking everything, not any single retriever — which is exactly what lets the legs genuinely complement and lets a full-budget pipeline reach recall $1$.

The corpus is one shared multi-view document set. Each document is a bag of unit-norm token vectors (the token view), seen three ways: a lexical leg matches a window of the tokens' quantized centroid identifiers (BM25 over a centroid vocabulary), a dense leg pools a disjoint window into a single vector (cosine MIPS), and a late-interaction leg scores a third window by cheap centroid-MaxSim. Three partial views of one document — the systems-level shape of multimodality, where a text leg, a transcript leg, and a chart leg each observe part of a filing and fusion recombines them. The cross-modal *alignment* that would map them into one joint embedding is a separate, future topic; here the legs are independent retrievers feeding fusion.

## Movement 1 — cascade recall composition

Fix a query and one true top-$k$ neighbor $x^\*$. Let $A_i$ be the event that $x^\*$ survives stage $i$ given it entered, and $r_i = \Pr[A_i] \in (0, 1]$ the stage's per-neighbor retention. The neighbor reaches the output exactly when it survives every stage.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The served cascade and its retentions">

The pipeline is the composition $S_L \circ \dots \circ S_1$ of $L$ filters. Stage $S_1$ generates candidates (a token-IVF probe), $S_2$ fuses the legs and prunes to a survivor set, and $S_3$ reranks the survivors by exact MaxSim. A true neighbor is *recalled* iff it lies in the final top-$k$; its end-to-end retention is $\Pr\!\big[\bigcap_{i=1}^{L} A_i\big]$, and the per-stage retentions $r_i$ are measured against the shared MaxSim truth.

</TheoremBlock>

The first law is the one every engineer half-knows and half-misstates.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The product law and its over-fetch (exact under independence)">

If the survival events $\{A_i\}$ are mutually independent, then end-to-end retention is the product

$$
R \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^{L} r_i,
$$

exactly, and to leave $k$ surviving true neighbors the front end must inject $k / \prod_i r_i$ of them in expectation. This over-fetch is a *single* negative-binomial law (the imported $k/r$ scan law of the [filtered-ANN topic](/topics/filtered-incremental-ann)) applied to the composite retention $\prod_i r_i$; the equality $\prod_i (1/r_i) = 1/\prod_i r_i$ is algebraic, not a multiplication of $L$ separate physical scan counts, because the intermediate stages are not independently re-ranked streams.

</TheoremBlock>

On the measured pipeline the three retentions are $1.000$, $0.922$, $0.922$ — candidate generation loses nothing at this breadth, fusion and rerank each drop a little — so the product is $0.851$ and the over-fetch a modest $1.18\times$. But the product law is exact only under an idealization, the same positional-independence assumption the over-fetch topic flagged for a single stage, now lifted to the whole pipeline. Real queries violate it, and the direction in which they violate it is the load-bearing claim.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Positive dependence makes the product a lower bound (FKG/Harris)">

If the survival events are positively associated — each $A_i$ an increasing event in a shared query-easiness latent, so that a query easy for one stage is easy for all — then

$$
R_{\text{true}} \;=\; \Pr\!\Big[\textstyle\bigcap_i A_i\Big] \;\ge\; \prod_i r_i,
$$

with equality at independence; negative association reverses the inequality. Under the positive dependence real pipelines exhibit, the independent product *under-states* true recall — it is a conservative lower bound — and provisioning the front end at $k / \prod_i r_i$ over-provisions, which is the safe direction.

</TheoremBlock>

The temptation is to guess the wrong sign: surely correlated failures *lose more*? They do not. Positive correlation piles the failures onto the same hard queries, which fail anyway, so fewer *distinct* documents are lost than independence predicts and true recall sits *above* the product. The argument is FKG/Harris — increasing events on a positively-associated law have $\Pr[\bigcap] \ge \prod \Pr$ — but because the intuition is so easy to invert, the notebook does not trust it: it generates dependence with a bivariate-normal survival copula and sweeps the correlation across both signs. The gap $R_{\text{true}} - \prod r_i$ runs from $-0.102$ at strong negative dependence through $-0.002$ at independence to $+0.167$ at strong positive dependence, monotone, with equality pinned at $\rho = 0$ — the first panel makes the slider live. The measured pipeline obliges: its realized recall, $0.922$, sits above its own product, $0.851$, exactly as a lower bound should.

## Movement 2 — hybrid fusion gain

The pipeline's middle stage fuses heterogeneous legs. Fix a query with true-relevant set $\mathcal{R}$ at depth $k$, let $A_L = \mathcal{R} \cap \mathrm{top}_k(L)$ be the relevant documents leg $L$ surfaces, and $\rho_L = |A_L| / |\mathcal{R}|$ its recall. The fused ranking is $\mathrm{RRF}(d) = \sum_L 1/(c + r_L(d))$ with $c = 60$, [reciprocal-rank fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) imported verbatim. The question is when fusing helps, and the honest answer is *a condition, not a guarantee*.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The coverage ceiling (exact upper bound)">

For any fusion rule emitting a list of depth at least $|\bigcup_L A_L|$,

$$
\rho_{\text{fused}} \;\le\; \frac{|\bigcup_L A_L|}{|\mathcal{R}|},
$$

since a relevant document no leg places in range cannot be retrieved. Disjoint coverage maximizes the ceiling. But the ceiling is *vacuous* — equal to $1$ — whenever a single leg already covers $\mathcal{R}$, so it bounds the gain from above and does **not** explain the failure below.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="Fused ≥ best leg is false; the flip needs co-endorsement">

There exist legs for which $\rho_{\text{fused}} < \max_L \rho_L$. Under $c = 60$ a single top-rank vote contributes $1/61 \approx 0.0164$, weaker than two mid-rank votes at $\approx 1/63 + 1/65 \approx 0.0313$, so a false positive overtakes a true neighbor only when it is **co-endorsed by both legs**. Concretely, a strong leg ranking $r_1, r_2, r_3$ first but also placing a false positive $x_1$ at rank $4$, fused with a noisy leg ranking $x_1, x_2$ on top and burying $r_3$, yields a fused top-$3$ of $\{r_1, x_1, r_2\}$: recall $2/3 < 1$. Remove $x_1$ from the strong leg — no co-endorsement — and $r_3$ survives, recall back to $1$.

</TheoremBlock>

This is the topic's headline-flip discipline made literal. The naive expectation — a noisy leg drowns out a good one — is right in spirit and wrong in mechanism, and the notebook builds and runs the counterexample before the prose is allowed to claim anything. A lone loud vote is not enough under RRF's near-uniform weights; it takes a conspiracy of two. The second panel reconstructs the RRF scores live and shows $r_3$ falling past the top-$3$ cut.

What survives the scrutiny is the conditional gain.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={4} title="The fusion gain under complementarity (demonstrated)">

On the multimodal finance corpus, where each leg observes a different partial view and so recalls true neighbors the others miss, the fused recall strictly exceeds the best single leg, $0.547 \to 0.580$ (a gain of $+0.032$), and the gain grows with leg de-correlation: from $0$ at a cloned, redundant leg to $+0.03$ at disjoint coverage. Inter-leg Kendall-$\tau$ is a *proxy* for that coverage disjointness — neither necessary nor sufficient on its own — and the gain is one cloud's number, not a universal ranking.

</TheoremBlock>

The complementarity is not incidental; it is engineered and it is the real multimodal story. When the three legs were three approximations of one scalar truth, they formed a quality ladder — each strictly better than the last — and no fusion could beat the best, because the worse legs added only noise. Making each leg a partial *view* of the document, blind to what the others see, is what gives fusion something to recombine. That is precisely the value a lexical leg adds over a dense one in a real financial retriever: it surfaces the exact tokens — tickers, line-item names, dollar figures — that a paraphrase-trained embedding smears, documents the dense leg never had.

## Movement 3 — end-to-end budget allocation

The whole pipeline runs on a budget. Each stage $i$ buys retention $g_i(c_i)$ by spending compute $c_i$, and the costs must share a unit — distance-computations per query — or a single budget is meaningless. A sparse lexical match touches a few centroid-id terms; a dense leg is one pooled dot product; late-interaction MaxSim scores every query token against every document token, an order of magnitude more per document. The system-design question is how to spend a fixed budget $B$ across these unequal stages.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Water-filling: equalize the marginal log-recall">

Model end-to-end recall by the separable product $R(c) = \prod_i g_i(c_i)$ and maximize it subject to $\sum_i c_i \le B$. Because $R > 0$ and monotone, $\arg\max R = \arg\max \sum_i \log g_i(c_i)$, a separable objective; if each $\log g_i$ is concave the Lagrangian $\sum_i \log g_i(c_i) - \lambda \sum_i c_i$ is concave, so any stationary point is the global maximum, and at every active stage

$$
\frac{g_i'(c_i)}{g_i(c_i)} \;=\; \lambda,
$$

the marginal *log*-recall equalized across stages — water-filling — with $g_i'(0)/g_i(0) \le \lambda$ on inactive stages and $\lambda$ fixed by the active-budget identity.

</TheoremBlock>

Two honesties belong with this theorem. First, the equal-marginal condition is not bought by the logarithm. The un-logged product has $\partial R / \partial c_i = g_i' \prod_{j \ne i} g_j = g_i' \,(R / g_i)$, and the common scalar $R$ cancels, so $g_i' / g_i = \lambda$ falls out of the product directly; what the log buys is *separability* — decoupling the stationarity equations — and, with concavity, a *global* optimum. Second, the separable model $\prod_i g_i$ is the Movement-1 object: measured stage-by-stage against the global truth and multiplied, it is a conservative lower bound on the true coupled-pipeline recall, which unions the legs and does at least as well. We optimize the bound, honestly labeled.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={5} title="Water-filling beats uniform and all-in-one (demonstrated); the anchor">

On the finance curves at the demo budget, water-filling reaches $R = 0.268$ — scanning the cheap lexical and dense legs deep and the expensive late-interaction leg shallow, with the marginal log-recall leveled across stages — against $0.154$ for naive uniform splitting and a mere $0.031$ for pouring the budget into any single leg, where a starved factor tanks the product. Concavity holds on the measured operating grid (verified, not assumed), and at full budget every $g_i \to 1$, so $R \to 1$: the collapse anchor.

</TheoremBlock>

That anchor is the one exact statement of the entire capstone. Probe every cell, prune nothing, rerank by exact MaxSim, and the pipeline *is* brute-force retrieval — recall $1.0$, the same probe-all/prune-nothing collapse the [PLAID topic](/topics/multi-vector-ann-retrieval) proved for its cascade, here for the whole stack. Everything above it — the product law, the fusion gain, the water-filling allocation — is a heuristic speed-for-recall trade we measure rather than guarantee.

## Finance case study

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text", "pdf", "chart", "news"]}>

The production system this series has tracked retrieves over a heterogeneous financial corpus: 10-K and 10-Q filing text, earnings-call transcripts derived from call audio, chart captions and table rows, and news headlines. Each modality enters as its own leg — BM25F over the filing and transcript text, a dense dual encoder over passage embeddings, the PLAID-served late-interaction leg over token tensors — and the three laws above are exactly the levers the system is tuned on.

The cascade law sets the over-fetch: to deliver ten grounded passages after fusion and a cross-encoder rerank, the candidate stage must fetch $10 / \prod_i r_i$, and because the legs are positively dependent across stages — a filing missed by the dense leg's coarse recall is often missed by the rerank too — the independent product is the conservative budget, never caught short. The fusion law sets which legs to combine: the lexical leg earns its place precisely where the dense leg is weakest, on the exact tokens a paraphrase model smears — a CUSIP, a GAAP line-item name, a basis-point figure — so fusing them gains exactly when the query is lexical-or-semantic rather than one or the other, and the dominated-leg flip is the warning against bolting on a redundant or noisy leg. The budget law sets the operating point: a fixed latency budget, allocated across IVF probe depth, fusion breadth, and rerank survivor count, is spent by equalizing the marginal recall-per-millisecond — the deep, cheap lexical scan and the shallow, expensive late-interaction rerank the water-filling prescribes. None of it is a benchmark; the corpus here is a designed synthetic cloud, and the numbers illustrate the mechanism a real system instantiates.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Honest accounting

<RigorFlag>

The capstone has exactly one exact statement — the collapse anchor, a full-budget pipeline recovers brute-force retrieval to floating point — and three measured laws, and the discipline is saying which is which. The cascade product is exact only under stage independence; under the positive dependence real pipelines exhibit it is a conservative lower bound (FKG/Harris, verified across both signs of a copula before the direction was written), and the over-fetch is one negative-binomial law on the composite retention, not a product of physical scan counts. The fusion gain is demonstrated on one corpus and conditional on leg complementarity; "fused ≥ best leg" is false in general, the dominated-leg flip needs a co-endorsed false positive, and the coverage ceiling — the one exact, upper bound — is vacuous under domination, so it does not explain the flip. The water-filling allocation is exact on the separable, log-concave, common-cost-unit model with an interior optimum; concavity is empirical on the measured curves, the separable product is itself the Movement-1 lower bound, and the equal-marginal condition follows from the un-logged product too (the log buys separability and a global optimum, not equal-marginal). The corpus is one shared synthetic token cloud — designed multi-view documents, not a trained multimodal encoder — so every number illustrates a mechanism, not a benchmark. Multimodality is systems-level: independent per-modality legs feeding fusion, with the cross-modal alignment that would replace them by a joint embedding named as future work, not derived. And the layer above retrieval — the information theory of what retrieval adds to generation, conformal factuality, determinantal context selection, GraphRAG, and retrieval distillation — is the unbuilt upper curriculum the series turns to next.

</RigorFlag>
