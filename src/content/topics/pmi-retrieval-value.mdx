## From "did the right document come back" to "how many bits did it add"

The evaluation layer scored retrieval by **presence**. A metric is a sample mean of per-query scores
([set metrics](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr)): recall asks what fraction of the relevant
documents were returned, average precision weights them by rank. These are estimators of *whether the right
documents came back*, and they are the right question when the failure mode is a missing document.

But a retrieval-augmented generator does not consume a recall number. It consumes the **documents**, and it
produces an **answer**. The question that matters for generation is not whether a document was returned but
what it *did* to the answer — and the natural unit for that is the **bit**. Generation begins, before any
retrieval, with a distribution over answers conditioned on the query alone, the prior $p(a \mid q)$. It is
uncertain: an analyst's question may pin a sector but not the specific company, so the prior spreads its
mass and carries entropy $H(A \mid Q)$. A retrieved document $d$ sharpens that distribution to a posterior
$p(a \mid q, d)$, and the amount of uncertainty it removes is measured in bits.

> Retrieval value is **entropy reduction**: the bits a document removes from the answer's uncertainty. Per
> answer it is the pointwise mutual information $\operatorname{pmi}(a; d \mid q) = \log_2 p(a \mid q,d)/p(a
> \mid q)$; on average it is the conditional mutual information $I(A;D \mid Q) = H(A\mid Q) - H(A\mid Q,D)$.

## The answer model: a prior, a posterior, and the log-ratio between them

To measure bits we need probabilities, and a retriever gives us geometry — query and document directions
and the inner products between them. We turn the geometry into an answer model in the way
[retrieval-augmented generation](https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.11401) does: a retrieval distribution over
documents, a per-document answer distribution, and a prior that is the **marginal** of the two. We reuse
the [dense-retrieval](/topics/dense-retrieval-dual-encoders) finance geometry — four sectors, two companies
each, one filing per company — so the answer prototypes $\mu_a$ *are* the company filing directions, and
the answer to a query is which company it concerns.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The answer prior, posterior, and pointwise mutual information">
Let the answer space be the $K$ companies with unit prototypes $\mu_a$, and let $q, d$ be unit query and
document vectors. The **retrieval distribution** is the dual-encoder MIPS softmax
$$
p(d \mid q) = \operatorname{softmax}_d\!\big(\langle q, \mu_d\rangle / \tau_{\mathrm{doc}}\big),
$$
the **per-document answer posterior** sums the query's and the document's evidence in log-space,
$$
p(a \mid q, d) = \operatorname{softmax}_a\!\big((\langle q, \mu_a\rangle + \langle d, \mu_a\rangle)/\tau\big),
$$
and the **prior is the RAG marginal** over which document is retrieved,
$$
p(a \mid q) = \sum_d p(d \mid q)\, p(a \mid q, d).
$$
The **pointwise mutual information** of document $d$ for answer $a$ is the log-ratio
$$
\operatorname{pmi}(a; d \mid q) = \log_2 \frac{p(a \mid q, d)}{p(a \mid q)}
  = \log_2 \Big[\tfrac{dp(\cdot \mid q, d)}{dp(\cdot \mid q)}\Big](a),
$$
the logarithm of the Radon–Nikodym derivative of the posterior answer measure with respect to the prior
answer measure. The marginal definition of the prior is load-bearing — it is what makes the mutual
information identity below close exactly, rather than approximately.
</TheoremBlock>

The orientation of pmi is the whole point: it is **positive** when a document raises an answer's
probability above its prior and **negative** when it lowers it. A document is not uniformly informative;
it carries different bits about different answers, and — as we will see — it can carry *negative* bits
about the true one.

## Information gain and the bits retrieval adds

Average pmi over the posterior and you get the **per-document information gain**, the KL divergence from
prior to posterior; average that over which document is retrieved and over queries and you get the
**conditional mutual information** — the bits retrieval adds. The
[Shannon entropy](https://formalml.com/topics/shannon-entropy) and the
[KL divergence](https://formalml.com/topics/kl-divergence) are developed in full on formalML; here we
assemble them into one identity and verify it three ways.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Retrieval value is entropy reduction">
For a fixed query $q$, with the prior the marginal of the posterior,
$$
\underbrace{\sum_{a,d} p(a,d\mid q)\,\log_2\frac{p(a,d\mid q)}{p(a\mid q)\,p(d\mid q)}}_{\text{joint sum}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\sum_d p(d\mid q)\,\mathrm{KL}\big(p(\cdot\mid q,d)\,\big\|\,p(\cdot\mid q)\big)}_{\text{expected gain}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{H(A\mid q) - H(A\mid q,D)}_{\text{entropy reduction}}
\;\ge\; 0,
$$
and averaging over queries gives $I(A;D\mid Q) = H(A\mid Q) - H(A\mid Q,D) \ge 0$, the bits retrieval adds.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Write $p(a,d\mid q) = p(d\mid q)\,p(a\mid q,d)$. Because the prior is the marginal, $p(a\mid q) = \sum_d
p(d\mid q)\,p(a\mid q,d)$, the ratio inside the joint sum is $p(a,d\mid q)/(p(a\mid q)p(d\mid q)) = p(a\mid
q,d)/p(a\mid q)$, so the joint sum collapses to $\sum_d p(d\mid q)\sum_a p(a\mid q,d)\log_2\frac{p(a\mid
q,d)}{p(a\mid q)} = \sum_d p(d\mid q)\,\mathrm{KL}(p(\cdot\mid q,d)\,\|\,p(\cdot\mid q))$, the expected-gain
form. Expanding the logarithm as $\log_2 p(a\mid q,d) - \log_2 p(a\mid q)$ and using the marginal once more
turns the same expression into $-\sum_d p(d\mid q)\sum_a p(a\mid q,d)\log_2\frac{1}{p(a\mid q,d)} + \sum_a
p(a\mid q)\log_2\frac{1}{p(a\mid q)} = H(A\mid q) - H(A\mid q,D)$. Nonnegativity is Gibbs' inequality: each
$\mathrm{KL} \ge 0$, so the $p(d\mid q)$-weighted average is too. The notebook computes all three forms and
asserts they agree — they coincide to machine precision (maximum pairwise disagreement $0$ to the printed
precision), with $H(A\mid Q) = 2.06$ bits dropping to $H(A\mid Q,D) = 0.88$ bits, a gain of $I(A;D\mid Q) =
1.18$ bits.
</TheoremBlock>

The three forms are not three approximations that happen to be close; they are one number written three
ways, and the proof is what guarantees it. The entropy-reduction form is the slogan — *retrieval removes
uncertainty* — the expected-gain form is the per-document decomposition the next sections lean on, and the
joint sum is the definition of mutual information itself.

## A relevant filing adds bits; a distractor costs them

The expected gain $\mathrm{KL}(\text{posterior}\,\|\,\text{prior})$ is always nonnegative — *any* document
that moves the belief has positive gain in the Shannon sense. But the gain is an average over answers, and
the **pointwise** pmi at a *particular* answer need not be positive. The answer that matters is the true
one, $a^\star$, and there the sign is diagnostic.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The sign of pmi at the true answer">
A relevant filing — the gold company's own document — raises the posterior on $a^\star$ above the prior, so
$\operatorname{pmi}(a^\star; d_{\mathrm{rel}} \mid q) > 0$. A plausible **distractor** — a different
company's filing in the same sector — sharpens the posterior onto the *wrong* company, lowering the
probability of $a^\star$, so $\operatorname{pmi}(a^\star; d_{\mathrm{distr}} \mid q) < 0$, even though the
distractor's information gain $\mathrm{KL}(\text{posterior}\,\|\,\text{prior}) > 0$ — it moves belief, just
toward the wrong answer. A document that costs bits at the truth is a document that, in expectation, makes
the generator *more* likely to be wrong.
</TheoremBlock>

This is a proposition about the toy, not a theorem about relevance labels, and it is one we *run* rather
than assume: across the query set the mean pmi at the true answer is $+0.98$ bits for relevant filings and
$-1.11$ bits for distractors, and $96.9\%$ of distractors land on the negative side (Panel B). The point of
[pmi as a word-association score](https://aclanthology.org/J90-1003/) was always that the sign carries
meaning — co-occurrence above chance is positive, below chance negative — and the same sign here separates
evidence that helps the generator from evidence that misleads it. It is also the precise sense in which a
high-recall retriever can hurt: returning a fluent, on-topic, *wrong* passage is returning negative bits.

## Diminishing returns: a redundant document adds almost nothing

If one document adds bits, do two add twice as many? Only if the second is *new*. The bits a sequence of
documents adds obey the chain rule of mutual information, and redundancy makes the later terms vanish.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Saturation is the chain rule of mutual information">
The information two documents add about the answer decomposes as
$$
I(A; D_1, D_2 \mid Q) = I(A; D_1 \mid Q) + I(A; D_2 \mid Q, D_1).
$$
When $D_2$ is **redundant** with $D_1$ — the same filing again — the conditional term $I(A; D_2 \mid Q,
D_1)$ is near zero: the posterior is already where $D_1$ put it, and a second copy barely moves it.
A **novel** $D_2$ — a different filing — leaves the conditional term large.
</TheoremBlock>

Measured as belief movement $\mathrm{KL}(\text{new}\,\|\,\text{old})$, the gold filing moves the posterior
$0.67$ bits from the prior; a *second identical copy* then adds only $0.09$ bits, while a *different* filing
adds $0.44$ (Panel C). The marginal value of evidence diminishes with redundancy — which is exactly why a
context window should be filled with *diverse* passages rather than the top-$k$ near-duplicates a similarity
search returns. Quantifying that diversity gain is a submodular-coverage problem, the subject of context
selection, the topic this one points forward to.

## The encoder maximized a lower bound on these bits

The dense encoder did not learn its geometry by accident. It was trained by
[contrastive learning](/topics/infonce-contrastive-objective), and the InfoNCE objective it minimized is,
up to sign, a **lower bound on the mutual information** between a query and its positive document — the same
$I(Q;D)$ whose answer-side payoff this topic measures.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="InfoNCE is a lower bound on I(Q;D), ceilinged at log(N+1)">
For an in-batch problem with one positive and $N$ negatives, the InfoNCE loss $\mathcal{L}$ satisfies
$$
I(Q;D) \;\ge\; \log(N+1) - \mathcal{L}.
$$
The bound can never exceed its ceiling $\log(N+1)$ — even a perfect critic ($\mathcal{L}\to 0$) certifies at
most $\log(N+1)$ bits — so it **saturates**: certifying more information requires more in-batch negatives,
not just a better encoder.
</TheoremBlock>

The ceiling is the load-bearing caveat, and Panel D shows it directly: as the batch grows from two
candidates to eight, the bound climbs but stays strictly under the rising $\log_2(N{+}1)$ line. So the
encoder's training and the retriever's value are two views of one quantity — training pushed $\mathcal{L}$
down to maximize a lower bound on $I(Q;D)$, and the bits a retrieved document adds to the answer are that
information cashed out at generation time. (The bound is loose, and the empirical $\log(N{+}1) - \mathcal{L}$
from a finite batch is a notoriously unreliable point estimate of the true MI — the
[variational-bounds](https://arxiv.org/abs/1905.06922) literature is a catalogue of the bias; the robust
statement is the saturation, not a number.)

## Bits added is a different axis from recall

Return finally to the set-metric layer. On this corpus retrieval is easy: the gold filing is the
query's nearest company prototype, so it is always rank one, and **recall@k and average precision are
saturated at $1.0$ for every query**. By the set-metric reading, every query is equally, perfectly served.

Yet the bits retrieval adds range from $0.68$ to $1.60$ across those same queries (Panel D, right). The
variation is real — an ambiguous query whose prior was spread wide gains more than one whose prior already
leaned toward the answer — and recall is blind to it. Recall measures **presence**: did the document come
back. Mutual information measures **contribution**: how much did it change the answer. They are different
axes, and an evaluation that reports only the first cannot see what retrieval actually did for generation.

<RigorFlag>
Every "bit" here is measured against a **synthetic answer model**, not a real LLM generator. The answer
distributions $p(a\mid q)$ and $p(a\mid q,d)$ are a softmax over a small fixed answer set induced from the
same von Mises–Fisher finance geometry the dense-retrieval topic used (four sectors, two companies each,
one filing per company) — an exponential-family stand-in chosen so that pmi, the per-document KL
information gain, and the conditional mutual information all have closed forms the notebook owns to the
decimal. A transformer's $p(a\mid q,\text{context})$ is intractable and would have to be **estimated**, with
its own variance. Four further joints. **MI estimation from samples is hard and biased**: the variational
bounds we name — InfoNCE among them — are the practical tools, and InfoNCE is a *lower* bound ceilinged at
$\log(N{+}1)$, so a measured "bits added" from a finite batch saturates exactly as the dense-encoder topic's
bound did; the identity $I = H(A\mid Q) - H(A\mid Q,D)$ holds for the true distributions, not their
finite-sample estimates. **The "distractor costs bits" claim is about $\operatorname{pmi}(a^\star; d\mid q) <
0$ for the true answer** under this model — a property of (answer, document) pairs, not of relevance labels;
pmi can be negative for a relevant document too when the answer set is coarse. **Saturation is chain-rule
arithmetic**, exact for the model, but real second documents are neither perfectly redundant nor
independent. And **bits-added is a different axis from recall** by construction here: recall is saturated
because retrieval is easy, which is the cleanest way to show the dissociation, not a defect. Queries are
sector-ambiguous synthetic vMF draws, not natural language.
</RigorFlag>

<FinanceCaseStudy title="How many bits does each retrieved filing add to the answer?" modality={["text", "pdf"]}>

boilerplate risk-factors section adds essentially nothing: the posterior is already placed. And the passage
the similarity search ranked first — the one with the highest cosine to the query — may add fewer bits than
a lower-ranked disclosure that actually resolves the ambiguity, because rank measures lexical and semantic
proximity, not the reduction in answer uncertainty. The numbers here come from a synthetic vMF stand-in for
that pipeline, not a benchmark; the *mechanism* is what the case study illustrates.
</FinanceCaseStudy>

## What retrieval adds, in one line

The evaluation layer asked whether the right documents came back; this topic asks how many **bits** they add
to the answer. The answer is the conditional mutual information $I(A;D\mid Q) = H(A\mid Q) - H(A\mid Q,D)$,
assembled from a prior $p(a\mid q)$, a posterior $p(a\mid q,d)$, and the pointwise log-ratio
$\operatorname{pmi}(a;d\mid q)$ between them. The pieces carry the lessons forward: a distractor with negative
pmi is the seed of the **faithfulness** question — when does retrieved evidence ground an answer versus
mislead it; the chain rule and diminishing returns are the relevance term in **context selection**; and the
prior-to-posterior sharpening, read as a channel, is the **retriever as a noisy channel** whose recall and
precision bound the bits it can deliver — Fano's inequality lower-bounds the answer error by the residual
entropy $H(A\mid Q,D)$. The dense encoder maximized a lower bound on exactly these bits. We now have the unit
the rest of the information-theory layer is measured in.
