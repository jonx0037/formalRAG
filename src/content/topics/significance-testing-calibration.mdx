## The question the variance only opened

[Set metrics](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr) and
[NDCG](/topics/ndcg-discount-geometry) made the same move and stopped at the same place. A retrieval
metric is not a property of a system; it is the **sample mean** of a per-query score over a finite,
fixed query set, and a different set of queries would give a different number. Both topics drew the
95% interval $\overline{m} \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}$ around each leg's mean and asked whether
two legs could be told apart — and both ended on a cliffhanger. The closest pair of legs, lexical and
dense, differs by only $0.05$ in mean NDCG, their intervals overlap across all forty queries, and the
prerequisite estimated that gap would need *roughly 185 queries* to resolve.

That estimate is a **crude** read, and conservative in a specific way: it compares two intervals built
*independently*, as if the two legs had been measured on different queries. They were not. Both legs
ran on the **same forty queries**, and an easy query — one with an unambiguous answer near the top —
is easy for *every* leg. The overlapping-intervals test throws that pairing away. The right instrument
keeps it, and this topic is built around the single idea that keeping it unlocks.

> A metric is an estimator; every question that remains is whether **two distributions of per-query
> quantities differ**. System versus system is the *significance* question. The score against the truth
> is the *calibration* question. Now against then is the *drift* question. Each is a paired comparison,
> and pairing — differencing query-by-query — is the geometric fact that runs through all three.

The lab above is the topic in four panels; we take each in turn. The arc: build the paired test and
watch it resolve the cliffhanger, make it distribution-free and correct it for multiple comparisons,
then turn the same comparison sideways to ask whether a score is calibrated and whether the

## The paired test: pairing cancels difficulty

Fix two legs $A$ and $B$ and let $m_A(q), m_B(q)$ be their per-query metric (AP or NDCG) on query $q$.
The prerequisite compared the two *means*. Instead, form the per-query **difference** and treat *it*
as the sample.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The paired difference and the paired t-statistic">
For the $Q$ queries let $d_q = m_A(q) - m_B(q)$. The paired estimator of the mean difference is
$\overline{d} = \frac{1}{Q}\sum_q d_q$ with sample standard deviation $s_d$, and the **paired
t-statistic** is
$$
t \;=\; \frac{\overline{d}}{s_d/\sqrt{Q}}, \qquad \text{degrees of freedom } Q-1,
$$
testing the null $H_0: \mathbb{E}[d] = 0$ (the systems tie). The effect size is Cohen's
$d_z = \overline{d}/s_d$, scale-free.
</TheoremBlock>

The whole advantage is in the variance of $d$. Because $A$ and $B$ are evaluated on the same queries,
their per-query scores are **positively correlated** — a hard query drags both down — and the variance
of the difference subtracts that shared component.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Pairing reduces variance">
For paired samples,
$$
\operatorname{Var}(d) \;=\; \operatorname{Var}(A) + \operatorname{Var}(B) - 2\,\operatorname{Cov}(A,B).
$$
Whenever $\operatorname{Cov}(A,B) > 0$ — whenever the two systems find the same queries easy — the
paired variance is strictly below the unpaired sum $\operatorname{Var}(A) + \operatorname{Var}(B)$, so
the paired standard error is smaller and the paired interval on $\overline{d}$ is narrower than the
gap between two independent intervals.

*Proof.* The identity is the bilinearity of variance:
$\operatorname{Var}(A-B) = \operatorname{Var}(A) + \operatorname{Var}(B) - 2\operatorname{Cov}(A,B)$.
Positivity of the covariance makes the third term a strict subtraction. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

On this corpus the legs correlate $0.32$ (MAP) and $0.45$ (NDCG) across queries, so the paired
variance is only $0.69$ and $0.59$ of the unpaired sum. Panel A shows the per-query difference strip
$d_q$ — mostly negative, lexical below dense, with a few queries where lexical wins — and the two
intervals on $\overline{d}$ shrinking under the query-count slider. The payoff is the resolution of
the cliffhanger.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Test–CI duality and the power-based query count">
The paired $(1-\alpha)$ confidence interval $\overline{d} \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\, s_d/\sqrt{n}$ **excludes
zero** if and only if the paired test rejects $H_0$ at level $\alpha$ — confidence intervals are
inverted tests. Holding the observed $s_d$ fixed, the interval clears zero once
$n > (z_{1-\alpha/2}/d_z)^2$. To *detect* the effect with power $1-\beta$, rather than clear zero on a
single realization, requires the larger
$$
n \;\approx\; \left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}}{d_z}\right)^2.
$$
</TheoremBlock>

For the closest NDCG pair, $d_z = 0.26$, and the three query counts form a clean ladder. The
prerequisite's overlapping-intervals read asked for **185**. The rigorous 80%-power count is
**116** — fewer, because the paired test is the sharper instrument. The single-realization
"interval clears zero" count is just **57**, but that is a one-time event with about 50% power, which
is why it sits below the power-based number rather than above it. The honest report is **185 → 116**:
the proper required query count is well under the crude one.

A second, equally honest fact: pairing tightens the test, it does not manufacture significance. On
MAP the same lexical–dense gap *is* significant at the forty queries we have ($t = -3.87$,
$p = 4.0\times10^{-4}$, separating by $n = 11$). On NDCG it is **not yet** ($t = -1.65$, $p = 0.107$):
the gap is real-looking but inside sampling error at $Q = 40$. The paired test answers "is the 0.05
NDCG gap real?" with *not on this evidence* — which is the correct answer, not a louder one.

## Distribution-free tests and the cost of asking three questions

The paired t-test assumes the differences are approximately normal. They are not: per-query AP and
NDCG are bounded in $[0,1]$ and skewed. Two instruments make no such assumption.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="The permutation and bootstrap tests">
The **permutation (randomization) test** uses that under $H_0$ the two systems are *exchangeable* on
each query, so each difference is equally likely to be $+d_q$ or $-d_q$. Enumerating (or sampling) the
$2^Q$ sign vectors $s \in \{\pm 1\}^Q$ gives the null distribution of $\overline{s \odot d}$, and the
two-sided p-value is the tail mass beyond $|\overline{d}|$. The **paired bootstrap test** resamples
the $Q$ differences with replacement, recenters to impose $H_0$, and reads the same tail. Neither
assumes normality; the permutation test is *exact* under exchangeability.
</TheoremBlock>

On the worked MAP pair all three agree — $t$-test $p = 4.0\times10^{-4}$, permutation
$p = 7.0\times10^{-4}$, bootstrap $p = 1.0\times10^{-4}$ — which is the cross-check the prerequisites'
rigor flag promised. The permutation test is the one the IR literature settled on (Smucker, Allan &
Carterette, 2007); Panel B draws its sign-flip null with the observed statistic marked and the tail
shaded.

Comparing three legs is not one test but $\binom{3}{2} = 3$, and running three tests at $\alpha = 0.05$
inflates the chance of a false "significant" somewhere. The
[multiple-comparisons](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/multiple-testing-and-false-discovery)
corrections adjust for it: **Bonferroni** multiplies each p-value by the number of tests, **Holm** does
so in a uniformly more powerful step-down, and **Benjamini–Hochberg** controls the false-discovery rate
instead of the family-wise error. On the NDCG family the two genuine separations survive every
correction, while the marginal lexical–dense pair is pushed from $0.107$ to $0.32$ under Bonferroni —
cemented as *not distinguishable*. Correcting for the questions you asked is what keeps a three-way
leaderboard honest.

## Calibration: is a score a probability?

Turn the comparison sideways. So far the two distributions were two systems' metrics; now they are a
score and the truth it predicts. A retrieval score $s(q,d)$ is **calibrated** as a relevance
probability if, among all query–document pairs scoring near $s$, a fraction near $s$ are actually
relevant. The geometric object is the reliability diagram.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Reliability diagram, ECE, and MCE">
Pool every $(q,d)$ pair, map the score into $[0,1]$, and partition the axis into bins $B_1,\dots,B_m$.
For bin $b$ let $\mathrm{conf}_b$ be the mean score and $\mathrm{acc}_b$ the empirical relevance rate.
Plotting $\mathrm{acc}_b$ against $\mathrm{conf}_b$ gives the **reliability diagram**; perfect
calibration is the diagonal $\mathrm{acc}_b = \mathrm{conf}_b$. The **expected calibration error** is
the count-weighted area off the diagonal,
$$
\mathrm{ECE} \;=\; \sum_{b=1}^{m} \frac{n_b}{N}\,\big|\mathrm{acc}_b - \mathrm{conf}_b\big|,
$$
a Riemann sum over the score partition; the **maximum calibration error** is the worst single bin,
$\mathrm{MCE} = \max_b |\mathrm{acc}_b - \mathrm{conf}_b|$.
</TheoremBlock>

Calibration is forced to be a *pooled* object — with ten relevant documents per query, a per-query
diagram would have one document per bin — and that is the point: pooling exposes whether a score means
the same thing across queries. It does not. The smooth cosine (dense) and MaxSim (late-interaction)
scores are massively **over-confident**: across the $4{,}800$ pooled pairs, with a base relevance rate
of $0.083$, the raw ECE is $0.43$ and $0.39$. A cosine of $0.9$ does not mean a $90\%$ chance of
relevance; it means whatever the query's own scale makes it mean, and the
[concentration of cosine similarities](/topics/high-dimensional-geometry) in high dimensions is why a
fixed threshold is incomparable across queries. Lexical BM25 is the interesting exception: its huge
mass of exact-zero scores sits at confidence near zero and *is* well-calibrated in bulk (ECE $0.05$),
but its tail is badly off (MCE $0.19$).

This is precisely the property [rank fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) routed around. Reciprocal-rank
fusion combines $1/(c + \mathrm{rank})$ — it uses only ranks, never scores — because raw scores are
incommensurable across queries and legs; fusing them directly would weigh one query's confidence
against another's on a scale that does not transfer. Rank fusion sidesteps calibration by construction;
this section is the reason it had to.

The fix is to *learn* the map from score to probability. **Platt scaling** fits a logistic
$\sigma(a\,s + b)$; **isotonic regression** fits the monotone step function minimizing squared error.
Both crush the ECE — dense from $0.43$ to under $0.01$ — and both do something that looks surprising
until you see why.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Monotone recalibration preserves the ranking">
A strictly increasing recalibration map (Platt's $\sigma(a\,s+b)$ with $a > 0$) leaves the order of
documents within every query unchanged, so per-query AP, NDCG, and the pooled AUC are **identical**
before and after recalibration.

*Proof.* A strictly increasing $g$ satisfies $s_i > s_j \iff g(s_i) > g(s_j)$, so $\arg\!\sort$ is
unchanged on every query; every ranking metric is a function of that order alone, and the
rank-based AUC is invariant under any strictly monotone transform of the scores. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The pooled AUC is identical to six decimals before and after Platt scaling on every leg — the badge in
Panel C — while the ECE collapses. **Calibration is orthogonal to ranking**: you can fix what the
scores *mean* as probabilities without moving a single document, because calibration reshapes the
score axis and the metrics read only the order. The Brier score — the mean squared error of the
probability against the label — decomposes (Murphy) into reliability, resolution, and uncertainty, and
recalibration drains the reliability term ($0.0002$ for recalibrated dense) while sparing the
resolution that the ranking depends on.

<RigorFlag>
The reliability diagram is necessarily pooled across queries, so it cannot separate "this score is
miscalibrated" from "this score means different things on different queries" — both register as a
gap off the diagonal, and the second is the dominant effect here. Isotonic regression attains a lower
in-sample ECE than Platt, but its extra flexibility can overfit on only $4{,}800$ pairs; a held-out
split (fit on half the queries, score the other half) confirms the recalibrated ECE still beats the
raw, so the improvement is real, not memorized. Calibration in the strict sense
(a score equals a probability) is also stronger than anything the ranking metrics ever needed, which
is exactly why it was free to ignore until a downstream decision — answer or abstain — depends on the
number itself.
</RigorFlag>

## Drift: has the distribution moved?

The last turn of the comparison is over *time*. In production the data shifts: the corpus grows, the
query mix changes, an embedding model is swapped, and retrieval quality decays silently. Drift
detection is the two-sample test of a **reference** window against a **current** one.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="KS, PSI, and the symmetrized-KL identity">
Let $\hat F_{\mathrm{ref}}, \hat F_{\mathrm{cur}}$ be the empirical CDFs of a monitored quantity (here
per-query NDCG). The **two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov** statistic is the supremum distance between the
two staircases,
$$
D \;=\; \sup_x \big|\hat F_{\mathrm{ref}}(x) - \hat F_{\mathrm{cur}}(x)\big|,
$$
distribution-free under the null. Binning the support into proportions $p_b$ (reference) and $q_b$
(current), the **population stability index** is
$$
\mathrm{PSI} \;=\; \sum_b (p_b - q_b)\ln\frac{p_b}{q_b} \;=\; \mathrm{KL}(p\|q) + \mathrm{KL}(q\|p),
$$
the **Jeffreys** (symmetrized KL) divergence, with the credit-risk traffic light $\mathrm{PSI} < 0.1$
stable, $0.1$–$0.25$ moderate, $> 0.25$ significant.
</TheoremBlock>

The middle equality is exact — $\sum_b(p_b-q_b)\ln(p_b/q_b) = \sum_b p_b\ln(p_b/q_b) - \sum_b
q_b\ln(p_b/q_b)$ — so PSI *is* a symmetrized [KL divergence](https://www.formalml.com/topics/kl-divergence),
and the continuous divergence it approximates is the integral of the log
[Radon–Nikodym derivative](https://www.formalcalculus.com/topics/radon-nikodym),
$\int \log(dP/dQ)\,dP$; the binned PSI is its Darboux sum, exactly as Average Precision was the area
under a curve. Panel D drives an embedding-degradation knob: as the dense leg's vectors are perturbed,
the per-query NDCG distribution shifts left, the KS sup-gap grows from $0$ to $0.93$, and PSI crosses
into the red. Under the matched-n null (the same distribution resampled) the detector is silent — mean
PSI $0.06$, KS $p = 0.92$.

Two subtler facts are the real lessons. First, a drift the *aggregate mean cannot see* is still
catchable — and the catcher is, once again, the paired test.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Drift monitoring, like system comparison, must be paired">
A small uniform degradation drops each query's score slightly but consistently. Comparing the two
windows by their **independent** mean intervals declares them indistinguishable — the intervals
overlap. The **paired** test on the per-query (now $-$ then) differences rejects, because pairing
cancels the shared per-query difficulty, exactly as in the system-comparison case.
</TheoremBlock>

At a degradation that moves the mean by under $2$ standard errors — overlapping intervals, a dashboard
staying green, and even the marginal KS test missing it ($p = 0.17$) — the paired test fires at
$p = 0.002$. The same pairing that resolved the significance cliffhanger is the right drift monitor.

Second, **input drift and quality decay are different things**. A pure *covariate shift* — the query
mix moves from easy to hard, the retriever unchanged — fires the input-distribution alarm (the top-1
score PSI is $0.43$, observable without any labels) while the model's quality on a fixed query set is
*exactly* unchanged (the paired outcome difference is zero). A genuine model decay, by contrast, drops
the paired outcome ($-0.60$, $p \approx 10^{-21}$). So an input-drift alarm cannot diagnose decay on
its own; you also need a labeled, paired *outcome* test. Monitor both, and read them together: input
moved with outcome flat is data drift; outcome moved is decay.

<RigorFlag>
Production monitoring is *online* — you test as each window arrives — so a fixed-$n$ KS p-value or a
static PSI threshold is invalid under continuous peeking; repeated looks inflate the false-alarm rate,
and PSI's $0.1$/$0.25$ cutoffs are heuristics that sidestep the problem rather than solve it (PSI has
no canonical academic derivation, and on forty queries its value is noisy). The principled fix is
[anytime-valid inference](https://www.formalml.com/topics/always-valid-inference) — confidence
sequences and e-processes that stay valid no matter when you stop — the sequential successor this
topic points to. And the recalibrated, drift-monitored retrieval confidence is itself the input to the
distribution-free correctness guarantees of conformal factuality and to a selective-generation gate,
the upper layer this evaluation track opens onto.
</RigorFlag>

<FinanceCaseStudy title="Deciding to switch retrievers, and watching the one in production" modality={["text", "audio"]}>
The production system this series tracks retrieves over 10-K and 10-Q filing text and earnings-call
transcripts, and the three questions here are the three the desk actually faces. *Is the new retriever
better?* A five-point NDCG gain over forty evaluation queries is not a reason to switch — the paired
test says lexical and dense are indistinguishable at that gap, and the power calculation says it would
take on the order of a hundred labeled queries to be sure, so the disciplined answer is a paired
p-value and a required-n, not a leaderboard delta. *Can we trust the confidence the retriever reports?*
Only if it is calibrated: a raw top-1 cosine of $0.42$ is meaningless across queries, but a calibrated
$0.42$ says roughly two in five passages scoring this high are the right disclosure — exactly the
number an answer-or-abstain gate needs, and the maximum calibration error on the high-confidence bin is
the dangerous quantity, because that is where a confident wrong answer reaches the analyst. *Has the
retriever quietly gotten worse?* Population stability index on the retrieval-score distribution is the
credit-risk monitoring standard, and the lesson transfers: when filing season changes the vocabulary
or an embedding model is swapped, input PSI crossing $0.25$ is expected covariate shift, not
necessarily decay — confirm decay with a paired outcome test on a fixed evaluation set before
re-indexing. The corpus here is a synthetic vMF token cloud standing in for those documents; every
value illustrates the mechanism, not a benchmark.
</FinanceCaseStudy>
