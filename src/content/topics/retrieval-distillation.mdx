## Overview & motivation

The cross-encoder topic ended on a tension. A joint scorer $h([q;d])$ that lets the query and document
attend at every layer is the most accurate relevance function we have — and the least deployable,
because scoring a corpus means one forward pass per query–document pair. We resolved that with a
cascade: retrieve cheaply, rerank the survivors expensively. This topic resolves it a second way, and
it is the reranking sub-track's real payoff. Rather than keep the cross-encoder at query time, we
**distill** it: we train a cheap, precomputable dual-encoder student to imitate the expensive teacher
once, offline, and then throw the teacher away. The student answers queries by precomputed
inner products — a dual encoder — yet carries the teacher's discrimination.

The question is what "imitate" should mean. The naive answer, regress the student's scores onto the
teacher's, is wrong in an instructive way, and the right answer — match the teacher's *margins* — turns
out to be the same truncated-SVD story the embedding-dimension and cross-encoder topics already taught,
applied to a centered version of the teacher's score matrix.

<RetrievalDistillationLaboratory client:visible />

Throughout, $T \in \mathbb{R}^{n_q \times n_d}$ is the teacher's score matrix — row $i$ is query $q_i$'s
scores over the $n_d$ documents — and the student is a dual encoder with score matrix $S = Q G^\top$,
$Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n_q \times d}$, $G \in \mathbb{R}^{n_d \times d}$, so $\operatorname{rank}(S) \le d$.
The teacher is the random-ReLU finance cross-encoder imported from the previous topic; it realizes the
relevance (recall@1 $= 1.0$) while leaving graded scores on the negatives.

## MarginMSE and the centered teacher

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="MarginMSE distillation">

For a positive document $d^+$ and a negative $d^-$, the teacher's *margin* is
$m_T(q, d^+, d^-) = T_{q,d^+} - T_{q,d^-}$, and MarginMSE trains the student to match it:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\text{pair}}(S, T) = \big[(S_{q,d^+} - S_{q,d^-}) - (T_{q,d^+} - T_{q,d^-})\big]^2 .
$$

Summed over every within-query document pair, the **all-pairs MarginMSE** is

$$
\mathcal{L}(S, T) = \sum_{i}\sum_{j,k}\big[(S_{ij} - S_{ik}) - (T_{ij} - T_{ik})\big]^2 .
$$

The pointwise alternative, $\lVert S - T\rVert_F^2$, regresses the absolute scores instead of the
margins. We will see these are different objectives with different optima.

</TheoremBlock>

## Movement 1 — MarginMSE and translation-invariance

The all-pairs margin loss looks like it depends on every pair of documents, but it collapses to a
single centered distance, and that collapse is the whole reason margins are the right target.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The MarginMSE reduction and translation-invariance">

Let $C = I - \tfrac{1}{n_d}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ be the operator that subtracts each query's mean
document score (right-multiplication centers the rows). Then

$$
\mathcal{L}(S, T) = 2\,n_d\,\lVert S C - T C\rVert_F^2 .
$$

Consequently the loss is **invariant to any per-query offset** of the teacher: for any
$b \in \mathbb{R}^{n_q}$,

$$
\mathcal{L}(S,\, T + b\mathbf{1}^\top) = \mathcal{L}(S, T),
$$

because $\mathbf{1}^\top C = 0$. The margin loss cannot see the teacher's per-query score level.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

Fix a query $i$ and write $\delta_j = S_{ij} - T_{ij}$. The inner sum is
$\sum_{j,k}(\delta_j - \delta_k)^2$. Expanding,

$$
\sum_{j,k}(\delta_j - \delta_k)^2
= 2 n_d \sum_j \delta_j^2 - 2\Big(\sum_j \delta_j\Big)^2
= 2 n_d \sum_j (\delta_j - \bar\delta)^2,
$$

the variance identity, where $\bar\delta = \tfrac{1}{n_d}\sum_j \delta_j$. Now
$\delta_j - \bar\delta = (S_{ij} - \bar S_i) - (T_{ij} - \bar T_i) = (SC)_{ij} - (TC)_{ij}$, so the
inner sum is $2 n_d \sum_j \big[(SC)_{ij} - (TC)_{ij}\big]^2$. Summing over $i$ gives
$\mathcal{L}(S,T) = 2 n_d \lVert SC - TC\rVert_F^2$. For the offset, $(T + b\mathbf{1}^\top)C = TC + b(\mathbf{1}^\top C) = TC$, since $\mathbf{1}^\top C = \mathbf{1}^\top - \tfrac{1}{n_d}(\mathbf{1}^\top \mathbf{1})\mathbf{1}^\top = \mathbf{1}^\top - \mathbf{1}^\top = 0$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is the load-bearing fact. A cross-encoder's absolute scores are *per-query miscalibrated* — the
same relevance can read as a different logit scale on different queries — which is precisely why
Hofstätter et al. chose to distill margins. Theorem 1 says the choice is exactly right: the margin loss
is blind to that miscalibration by construction. Panel A of the laboratory makes this visible. A
miscalibration slider adds a per-query offset $\alpha\, b$ to the teacher; the pointwise loss of a fixed
margin student is a parabola that inflates without bound, while its margin loss is a flat line, and the
teacher's heatmap rows brighten and darken with $\alpha$ while the centered teacher does not move.

## Movement 2 — the closed-form distilled student

Because the loss is a Frobenius distance, the optimal rank-$d$ student is a truncated SVD — no gradient
descent required, and the two objectives differ only in *what* they take the SVD of.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The pointwise-optimal student">

The pointwise-MSE-optimal rank-$d$ student is the truncated SVD of the teacher:

$$
S^\star_{\text{point}} = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\operatorname{rank}(S)\le d} \lVert S - T\rVert_F^2 = \operatorname{best\,rank}_d(T),
$$

by Eckart–Young–Mirsky.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="The margin-optimal student is the centered SVD">

The margin-optimal rank-$d$ student is the truncated SVD of the *per-query-centered* teacher:

$$
S^\star_{\text{margin}} = \operatorname*{arg\,min}_{\operatorname{rank}(S)\le d}\mathcal{L}(S, T) = \operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC).
$$

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

By Theorem 1 we minimize $\lVert SC - TC\rVert_F^2$ over rank-$d$ matrices $S$. For any such $S$, the
product $SC$ has rank at most $d$, so

$$
\lVert SC - TC\rVert_F^2 \ \ge\ \min_{\operatorname{rank}(R)\le d}\lVert R - TC\rVert_F^2 = \lVert \operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC) - TC\rVert_F^2
$$

by Eckart–Young. The bound is achieved by $S = \operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC)$: because $TC$ has zero
row sums, its right singular vectors with nonzero singular value are orthogonal to $\mathbf{1}$, so
$\operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC)$ also has zero row sums, hence $SC = S$ and
$\lVert SC - TC\rVert_F^2 = \lVert \operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC) - TC\rVert_F^2$. So the minimum is
attained, and the minimizing $S$ is itself a genuine rank-$d$ dual encoder, realizable as $Q G^\top$
through a thin SVD. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The contrast is the whole story. Pointwise distillation spends its $d$-dimensional budget reproducing
the *entire* teacher matrix — including its per-query level, which is irrelevant to ranking. Margin
distillation centers the teacher first, throwing the level away, and spends every dimension on the
ordering. And the budget is genuinely scarce: a dual encoder is rank-$d$, so it inherits the
embedding-dimension rank ceiling. Distillation moves the student *toward* the teacher within that
ceiling and stops at it.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="The rank ceiling binds the student">

For every $d$, the student's recall is at most the teacher's, with equality of *ranking* once
$d \ge \operatorname{rank}(TC)$: at full rank the margin student reproduces the centered teacher exactly,
so its argmax-per-row — and hence its recall@1 — equals the teacher's. A rank-$d$ student approaches
the teacher's recall from below; it cannot exceed it.

</TheoremBlock>

## Movement 3 — margin beats pointwise, and the cost payoff

Theorem 2 predicts a recall gap at restricted rank, and the laboratory confirms it. The teacher's score
matrix carries a single dominant singular value — its per-query level, magnified by the miscalibration
offset — so the pointwise student best_rank_d(T) burns a whole dimension reproducing a constant that
does nothing for ranking. The centered teacher $TC$ has a flat spectrum: every direction is ranking
signal. At the restricted rank $d = 3$ the margin student's recall@1 ($0.69$) leads the pointwise
student's ($0.50$), the gap is positive at every rank tested and seed-robust, and both rise to the
teacher's $1.0$ only as $d$ approaches full rank — the ceiling of Corollary 1. Panel B plots the two
recall curves against the rank slider, with the two spectra beside them so the wasted dimension is
visible as the teacher's lone tall singular value.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The inference-cost payoff">

A cross-encoder reranker costs $|C|\, c_{\text{ce}}$ per query — one joint forward pass per document,
not precomputable. The distilled dual-encoder student costs $|C|\, c_{\text{ret}}$ — precomputed
document embeddings, one inner product per document (sublinear with an ANN index). With the cascade's
unit costs $c_{\text{ret}} = 1$, $c_{\text{ce}} = 25$, the student is a constant
$c_{\text{ce}}/c_{\text{ret}} = 25\times$ cheaper at query time, and the teacher's cost becomes a
one-time offline training expense.

</TheoremBlock>

This is the reranking sub-track's destination. The cross-encoder topic could only afford the teacher on
a small candidate pool; distillation pays the teacher once, offline, and folds its judgments into a
student that is as cheap to query as any dual encoder. Panel C's corpus slider shows the absolute cost
gap widening with corpus size while the speedup holds at $25\times$ — cross-encoder ranking at
dual-encoder inference cost.

## Movement 4 — dark knowledge, and an honest in-sample finding

Distillation's classical justification (Hinton et al., 2015) is *dark knowledge*: a teacher's graded
soft targets carry information a one-hot label does not — which wrong answer is the more dangerous
confusion. The teacher here has that knowledge. Mining the hardest negative per query from the labeled
DPR pool — the nearest other-company query, whose gold document is a genuine hard negative — and reading
off the teacher's margin on those pairs gives a *graded* spread (mean $\approx 0.66$, ranging from
$0.12$ to $1.29$), where a binary label's margin is the constant $1$. Some hard negatives are far more
confusable than others, and the teacher knows it.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="On this in-sample toy, binary compresses at least as well">

Yet build-and-run delivers an honest surprise: distilling the *soft* teacher's centered scores does not
beat distilling the *hard* binary labels at restricted rank. The binary relevance matrix is perfectly
block-structured — four queries per company share one gold document — so its centered SVD compresses to
low rank cleanly, recovering recall@1 $= 0.875$ at $d = 3$ against the soft teacher's $0.69$. The
soft-beats-hard advantage of dark knowledge is a *generalization* phenomenon: it helps when labels are
scarce and the student must extrapolate to unseen queries, which a closed-form *in-sample* fit cannot
exhibit. The reason to distill the teacher is not that its soft targets compress better here — it is
that in production you have no gold labels for most queries, and the teacher is the scalable labeler.

</TheoremBlock>

This is the build-and-run discipline doing its job: the obvious headline is false on the toy, and the
honest finding — dark knowledge is real but its payoff is out-of-sample — is the more useful lesson.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="A reranker the desk never pays for twice" modality={["text", "pdf", "news"]}>

The production multimodal financial RAG system runs a cross-encoder reranker that separates same-sector
filings a single embedding confuses — the bank whose 10-K reads like its three peers'. It is accurate

quirks, and answers desk queries by precomputed inner products at $25\times$ lower cost. The rank
ceiling sets the honest expectation: the student approaches the cross-encoder's separation, it does not
match it, and where the last few points of recall matter the cross-encoder still reranks the student's
shortlist.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Honest accounting

<RigorFlag>
Two results here are theorems and the rest is honest modeling. The MarginMSE reduction is EXACT: the
all-pairs margin loss equals the centered Frobenius distance $2 n_d \lVert SC - TC\rVert_F^2$ (a variance
identity), from which translation-invariance — that any per-query offset $T \mapsto T + b\mathbf{1}^\top$
is invisible because $\mathbf{1}^\top C = 0$ — is immediate, and the closed-form optima follow by
Eckart–Young–Mirsky: the pointwise-optimal rank-$d$ student is $\operatorname{best\,rank}_d(T)$, the
margin-optimal one $\operatorname{best\,rank}_d(TC)$. Those are proved. The softer seams: the teacher is
the deterministic random-ReLU cross-encoder reused from the cross-encoders topic — a seeded stand-in for
a trained transformer, tuned to a width where it is a good teacher (recall@1 $= 1.0$) with graded scores,
not a real BERT reranker. The per-query offset is a model of the documented cross-encoder per-query
miscalibration; its magnitude is tuned so the margin-over-pointwise recall gap is seed-robust, since the
teacher's own scores already carry a large constant baseline the centering removes (the gap exists
without the offset but is not seed-robust without it). MarginMSE itself is a heuristic loss — the
reduction characterizes its optimum, but the loss was chosen empirically, not derived from a ranking
objective. The rank ceiling binds the student no matter how accurate the teacher. And the dark-knowledge
result runs the other way on this toy: the teacher's margins on the mined hard pairs are genuinely
graded, but on a clean, block-structured, in-sample corpus the binary target compresses to low rank at
