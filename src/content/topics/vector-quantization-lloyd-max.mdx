## Overview & motivation

A vector retriever pays for every dimension twice, and the [previous topic on PCA](/topics/pca-dimensionality-reduction) attacked one of those costs by shrinking the *width* of each vector. This topic attacks the other: the number of *bits*. PCA still hands back a vector of real numbers — narrower, but $128$ or $256$ `float32` coordinates is still kilobytes per item across tens of millions of items. Vector quantization makes a more aggressive trade. Instead of storing each embedding, store the *index* of the nearest entry in a small shared **codebook** of $k$ representative vectors. A codeword index costs $\log_2 k$ bits — eight bits for a codebook of $256$ — in place of the thousands of bits the raw vector needed. The reconstruction is lossy, and the entire question is how to choose the codebook so that the loss, measured as squared error, is as small as possible.

That question has a clean and classical answer, and it is the subject of this topic. We will define a quantizer as a decoder composed with an encoder, write its quality as an expected squared **distortion**, and prove the two conditions an optimal quantizer must satisfy — one for the encoder, one for the decoder. Alternating those two conditions is **Lloyd's algorithm**; we will show it drives distortion downhill to a fixed point, recognize it as exactly $k$-means, and then confront the honest catch that the fixed point it reaches is only a *local* optimum. The codebook this topic builds is the atom that *product quantization* later splits across subspaces, and the partition it induces is the coarse index the *inverted file* reuses — both topics that follow in this track.

Before the algebra, run the algorithm. Step through Lloyd's two half-steps on a small cloud and watch the distortion fall; then reseed and watch a different starting codebook get stuck somewhere worse:

## The quantization problem

Fix a source: a random vector $X \in \mathbb{R}^d$ with distribution $P$, the embedding a real encoder would produce. A quantizer compresses $X$ through a finite codebook and is built from two maps.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Vector quantizer">

A **codebook** is a finite set $\mathcal{C} = \{c_1, \dots, c_k\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ of $k$ **codewords**. An **encoder** $\alpha : \mathbb{R}^d \to \{1, \dots, k\}$ assigns each point an index, and a **decoder** $\beta : \{1, \dots, k\} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ with $\beta(i) = c_i$ reads the index back to its codeword. The **quantizer** is the composition

$$
Q = \beta \circ \alpha, \qquad Q(x) = c_{\alpha(x)} .
$$

The encoder induces a partition of $\mathbb{R}^d$ into **cells** $R_i = \alpha^{-1}(i) = \{x : \alpha(x) = i\}$. Storing $Q(X)$ costs the **rate** $\log_2 k$ bits per vector, and the quality is the **distortion**, the expected squared error

$$
D(\alpha, \beta) = \mathbb{E}\lVert X - Q(X)\rVert^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \int_{R_i} \lVert x - c_i\rVert^2 \, dP(x) .
$$

</TheoremBlock>

The factorization $Q = \beta \circ \alpha$ is the whole structural insight. The distortion couples the encoder and the decoder, but only loosely: with the codebook held fixed we can optimize the encoder alone, and with the partition held fixed we can optimize the codebook alone. Each of those one-sided problems has an exact, closed-form solution — the next two theorems — and alternating between them is the algorithm. The rate–distortion functional $D$ itself, and the information-theoretic limits on how small it can be at a given rate, are developed in [rate–distortion theory](https://formalml.com/topics/rate-distortion); here we take the operational view and minimize it for a finite codebook.

## The nearest-neighbor condition: the optimal encoder

Hold the codebook fixed and ask for the best encoder. Because the decoder just looks up $c_{\alpha(x)}$, the encoder's only job is to choose, for each $x$, which codeword to point at — and the distortion is a sum of independent per-point penalties, so the choice is pointwise.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The nearest-neighbor (Voronoi) condition">

For a fixed codebook $\mathcal{C} = \{c_1, \dots, c_k\}$, the encoder that minimizes distortion assigns each point to its nearest codeword,

$$
\alpha^\star(x) = \arg\min_{i \in \{1, \dots, k\}} \lVert x - c_i\rVert^2 ,
$$

so the optimal cells are the **Voronoi regions** of the codebook, $R_i^\star = \{x : \lVert x - c_i\rVert \le \lVert x - c_j\rVert \ \text{for all } j\}$, and the minimized distortion is $D = \mathbb{E}\,\min_i \lVert X - c_i\rVert^2$.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

The distortion $D(\alpha, \beta) = \int \lVert x - c_{\alpha(x)}\rVert^2 \, dP(x)$ is the integral of a nonnegative integrand, and with the codebook fixed the value of $\alpha(x)$ at one point $x$ does not constrain its value anywhere else. So the integral is minimized by minimizing the integrand pointwise: for each $x$ independently, choose $\alpha(x)$ to make $\lVert x - c_{\alpha(x)}\rVert^2$ as small as possible, which is the index of the nearest codeword. The points equidistant from two nearest codewords — the cell boundaries — form a measure-zero set under a continuous $P$ and may be assigned by any fixed rule; we break ties by the lowest index, the convention the companion code and the laboratory share. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The optimal partition is therefore *always* a Voronoi diagram of the current codebook: nothing else can do better once the codewords are fixed. This is more than a bookkeeping fact. It is the reason an inverted-file index can use a coarse $k$-means codebook as its routing structure — the cells it searches are exactly these Voronoi regions — and it is the geometry the laboratory redraws on every assignment step.

## The centroid condition: the optimal decoder

Now reverse the roles. Hold the partition fixed and ask for the best codebook. The distortion decouples across cells, $D = \sum_i \int_{R_i} \lVert x - c_i\rVert^2 \, dP$, so each codeword is chosen independently to minimize its own cell's contribution, and that is a one-dimensional question about a single representative point.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="The centroid (conditional-mean) condition">

For a fixed partition $\{R_1, \dots, R_k\}$ with $P(R_i) > 0$, the codebook that minimizes distortion sets each codeword to the conditional mean of its cell,

$$
c_i^\star = \mathbb{E}[X \mid X \in R_i] = \frac{\int_{R_i} x \, dP(x)}{P(R_i)} .
$$

The codeword is the unique minimizer, and the resulting per-cell distortion is the within-cell variance — the trace of the conditional covariance.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

Fix one cell and minimize $f(c) = \int_{R_i} \lVert x - c\rVert^2 \, dP(x)$ over $c \in \mathbb{R}^d$. Write $\mu_i = \mathbb{E}[X \mid X \in R_i]$ and add and subtract it:

$$
\int_{R_i} \lVert x - c\rVert^2 \, dP = \int_{R_i} \lVert x - \mu_i\rVert^2 \, dP + P(R_i)\,\lVert \mu_i - c\rVert^2 ,
$$

where the cross term $-2\langle \mu_i - c, \int_{R_i}(x - \mu_i)\,dP\rangle$ vanishes because $\int_{R_i}(x - \mu_i)\,dP = 0$ by the definition of $\mu_i$. The first term does not depend on $c$; the second is nonnegative and is zero exactly when $c = \mu_i$. Equivalently, $f$ is a strictly convex quadratic — its gradient $\nabla_c f = -2\int_{R_i}(x - c)\,dP$ vanishes only at $c = \mu_i$, and its Hessian $2P(R_i) I$ is positive definite — so the stationary point is the unique global minimum, the [convex-quadratic minimization](https://formalcalculus.com/topics/convex-optimization) the centroid step solves. For a finite sample the conditional mean is the cell's sample mean, the [conditional expectation](https://formalstatistics.com/topics/multivariate-distributions) of the empirical law restricted to the cell. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

One pathology is worth naming now, because it returns in the algorithm: if a cell is empty, $P(R_i) = 0$, the conditional mean is a $0/0$ and the codeword is undefined. For a continuous source this never happens, but on a finite sample a codeword can capture no points, and a faithful implementation must say what to do. We reseed an orphaned codeword onto the worst-served point — the data point currently farthest from its own codeword — a deterministic repair that, as we will see, can only lower distortion.

## Lloyd's algorithm: alternating optimization

We have two exact half-solutions: the best encoder for a fixed decoder, and the best decoder for a fixed encoder. Neither alone solves the joint problem, because each assumes the other is fixed. Lloyd's idea is to alternate them.

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={1} title="Lloyd's algorithm (generalized Lloyd / LBG)">

Input: a source $P$ (or a finite sample), a codebook size $k$, and an initial codebook $\mathcal{C}^{(0)}$. For $t = 0, 1, 2, \dots$ repeat:

1. **Assignment step (nearest-neighbor condition).** Hold $\mathcal{C}^{(t)}$ fixed and form the Voronoi partition $R_i^{(t)} = \{x : \alpha^\star(x) = i\}$ by Theorem 1.
2. **Update step (centroid condition).** Hold the partition fixed and set $c_i^{(t+1)} = \mathbb{E}[X \mid X \in R_i^{(t)}]$ by Theorem 2.

Stop when the codewords stop moving (equivalently, when the assignment no longer changes).

</TheoremBlock>

Each step solves one of the two sub-problems exactly, so neither can raise the distortion — and that is enough to prove the algorithm converges.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="Monotone distortion decrease and convergence to a fixed point">

The distortion sequence $D^{(t)} = D(\alpha^{(t)}, \beta^{(t)})$ is non-increasing,

$$
D^{(0)} \ge D^{(1)} \ge D^{(2)} \ge \dots \ge 0 ,
$$

and, bounded below by zero, it converges. On a finite sample there are finitely many distinct partitions, so the algorithm reaches a **fixed point** in finitely many steps — a configuration satisfying *both* Theorem 1 and Theorem 2 at once. Such a fixed point is a **local** minimum of $D$; it need not be global.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

The assignment step minimizes $D$ over the encoder with the decoder fixed (Theorem 1), so $D(\alpha^{(t+1)}, \beta^{(t)}) \le D(\alpha^{(t)}, \beta^{(t)})$. The update step minimizes $D$ over the decoder with the encoder fixed (Theorem 2), so $D(\alpha^{(t+1)}, \beta^{(t+1)}) \le D(\alpha^{(t+1)}, \beta^{(t)})$. Chaining the two inequalities gives $D^{(t+1)} \le D^{(t)}$, and a monotone sequence bounded below converges. On a finite training set the distortion depends only on the partition, of which there are at most $k^n$; monotone decrease through a finite set, with the lowest-index tie-break preventing cycles, forces termination at a partition where neither step changes anything — a point satisfying both conditions. That this point is only *local* is structural: $D$ is non-convex jointly in $(\alpha, \beta)$, and Lloyd is **block-coordinate descent** on that non-convex objective, which the per-step optimality cannot upgrade to global optimality. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

So Lloyd's algorithm is descent by construction: it cannot get worse, and on real data it converges quickly. The empty-cell repair fits inside this guarantee. Reseeding an orphaned codeword onto the farthest-served point changes nothing in the current step's distortion — the empty cell contributed nothing either way — but on the next assignment that point, now sitting on a codeword, is captured at zero cost, strictly lowering distortion. The monotone descent of Theorem 3 is the pedagogical claim the laboratory makes visible and the companion harness asserts iteration by iteration.

## $k$-means as the empirical special case

Theorems 1–3 are stated for a distribution $P$, but in practice we never have $P$ — we have a sample of corpus embeddings. Replace $P$ by the **empirical measure** $\hat{P} = \tfrac{1}{n}\sum_{j=1}^{n} \delta_{x_j}$ over the points $\{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$, and every object above specializes.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Lloyd under the empirical measure is k-means">

Under $\hat{P}$ with squared-Euclidean distortion, the distortion becomes the **within-cluster sum of squares**

$$
D = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{x_j \in R_i} \lVert x_j - c_i\rVert^2 ,
$$

the centroid condition becomes the sample mean $c_i = \tfrac{1}{|R_i|}\sum_{x_j \in R_i} x_j$, and Lloyd's algorithm becomes the standard $k$-means iteration.

</TheoremBlock>

This is not an analogy: $k$-means *is* Lloyd's algorithm under the empirical measure, and the within-cluster sum of squares *is* the vector-quantization distortion. The cluster centroids are the decoder codewords, and the whole apparatus — the alternating updates, the monotone objective, the local-optimum and initialization caveats — is developed in formalML's [clustering](https://formalml.com/topics/clustering) topic. A production codebook is trained exactly this way: run $k$-means on a sample of the corpus embeddings and keep the centroids as the codebook (the $k$-means quantizer behind a Faiss IVF or PQ index). The companion harness confirms the equivalence by matching our converged distortion, from the same initialization, against `scipy.cluster.vq.kmeans2`.

## Rate, distortion, and the curse of dimensionality

Larger codebooks cost more bits and earn less distortion, and the exchange rate between the two is governed by the dimension. The asymptotic law is Zador's, and it is the place where the geometry of the prerequisite topic returns with teeth.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={4} title="Zador's asymptotic distortion law (high-rate)">

For a source with a density on $\mathbb{R}^d$ satisfying mild regularity, the minimal distortion of the optimal $k$-codeword quantizer satisfies, as $k \to \infty$,

$$
D^\star(k) \sim C(d) \left( \int p(x)^{\frac{d}{d+2}}\, dx \right)^{\!\frac{d+2}{d}} k^{-2/d} ,
$$

so $D^\star(k) = \Theta\!\left(k^{-2/d}\right)$. In terms of the rate $R = \log_2 k$ bits per vector, $D^\star \sim \text{const}\cdot 2^{-2R/d}$. The constant $C(d)$ depends on the optimal point density and the best lattice in $\mathbb{R}^d$ and is known in closed form only in special cases.

</TheoremBlock>

We do not reprove this asymptotic result — it is Zador's (1982), surveyed in Gray and Neuhoff (1998) — but the exponent has a one-line explanation. To halve the diameter of every cell in $d$ dimensions you must tile space with $2^d$ times as many cells, so the codebook size $k$ must grow like the inverse $d$-th power of the cell diameter; since distortion scales as the *square* of the diameter, $D \sim k^{-2/d}$.

<TheoremBlock type="remark">

The exponent $2/d$ *is* the curse of dimensionality, in quantization form. In low dimension a codebook buys distortion cheaply — for $d = 1$, distortion falls like $k^{-2}$, so each extra bit (doubling $k$) cuts distortion fourfold — but as $d$ grows the exponent flattens toward zero and additional codewords barely help. This is the same ambient $d$ that, in [high-dimensional geometry](/topics/high-dimensional-geometry), concentrates pairwise distances; the escape is also the same. Real embeddings have low *effective* dimension, so the $d$ that governs $k^{-2/d}$ in practice is the intrinsic dimension, not the ambient one, which is why a modest codebook can reach usable distortion at all. The companion harness verifies the *scaling* directly: on uniform sources in $d = 1$ and $d = 2$, the fitted log-log slope of distortion against $k$ is $-2.01$ and $-1.05$, matching $-2/d$; we verify the exponent, not Zador's constant.

</TheoremBlock>

So the exact theorems are Theorems 1–3; the asymptotic law is Theorem 4, whose scaling we confirm numerically and whose constant we do not claim. With that honesty in place, the finance case study makes the tradeoff concrete.

## Finance case study

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text"]}>

The production financial-RAG system behind the [BM25](/topics/bm25-binary-independence-model) and [rank-fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) case studies stores high-dimensional embeddings of 10-K passages and earnings-call chunks. At ten million chunks the index is tens of gigabytes of `float32`, and the natural question is how cheaply each vector can be stored without wrecking retrieval. Vector quantization gives a direct answer: train a codebook and store an index per vector.

We make the point with a self-contained experiment on a synthetic cloud of $256$-dimensional embeddings — six topical groups (rates, credit, FX, equity, macro, earnings) on a low-rank-plus-noise manifold, a stand-in for the geometry a real encoder produces. We train codebooks of growing size by $k$-means and measure the rate–distortion tradeoff:

- a **raw vector is $256 \times 32 = 8192$ bits**; a **codeword index at $k = 256$ is $8$ bits** — a $\mathbf{1024\times}$ reduction in code size;
- **distortion falls from $71\%$ of the single-codeword baseline at $k = 2$ to $\mathbf{12.9\%}$ at $k = 256$**, monotonically in the rate but with diminishing returns — Zador's $k^{-2/d}$ with a high effective $d$;
- **recall@10 retained climbs from near zero to $\mathbf{52.8\%}$** at $k = 256$, the fraction of exact nearest neighbors the quantized index still surfaces.

The shape of the rate–distortion curve is the message. Each extra bit buys less than the last, because a single codebook over the whole $256$-dimensional space is fighting the curse of dimensionality head-on: to drive distortion down by a constant factor, the $k^{-2/d}$ law demands the codebook grow by a factor exponential in the effective dimension. That is the wall *product quantization* breaks — by splitting the vector into low-dimensional subspaces and quantizing each with its own small codebook, so the effective codebook size multiplies while each sub-codebook stays cheap — and the Voronoi partition of Theorem 1 is the coarse index the *inverted file* searches. Both are the next two topics in this track.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

It is not the output of a trained encoder, and a real encoder's angular geometry would call for *spherical* quantization — codewords as mean *directions* on the [hypersphere](/topics/hypersphere-vmf-geometry) rather than Euclidean centroids — which is exactly the squared-Euclidean-versus-cosine caveat below.

## The honest catch: a fixed point is only a local optimum

Theorems 1–3 guarantee that Lloyd's algorithm *converges* and that it *decreases* distortion to a configuration satisfying both optimality conditions. They do not guarantee that the configuration is the best one. The distortion is non-convex in the codebook, so the fixed point Lloyd reaches depends on where it starts, and different initializations land in different basins — as the laboratory's **Reseed** button shows directly: on the same four-blob cloud, one initialization reaches the global optimum at distortion $285.2$, another a near-miss local optimum at $313.9$, and a third gets stuck at $541.1$, nearly twice as bad, by merging two diagonally opposite blobs into a single cell that no local move can separate.

The pessimism is fundamental, not incidental: finding the globally optimal $k$-means partition is NP-hard, so no efficient algorithm reaches the global optimum in general. What practice offers is not a fix but a discipline. **$k$-means++ seeding** spreads the initial codewords out by sampling them with probability proportional to their squared distance from the codewords chosen so far, which comes with an $O(\log k)$ expected-approximation guarantee (Arthur and Vassilvitskii, 2007) — the one provable handle on the problem, and still only in expectation. **Multiple restarts**, keeping the lowest-distortion run, trade computation for a better local optimum. The companion harness shows both effects: across twelve random initializations the final distortion varies by hundreds, and $k$-means++ seeding lowers the mean. We present these as the honest state of the art — heuristics that improve the expected result without certifying the global one.

## Honest caveats

<RigorFlag>

Five qualifications keep the optimality honest:

1. **Local, not global.** Lloyd decreases distortion to a fixed point satisfying both conditions (Theorems 1–3), but $D$ is non-convex in the codebook, so the optimum is local and initialization-dependent. The global $k$-means problem is NP-hard; $k$-means++ ($O(\log k)$ in expectation) and restarts are cited heuristics, not fixes.
2. **Zador is asymptotic.** The law $D \sim C(d)\,k^{-2/d}$ is high-rate, and its constant $C(d)$ is known in closed form only in special cases. We verify the $k^{-2/d}$ *scaling* numerically on uniform sources and tie the exponent to the prerequisite geometry; we do not claim the constant.
3. **Ties and empty cells.** Cell boundaries and empty cells are measure-zero for a continuous source but real on a finite sample. We handle them by an explicit lowest-index tie-break and a deterministic furthest-point reseeding rule, not by pretending they do not occur.
4. **Squared-Euclidean is not cosine.** The centroid is the optimal representative for $\ell_2$ distortion, but cosine retrieval wants the normalized mean direction. The gap between Lloyd's Euclidean codeword and the angular geometry retrieval scores on is a modeling choice, not a theorem.
5. **The finance embeddings are synthetic.** The companion cloud is a low-rank-plus-noise stand-in, not a trained encoder, and the distortion and recall numbers are measured on it.

</RigorFlag>

It implements Lloyd's two half-steps from scratch — nearest-neighbor assignment with a lowest-index tie-break, centroid update with a furthest-point empty-cell repair — and runs them on the two-dimensional toy cloud the laboratory mirrors and on the synthetic finance cloud, training codebooks across the rate grid and measuring distortion and quantized recall@10.
