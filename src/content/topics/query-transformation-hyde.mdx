## From expanding the query to transforming it

The previous topic on pseudo-relevance feedback fixed a *vocabulary* mismatch. A query said "rate
guidance"; the relevant filings said "outlook" and "forecast" and never the word "guidance," so the
bare query missed them — until Rocchio and RM3 expanded the query with terms harvested from the
top-ranked feedback documents and bridged the gap. That mechanism lives in *term* space: it adds
words.

Dense retrieval moves the whole problem onto the sphere. A dual encoder maps the query and the
document into one shared embedding space and scores them by cosine similarity, so there is no
vocabulary to bridge — only geometry. And the geometry has its own version of the mismatch, sharper
and harder to see. A query is *question-shaped*: "What did the company guide for next quarter?" A
document is *answer-shaped*: three sentences of a 10-K stating the number. The two encoders are
trained to bring a matched pair close, but the *distributions* they induce on the sphere do not
coincide — questions cluster in one region, answers in another. A bare query therefore lands slightly
**off the document manifold**, and its nearest documents are mediocre: close enough to be plausible,
not close enough to be right.

We make this precise on the finance geometry the dense-retrieval topic built. Its document manifold is
a set of unit company prototypes — one passage per company, grouped into sectors. We reuse that
manifold exactly, and we build our own queries, because the dense topic drew its queries
$\kappa = 350$-tight on their own company, so its recall@1 is already $1.0$ — the "too-easy corpus"
that would hide the very gap we want to study.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The query–document gap">

Let $u_c$ be the unit embedding of company $c$'s document (its answer prototype) and let $g$ be a
fixed unit **offset axis** — the "generic document-ness" direction, taken as the normalized corpus
centroid. A bare query for company $c$ at **distribution-shift angle** $\theta$ is the great-circle
interpolation from the answer direction toward the offset axis,

$$
q_c(\theta) \;=\; \operatorname{normalize}\!\big(\cos\theta\,u_c \;+\; \sin\theta\,g\big),
$$

plus a small von Mises–Fisher spread. At $\theta = 0$ the query sits on the manifold (it *is* the
answer direction) and recall@1 is $1.0$. As $\theta$ grows every query is pulled toward the *same*
generic axis $g$, so the queries lose their company specificity and the document nearest $g$ starts to
win regardless of which company was asked about. Recall@1 falls. The gap is not noise — it is a
systematic rotation of the query distribution away from the document distribution.

</TheoremBlock>

Panel A of the laboratory traces this. At the operating shift $\theta = 75^\circ$ the bare query's recall@1
has fallen to $0.375$: most queries retrieve the wrong company's filing. The two-dimensional
projection shows why — the bare query (hollow square) sits apart from the document cloud, nearer the
generic center than the gold answer.

## HyDE: retrieve where the answers live

HyDE's move is disarmingly simple. Do not retrieve with the query. Instead ask a language model to
*write a hypothetical answer* — a fake document that would answer the question if it existed — embed
*that*, and retrieve real documents near its embedding. The hypothetical need not be factually correct;
it only needs to land in the right *neighborhood* of the document manifold. A generated paragraph about
a company's revenue guidance, however invented its numbers, embeds like a 10-K passage, not like a
question. It is answer-shaped by construction.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="HyDE corrects the distribution shift (demonstrated)">

Model the generator as drawing a hypothetical document $h$ from a von Mises–Fisher distribution
centered at the answer direction $u_c$. Because $h$ lives on the document manifold near $u_c$,
retrieving with $h$ recovers the gold document — *independently of the bare query's distribution-shift
angle $\theta$*. HyDE ignores where the query landed and synthesizes an on-manifold proxy in its place.
On the finance geometry a faithful generator recovers recall@1 $= 1.000$ at every $\theta$, against the
bare query's $0.375$ at $\theta = 75^\circ$ — the flat green line above the collapsing red one in Panel A.

The **collapse anchor** pins the construction: a *perfect* hypothetical — infinitely concentrated,
faithful — is exactly the gold document $u_c$, so HyDE retrieval with it reproduces the gold document's
own retrieval byte-for-byte (top-1 is the answer, and the full ranking is identical). The notebook
asserts this for every company.

</TheoremBlock>

That HyDE's recall does not depend on $\theta$ is the whole point, and worth dwelling on. The bare
query's position in embedding space carries the distribution shift; HyDE discards that position and
keeps only the query's *intent*, which the generator re-expresses in the document distribution's own
dialect. Distribution shift cannot survive a step that re-samples from the target distribution.

## Averaging hypotheticals: a Monte-Carlo estimator with a bias floor

One hypothetical is a noisy draw. HyDE in practice averages several — generate $k$ hypothetical
documents, embed each, and retrieve with the normalized mean
$\hat h_k = \operatorname{normalize}\!\big(\tfrac1k\sum_i h_i\big)$. This is precisely a **Monte-Carlo
estimator** of the generation distribution's center, and it inherits that estimator's two-sided
behavior.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Variance falls toward the 1/k rate (demonstrated)">

For a faithful generator, $\hat h_k$ is a consistent estimator of the answer direction $u_c$ — the
maximum-likelihood mean-direction estimate of the von Mises–Fisher samples. Its angular deficit
$1 - \langle \hat h_k, u_c\rangle$ falls monotonically in $k$ and approaches the Monte-Carlo $1/k$ rate
asymptotically: each doubling of $k$ drives the deficit-halving ratio toward $\tfrac12$. On the
finance geometry the deficit falls $0.658 \to 0.153$ across $k = 1 \to 20$, and as the estimate
sharpens, recall@1 *rises* with $k$ from $0.602$ toward $1.0$ (Panel B, the green curve). Averaging
denoises the hypothetical.

</TheoremBlock>

So far this only argues for sampling more. The honest result is the other side of the estimator.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="Hallucination bias is irreducible (demonstrated)">

Suppose the generator **hallucinates** on a fraction $p$ of queries — for those, it writes a document
about the *wrong* company, so its generation center is not $u_c$ but a wrong direction. Then $\hat h_k$
is consistent for the *wrong* center: $\langle\hat h_k, \text{center}\rangle \to 1$ while
$\langle\hat h_k, u_c\rangle$ stays bounded away from $1$, and the query retrieves the wrong document
no matter how large $k$ is. Recall therefore plateaus at a **ceiling near $1 - p$**: on the finance
geometry the large-$k$ recall is $1.000$, $0.775$, $0.515$ at hallucination rates $p = 0,\ 0.25,\ 0.5$
(Panel B, the dashed reference lines). Averaging reduces variance; it cannot reduce bias.

</TheoremBlock>

This is the load-bearing honesty of HyDE, and the cleanest way to state what it costs.

<RigorFlag>

HyDE does not retrieve for free. It trades a problem it can fix — the query-document distribution
mismatch, which a single re-encoding removes — for a problem it cannot: the generator's bias. A bare
query is *unbiased but off-manifold*; a hypothetical is *on-manifold but possibly wrong*. Averaging
$k$ hypotheticals is exactly the move that separates the two: it drives variance toward zero at the
$1/k$ rate, exposing the bias underneath as an irreducible recall ceiling. When the generation is
reliable the trade is overwhelmingly worth it (recall $0.375 \to 1.000$ here); when the generator
hallucinates about the entity in the question, no sampling budget rescues those queries. The honest
deployment question is not "does HyDE help" but "is my generator faithful enough that $1 - p$ clears
the bar." Whether a hypothetical is *faithful* is itself a measurable quantity — the subject of
faithfulness and groundedness, which this topic unblocks.

</RigorFlag>

## HyDE is the neural generalization of pseudo-relevance feedback

Step back and the shape is familiar. Rocchio's relevance feedback moves a query toward the centroid of
pseudo-relevant documents, $q' = a\,q + b\,\text{centroid}$; RM3 does the language-model version. HyDE
is the *same update*, lifted onto the sphere, with one substitution: the centroid is taken over
**generated** hypotheticals instead of **retrieved** documents.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={4} title="Generated feedback beats retrieved feedback on an off-manifold query (demonstrated)">

Write the embedding-space update $q'(\alpha) = (1-\alpha)\,q + \alpha\,\text{centroid}$, renormalized;
$\alpha = 0$ is the bare query and $\alpha = 1$ is the pure pseudo-document (canonical HyDE). Two ways
to form the centroid:

- **HyDE** — the mean of generated hypothetical embeddings. On an off-manifold query this centroid is
  clean (the generator is answer-shaped), so recall climbs from $0.406$ at $\alpha = 0$ to $0.984$ at
  $\alpha = 0.75$ and $0.961$ at $\alpha = 1$ — a little of the original query mixed in is even slightly
  better than the pure hypothetical.
- **Real pseudo-relevance feedback** — the mean of the bare query's top-retrieved documents. Here the
  feedback is *polluted*: the off-manifold query retrieves the wrong documents, so its centroid points
  the wrong way and recall *falls* from $0.406$ to $0.195$ as $\alpha$ grows.

Panel C shows the two curves diverging. The lesson is exactly when the neural generalization earns its
keep: classical relevance feedback bootstraps from retrieved documents, so it inherits the bad query's
mistakes; HyDE bootstraps from a generator that never saw the corpus, so a bad query cannot poison it.

</TheoremBlock>

The ancestry is not just an analogy — the notebook *imports* the pseudo-relevance-feedback module and
reruns its term-space RM3 and Rocchio, reproducing the prerequisite's improve-then-drift curve
(recall@4 $0.5 \to 1.0$ with a little feedback, drifting back to $0.5$ when the feedback set
over-expands). That is the inset in Panel C. HyDE inherits the improvement and, by generating rather
than retrieving its feedback, sidesteps the drift that comes from feeding back the wrong documents.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="A news query against a 10-K answer" modality={["text", "pdf", "news"]}>

A portfolio manager asks, in the register of a news headline, *"Is the company walking back its
margin guidance?"* The answer lives in a 10-K's MD&A section, written in the register of an
accountant: "gross margin is expected to be in the range of…". The two are answer- and
question-shaped respectively, and a bare dense query retrieves adjacent-but-wrong passages — last
year's guidance, a competitor's filing, the risk-factors boilerplate. HyDE asks the model to draft the
*kind of paragraph that would answer the question* — a plausible MD&A sentence about margin guidance —
and retrieves against that. The draft may invent the number; it does not matter, because the embedding
only has to land in the MD&A neighborhood of the right company. The failure mode is the honest one: if
the model drafts a paragraph about the *wrong* company's margins, HyDE confidently retrieves the wrong
filing, and averaging more drafts of the same mistake does not help. Faithfulness of the hypothetical
is the thing to monitor.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Where this sits

HyDE is a *query-side* transform: it runs before retrieval, spending one generation call to move the
query onto the document distribution. Read alongside its sibling on retrieval versus long context, the
two are the same rate-distortion trade taken on opposite sides of the pipeline — long context spends
read-time compute to lower answer distortion; HyDE spends generate-time compute to lower retrieval
distortion. Both are governed by the same geometry of the finance manifold, and both are honest about
the cost.

What HyDE leaves open is the quality of the thing it generates. Its whole risk is concentrated in
whether the hypothetical is faithful to the question's intent — a property we have so far only modeled
with a hallucination rate $p$. Turning that rate into a *measured* quantity, on real generated text
rather than a synthetic von Mises–Fisher knob, is the next step.
