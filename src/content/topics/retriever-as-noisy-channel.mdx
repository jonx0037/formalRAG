## From "how many bits did it add" to "how many bits survive the channel"

The [previous topic](/topics/pmi-retrieval-value) measured what a retrieved document is worth to the
generator in bits: the conditional mutual information $I(A;D\mid Q) = H(A\mid Q) - H(A\mid Q,D)$, the entropy
retrieval removes from the answer. That measurement assumed the document arrived. A deployed retriever is not
so obliging — it misses relevant filings and returns plausible wrong ones — and the question this topic asks
is what those failures do to the bits, and what the bits do to the answer.

The reframing is a single move: read the retriever as a **communication channel**. A query $q$ enters; the
channel emits a context — one retrieved document $d \sim p(d\mid q)$; the generator decodes an answer
$\hat a$. This is the noisy-channel reading of retrieval that goes back to
[Berger and Lafferty](https://doi.org/10.1145/312624.312681), made information-theoretic: a channel has a
**capacity** — the most information it can carry — and a **converse** — Fano's inequality, which turns
whatever uncertainty the channel leaves into a floor on the decoder's error. The two failure modes of a
retriever are the two classical ways a channel corrupts a message, and they map onto the two words of this
topic's title. A **recall** failure is an *erasure*; a **precision** failure is a *substitution*. They are
co-equal headlines, and they tell opposite stories about what an information bound can see.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The retrieval channel and its two corruptions">
The **clean channel** is the answer model of the previous topic: input $q$, output a document
$D \sim p(d\mid q)$, decoder $\hat a = \operatorname{argmax}_a p(a\mid q, D)$. Its delivered information is
$I(A;D\mid Q)$ and its residual uncertainty is $H(A\mid Q,D) = \mathbb{E}_{q}\,\mathbb{E}_{d\mid q}\,
H\big(p(\cdot\mid q,d)\big)$.

The **erasure channel** (a recall failure) returns the retrieved context with probability $\mathrm{recall}$;
with probability $1-\mathrm{recall}$ the context is *erased* and the generator falls back to the
non-informative uniform belief — without a document, it knows nothing.

The **substitution channel** (a precision failure) returns the relevant filing with probability $1-
\varepsilon$ and, with probability $\varepsilon$, a plausible same-sector distractor in its place.
</TheoremBlock>

We will treat the two channels in turn. The corpus is the one the previous topic built and this one never
rebuilds: four sectors, two companies each, one filing per company, so $K=8$ answers that are also the $8$
documents, with $32$ sector-ambiguous queries and temperatures $\tau = \tau_{\mathrm{doc}} = 0.2$. On the
clean channel the answer model carries $I(A;D\mid Q) = 1.18$ bits, dropping $H(A\mid Q) = 2.06$ bits of prior
uncertainty to $H(A\mid Q,D) = 0.88$ bits — the numbers the notebook owns and asserts.

## Recall is an erasure channel; its capacity is the recall

The cleanest channel in information theory is the **binary erasure channel**: each symbol arrives intact with
probability $1-\varepsilon$ or is replaced by an erasure mark $?$ with probability $\varepsilon$, and the
receiver always knows *which*. Its capacity is $1-\varepsilon$: the fraction of symbols that survive. A
retriever that misses the relevant filing with probability $1-\mathrm{recall}$ is exactly this channel for
the answer, and the consequence for the delivered bits is an identity, not an approximation.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The delivered bits are the recall fraction of the clean bits">
Under the erasure channel, the information the channel delivers about the answer is
$$
I_{\mathrm{erasure}}(A; D \mid Q) = \mathrm{recall}\cdot I(A;D\mid Q).
$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">
Condition on the channel's binary state $E$ (transmitted vs. erased), which is independent of the answer
given the query. With probability $\mathrm{recall}$ the generator sees the clean document, contributing
$I(A;D\mid Q)$; with probability $1-\mathrm{recall}$ it sees the erasure symbol. The erasure symbol is a
*deterministic function of the query* (the uniform fallback depends on nothing else), so conditioned on $Q$
it carries no information about $A$: $I(A;\,?\mid Q) = 0$. Hence
$I_{\mathrm{erasure}}(A;D\mid Q) = \mathrm{recall}\cdot I(A;D\mid Q) + (1-\mathrm{recall})\cdot 0$. The
notebook computes the left side directly across the recall grid and matches $\mathrm{recall}\cdot I_0$ to
machine precision (<code>test_bec_capacity_linear</code>).
</TheoremBlock>

So **recall is literally the surviving fraction of the relevant bits** — the erasure-channel capacity, read
off the retriever. Panel A of the laboratory makes the picture concrete: as you lower the recall, the
delivered-bits meter empties in proportion and the answer belief flattens from its peaked clean shape toward
the uniform distribution. Nothing about the *answers* changed; the channel simply passed fewer of the bits
that distinguish them.

## Fano's inequality: the residual entropy is a floor on the answer error

What recall fails to deliver does not vanish; it stays behind as residual uncertainty. Under the erasure
channel the residual entropy is a convex blend,
$$
H_{\mathrm{erasure}}(A\mid Q,D) = \mathrm{recall}\cdot H(A\mid Q,D) + (1-\mathrm{recall})\cdot \log_2 K,
$$
rising from $0.88$ bits on the clean channel toward the full $\log_2 8 = 3$ bits of a uniform guess. The
reason this residual matters is **Fano's inequality**, the converse half of channel coding: it lower-bounds
the error of *any* decoder by the uncertainty the channel leaves.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Fano's inequality — the hallucination floor">
Let the answer $A$ take one of $K$ values and let $\hat a = \hat a(q,d)$ be any decoder with Bayes error
$P_e = \Pr[\hat a \neq A]$. Then
$$
H(A\mid Q,D) \;\le\; H_b(P_e) + P_e \log_2(K-1),
\qquad\text{hence}\qquad
P_e \;\ge\; \frac{H(A\mid Q,D) - 1}{\log_2 K},
$$
where $H_b$ is the binary entropy. No decoder — no generator, however capable — drives the answer error
below this floor.
</TheoremBlock>

The loose form on the right makes the structure visible. The floor is **vacuous while $H(A\mid Q,D) < 1$
bit**: a channel that leaves less than one bit of uncertainty permits, in principle, a perfect decoder. As
recall falls and the residual climbs past one bit, the floor lifts off zero and rises — on this corpus the
crossover is at $\mathrm{recall} \approx 0.94$, after which the floor climbs to $0.67$ at full erasure, where
the generator can do no better than guess. Panel B draws all of it: the residual entropy rising, the Fano
floor (loose and tight) lifting off at the one-bit line, and the model's Bayes error sitting above the floor
at every operating point, exactly as the theorem requires (<code>test_fano_is_a_theorem</code>). This is the
sense in which **recall sets the floor**: the bits the retriever fails to deliver become a hard lower bound
on the generator's error that no amount of generation skill can beat.

## Precision is a substitution channel — and Fano goes blind

The recall story is the optimistic one: the channel is honest about what it does not know, the residual
entropy rises, and Fano's floor faithfully tracks the growing difficulty. The precision story is the
treacherous one. A false positive does not erase the relevant filing — it **substitutes** a plausible
distractor, and the generator reads it with full confidence.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Confident contamination is invisible to the entropy floor">
Under the substitution channel, the realized error against the true answer rises linearly to $1$ as
$\varepsilon \to 1$: the generator reads the distractor and answers its company. Yet the residual entropy
$H(A\mid Q,D)$ stays *below one bit* throughout — both the gold filing and the distractor produce a sharp,
confident posterior; they merely peak on different companies. Consequently the Fano floor stays pinned at
zero, and the gap between the realized error and the model's Bayes error widens without bound. An
entropy-based limit cannot see a confidently wrong channel.
</TheoremBlock>

This is a proposition we *run*, not assume. Across the query set, reading the gold filing is correct for
every query and reading the same-sector distractor is wrong for every query, so the realized error is exactly
$\varepsilon$; meanwhile the residual entropy creeps only from $0.60$ to $0.87$ bits — never reaching the one
bit Fano needs to react — so the floor reads $0$ at every $\varepsilon$ and the confident-wrong gap grows
from $-0.12$ to $+0.76$ (Panel C, <code>test_confident_wrong_gap_widens</code>). The asymmetry with the
recall case is the whole point. Recall failures are *honest*: they raise the entropy, and Fano accounts for
them. Precision failures are *confident*: they keep the entropy low while making the answer wrong, and no
entropy bound accounts for them at all.

<RigorFlag>
Fano's inequality bounds the **Bayes error** — the irreducible risk of a decoder that trusts the model's own
posterior — not the **realized error** against the truth. The two coincide only when the model is calibrated.
A confidently-wrong retriever is precisely a miscalibrated channel: its posterior is sharp (low entropy) but
pointed at the wrong answer, so the realized error runs far above the Fano floor. Closing that gap is not an
information-theory problem — it is the job of the [calibration and significance
layer](/topics/significance-testing-calibration) and of faithfulness evaluation, which measure the
realized-vs-Bayes discrepancy the entropy floor cannot. The information limit is real and binding for an
honest channel; it is silent for a confident one. As with the previous topic, the answer model $p(a\mid q,d)$
is a synthetic von Mises–Fisher softmax stand-in for a trained generator, so every bit is exact for the model
and only illustrative of a deployed system; and because this corpus places the gold filing at rank one for
every query, recall@k is saturated and the precision panel measures contamination as bits of uncertainty
added rather than as an error U-curve.
</RigorFlag>

## The operating point is a rate–distortion choice

The two failures meet in a single design decision. Reading more documents raises recall (more chances to
catch the relevant filing) but lowers precision (more false positives in the context), and the generator's
belief blends them. On this corpus the gold filing is always rank one, so recall@k is pinned at $1$ and
contamination cannot show up as a wrong answer — it shows up as **bits of uncertainty added**: as $k$ grows
from $1$ to $8$, precision falls as $1/k$ while the blended belief's entropy rises from $0.60$ toward the
full marginal $H(A\mid Q) = 2.06$ bits (Panel D). The retriever is choosing a point on a
[rate–distortion](https://formalml.com/topics/rate-distortion) curve — spending *rate* (bits read) to buy
down *distortion* (answer error) — which is also the
[information bottleneck](https://formalml.com/topics/information-bottleneck) the generator imposes on the
corpus: keep the bits that predict the answer, discard the rest. Erasure drops the bits the answer needs;
substitution injects bits it must not trust.

## What the channel can and cannot deliver, in one line

A retriever is a channel, and its two metrics are the channel's two error mechanisms. **Recall is the
erasure capacity** — the surviving fraction of the relevant bits — and what it fails to deliver becomes, by
Fano's inequality, a floor on the answer error that no generator beats: $P_e \ge (H(A\mid Q,D) -
1)/\log_2 K$. **Precision is the substitution rate**, and its failures are confident: they keep the residual
entropy low while driving the realized answer wrong, so the information floor sees nothing and the gap it
misses is the calibration problem. The bits a retriever delivers bound what the generator *can* do; whether
the generator *does* it is a question of trusting the right bits — the relevance term in context selection,
the faithfulness of the grounded answer, and the calibration of its confidence, each a layer above this one.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="What does a missed filing cost the answer, in bits?" modality={["text", "pdf"]}>
Take a sector-ambiguous query over a pair of same-sector companies — say two regional banks whose 10-Ks read
alike. The clean retriever delivers $1.18$ bits about which company the query means, dropping the answer
uncertainty from $2.06$ to $0.88$ bits, and a calibrated generator answers correctly. Now degrade the
retriever two ways. **Erase** the relevant 10-K a quarter of the time (recall $0.75$): the delivered bits
fall to $0.89$, the residual entropy climbs past one bit, and Fano's floor lifts off zero — the answer error
now has a hard lower bound the generator cannot argue its way out of. **Substitute** the *other* bank's 10-K
instead (a precision failure): the generator reads it and confidently reports the wrong company's revenue,
the residual entropy barely moves, and every entropy-based monitor reads "low uncertainty, all is well." The
first failure is loud and bounded; the second is silent and unbounded — which is why a production
filings-RAG system needs both a recall floor it respects and a faithfulness check the floor cannot provide.
</FinanceCaseStudy>
