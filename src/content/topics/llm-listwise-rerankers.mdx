## Overview & motivation

The [previous topic](/topics/lambdarank-lambdamart-listwise) closed on a quiet limitation. Every learned
ranker we have built — pointwise regression, pairwise RankNet, LambdaRank, the listwise ListMLE loss —
scores each document from a **fixed feature vector** $\mathbf{x}_d$. The documents are coupled only through
the loss: ListMLE's Plackett–Luce likelihood compares a document against the suffix of the ranking, but the
*score* of any one document never depends on which other documents are present. A relevance model that
reads document $d$ in isolation can never notice that $d$ is redundant given $d'$, or that $d$ answers the
query only because $d'$ supplies the context.

An LLM reranker drops that assumption. Feed the whole candidate list into one model and it emits a
**permutation** directly — it ranks the documents by reading them in each other's presence. This is the
most listwise objective there is: not a loss that couples documents, but a model whose output *is* the
ordered list. We will build it up in four movements, each a piece of genuine mathematics, and we will be
honest throughout about what a synthetic study can and cannot show.

The lab is the topic in miniature. Panel A is the sliding window as a bubble sort — the call-count law and

negatives — and grades $g_d \in \{0,1,2,3\}$ from the [NDCG topic's](/topics/ndcg-discount-geometry)
exact-MaxSim oracle tertiles.

A word on the model, because it is load-bearing. This site bakes only reproducible numbers and never calls
a cloud service, so there is **no real LLM** anywhere in this topic. We model the LLM as a *seeded noisy
permutation oracle*: it sees a window of candidates and emits the ideal ranking $\pi^\*$ corrupted by
Plackett–Luce noise at a temperature $\tau$ and, in one movement, a position-dependent bias. Every provable
claim is therefore **algorithmic** — a sorting guarantee, an aggregation variance, a distillation cost —
never "the LLM is the most accurate reranker." That last claim is empirical, and we mark it out of scope.

## The listwise permutation objective

Begin where the predecessor ended: the **Plackett–Luce** model, the probability distribution over
permutations whose negative log-likelihood is the ListMLE loss. We use it now not as a loss to minimize
but as a *generative* model of what the LLM emits.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The LLM as a Plackett–Luce sampler">
Assign each document an **ability** $a_d$ equal to $-\,\mathrm{rank}_d$, the negative of its position in
the ideal order $\pi^\*$ (so the best document has the largest ability). At temperature $\tau$ the LLM emits
a permutation $\pi$ with probability
$$
P_\tau(\pi \mid a) \;=\; \prod_{r=1}^{n} \frac{\exp(a_{\pi(r)}/\tau)}{\sum_{r' \geq r}\exp(a_{\pi(r')}/\tau)},
$$
a sequential softmax: at each rank the next document is drawn in proportion to its exponentiated ability
among those not yet placed. Equivalently — and this is how we sample reproducibly — $\pi$ is the argsort of
$a/\tau$ perturbed by independent Gumbel noise.
</TheoremBlock>

The temperature is the noise dial. As $\tau \to 0$ the deterministic abilities dominate any finite Gumbel
draw and the sample is exactly $\pi^\*$; as $\tau \to \infty$ the noise dominates and the order is uniform.
Because we index the abilities by $\pi^\*$-rank rather than by raw oracle score, the $\tau \to 0$ limit
reproduces the *grade-first* ideal order byte-for-byte, for any relationship between grades and scores.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The ListMLE bridge (collapse anchors)">
Two facts tie the simulated oracle to the predecessor's exact machinery. First, the $\tau \to 0$ sample
equals the imported `optimal_permutation` $\pi^\*$ for every query and every seed. Second, the negative
log-likelihood of *any* emitted permutation under the temperature-$\tau$ abilities equals the imported
`listmle_loss_scores` evaluated at those abilities, to machine precision. The LLM reranker is, by
construction, a Plackett–Luce sampler whose mean is the listwise objective we already studied.
</TheoremBlock>

This is the whole conceptual move. The LLM does not optimize a loss when it emits a permutation; it
*samples* from a distribution whose mode is the ideal ranking. Everything that follows asks what we can
guarantee about that sampling under the one real constraint a language model imposes: a bounded context.

## The sliding window is a bubble sort

An LLM context fits only $w \ll n$ candidates. RankGPT (Sun et al.) handles this exactly as a
comparison sort with a bounded comparator: slide a window of size $w$ across the list with step $s$,
re-sorting the documents inside each window, and repeat for $P$ passes. The window slides **back-to-front**
— the last window first — so that a relevant document buried in the tail can rise toward the front in a
single sweep.

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={3} title="Sliding-window reranking">
Given the current order of $n$ candidates, a window size $w$, a step $s$, and a pass count $P$:
for each pass, for each window start $t = n-w,\, n-w-s,\, \dots,\, 0$ (back to front), replace the block of
documents at positions $[t,\, t+w)$ with the oracle's emitted permutation of that block. Return the final
order. Each window placement is one oracle call.
</TheoremBlock>

The cost is the heart of the systems story. There are $\lceil (n-w)/s \rceil + 1$ window placements per
pass, so the total is
$$
\text{calls} \;=\; P\cdot\Big(\Big\lceil \tfrac{n-w}{s} \Big\rceil + 1\Big) \;=\; O\!\Big(\tfrac{n}{s}\Big)\,P.
$$
For our pool ($n = 60$, $w = 15$, $s = 10$, $P = 4$) that is **24** oracle calls — against the
$\binom{60}{2} = 1770$ comparisons an all-pairs method would make, a structural saving of about $74\times$.
Pointwise scoring sits between, at $n = 60$ single-document judgments.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={4} title="One pass bubbles the best; passes refine">
With a *perfect* comparator ($\tau \to 0$), one back-to-front pass places the global best document inside
the top window $[0, w)$ — the bubble-sort guarantee, since each overlapping window carries the strongest
remaining document one window-length toward the front. With a *noisy* comparator the guarantee weakens to a
trend: the seed-averaged top-k recall is monotone in the number of passes, climbing from $0.167$ (the
scrambled pool) through $0.525,\ 0.758,\ 0.846$ to $0.868$ at four passes, then plateauing. And the slide
direction matters: one back-to-front pass reaches recall $0.508$ where front-to-back reaches only $0.247$,
because front-to-back advances a buried document by just one window per pass.
</TheoremBlock>

<RigorFlag>
A single pass guarantees the *top-1*, not the full top-k. The comparison-sorting lower bound
$\Omega(n\log n)$ (Knuth) is the reason: a bounded-window sweep makes $O(n/s)$ comparisons per pass, far
fewer than the $n\log n$ a correct sort needs, so it cannot be exact in one pass. Recall climbs with passes
toward a noise-limited plateau and may dip slightly past it as the noisy oracle re-sorts an already-good list —
which is why we report a seed-averaged curve and never claim per-seed monotonicity.
</RigorFlag>

## Positional bias: lost in the middle

A real LLM does not attend to its context uniformly. It attends most to the **ends** and least to the
**middle** — the lost-in-the-middle effect (Liu et al.) we met in the
[long-context topic](/topics/retrieval-vs-long-context). We import that topic's U-curve verbatim and add it
to each document's ability as a function of its position in the window: a document near the center of the
window is perceived as weaker than it is.

The bias **bites**. With a sharp oracle, so that the position effect is isolated from sampling noise,
recall@10 falls from $0.981$ (the unbiased oracle) to $0.910$ when the dip is on. The fix is the one the
literature uses: present each window in several *random* orders and aggregate the results. A document at the
center in one presentation is at an edge in another, so the position-dependent penalty averages to a
constant across documents and its systematic component cancels — recovering recall to $0.968$, toward but
not beyond the unbiased ceiling. (Reversing a window does *not* work: the center of a reversed window is
still its center, so the trough is fixed; random presentations are required.)

<RigorFlag>
The U-curve is an *empirical* stand-in for trained-transformer behavior (Liu et al.), baked as a
per-position attention multiplier, not derived from first principles. Its depth is a model parameter; we
flag it as a knob, not a constant. What is provable is the mechanism: averaging a position-indexed penalty
over presentations in which each document visits every position flattens that penalty to its mean.
</RigorFlag>

## Aggregating noisy permutations

A reranker that emits multiple windows, or is sampled multiple times, or is shown the candidates in several
orders, produces $K$ noisy permutations that must be reconciled into one. This is the **rank-aggregation**
problem from social choice, and we reuse the [fusion topic's](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) machinery directly:
Borda count, reciprocal-rank fusion, and the **Kemeny median** — the permutation minimizing total
Kendall-$\tau$ disagreement, which is NP-hard.

The elegant aggregator is the one Dwork, Kumar, Naor, and Sivakumar built for the web: a **Markov chain**.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={5} title="Aggregation as a comparison random walk">
Build a random walk over the candidates: from document $i$, step to a uniformly chosen document $j$ if the
*majority* of the $K$ ballots rank $j$ above $i$, and otherwise stay. With a small restart probability the
chain is ergodic, and its **stationary distribution** scores the documents — a candidate beaten by many
others is rarely visited, a candidate that beats many is visited often. Ranking by stationary mass gives a
consensus that approximates the Kemeny median in polynomial time. On a six-document subset its Kendall-cost
ratio to the brute-force optimum is $1.00$, against $1.03$ for Borda and RRF.
</TheoremBlock>

Why aggregate at all? Because $K$ noisy ballots concentrate on the truth. The per-document averaged rank is
a sample mean, so its standard deviation shrinks at the central-limit rate $1/\sqrt{K}$ — in our run the
quantity $\mathrm{std}\cdot\sqrt{K}$ stays near-constant across $K$. The consequence is that the consensus
Kendall-$\tau$ to $\pi^\*$ falls monotonically: from about $29.5$ discordant pairs at $K=1$ down through
$15.3,\ 9.9,\ 6.4$ to $3.2$ at $K=32$, for all three aggregators.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" number={6} title="K = 1 is the single ballot">
Borda and RRF return the lone permutation unchanged when $K = 1$ — a single voter's consensus is its own
ballot — so the aggregation curve begins exactly at the un-aggregated reranker and improves from there. The
Markov chain is the one exception: with a single ballot its walk is absorbed at that ballot's top document,
so its $K = 1$ behavior is degenerate; we anchor it instead to the Kemeny approximation and the monotone
$\tau$-decrease.
</TheoremBlock>

## Distilling the permutation oracle

The teacher is expensive: at $O(n/s)$ windowed calls per query, each call an autoregressive generation over
$w$ documents, it cannot run at retrieval scale. The fix that made open listwise reranking practical —
RankVicuna, RankZephyr — is **distillation**: train a cheap student to imitate the teacher's permutations.

Our student is a linear listwise scorer, fit by the imported ListMLE solve to the teacher's *emitted*
orders in place of the ideal $\pi^\*$. This gives a clean collapse anchor.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={7} title="A perfect teacher's student is the predecessor's fit">
A perfect teacher ($\tau \to 0$) emits $\pi^\*$ for every query, and the listwise fit to $\pi^\*$ *is* the
predecessor's `fit_listmle`. Therefore the distilled student of a noiseless LLM teacher equals
`fit_listmle` byte-for-byte — the distilled student of a perfect permutation oracle is exactly the listwise
scorer we started from. A noisy teacher's student degrades gracefully but still beats the best single leg,
and — the payoff — it answers at **zero** inference LLM calls, the teacher's cost having been paid once,
offline. Pricing the windowed teacher call an order of magnitude above a precomputed-embedding lookup, the
student's query-time speedup is about $6000\times$.
</TheoremBlock>

This is the honest reading of "the LLM is a heuristic, not a loss." The teacher does not descend an
objective when it emits a permutation per query; the Plackett–Luce loss enters only when we *train the
student* on the teacher's outputs. The objective lives in the distillation, not in the inference.

## The cost–quality frontier

<FinanceCaseStudy>
A production financial reranker fuses a lexical signal over filings, a dense signal over earnings-call
transcripts, and a late-interaction signal over news — the same three-leg substrate throughout this track.
Putting six rerankers on one shared pool, with cost measured in **LLM calls per query** (the cross-encoder
is the unit a windowed call is priced against, not a competitor here), traces a clean Pareto frontier. The
two no-LLM baselines anchor the cheap end: the best single leg reaches NDCG@10 $0.689$ and reciprocal-rank
fusion $0.732$, both at zero LLM calls. The **distilled student** dominates them at the same zero cost,
$0.769$. The LLM rerankers climb the quality axis at rising cost: the **sliding window** reaches $0.865$ at
$24$ calls, **pointwise** $0.868$ at $60$, and the **all-pairs** method $0.944$ at $1770$ — the quality
ceiling, because it aggregates the most comparisons, at the highest price.
</FinanceCaseStudy>

The frontier is the systems verdict, and it is structural rather than statistical. The sliding window
reaches within a confidence interval of the all-pairs ceiling at a $74\times$ saving in calls; the distilled
student buys most of the no-LLM ceiling's improvement at zero inference cost. These are the seed-free wins —
the $O(n/s)$ call-count law, the $1/\sqrt{K}$ aggregation rate, the distillation speedup — and they hold
regardless of the seed. The *quality* deltas among the methods, by contrast, sit inside the confidence
interval on this forgiving corpus, exactly as the predecessor warned, and we do not over-read them.

## Where this points

This is the **terminal node** of the ranking-fusion learning-to-rank sub-track. With it the track is
complete: pointwise and pairwise reductions, LambdaRank and the listwise objectives, the cross-encoder
cascade, and now the LLM reranker that emits a permutation directly. There is no further topic in this
sub-track to point to.

The thread that carries forward is one of honesty about objectives. An autoregressive permutation model,
like LambdaRank before it, descends *no scalar loss* at inference — it is a heuristic ordering procedure,
and the Plackett–Luce loss enters only when we distill it into a student. The reader who wants to keep
pulling that thread can return to the [evaluation layer](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr),
where a metric is an estimator with a confidence interval, and ask the question this topic has been quietly
answering all along: when the seed-free wins are structural and the quality deltas sit inside the interval,
which differences are real?

<RigorFlag>
Three honesty checks close the topic. The LLM is a *simulated* noisy permutation oracle — no real model is
called, so every quality figure is a controlled stand-in and "the LLM is most accurate" is out of scope.
The provable content is algorithmic: the call-count law $P\cdot(\lceil (n-w)/s\rceil + 1)$, the bubble-sort
bubbling and its $\Omega(n\log n)$ floor, the presentation-averaging that flattens the positional bias, the
$1/\sqrt{K}$ concentration of $K$ ballots, and the byte-for-byte collapse of a perfect teacher's student to
the predecessor's fit. And the seed-free wins are structural — the costs and the rates — not the aggregate
scores, which sit inside the confidence interval.
</RigorFlag>
