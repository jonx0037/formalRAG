Every retrieval model so far has matched a query against documents *as written*. But a query and the documents that answer it routinely use different words. An analyst searches for *rate guidance*; the filing that answers the question discusses the company's *outlook* and *rate forecast* and never once writes "guidance." [BM25](/topics/bm25-binary-independence-model) and [query likelihood](/topics/query-likelihood-language-models) cannot match a term that is not there, so the most on-point document sinks beneath worse ones that happen to share the query's vocabulary. This is the **vocabulary mismatch** problem, and relevance feedback is the classical fix: use the documents you have already retrieved to *repair the query*, then search again.

The catch is in the assumption. We rarely have real relevance judgments at query time, so **pseudo-relevance feedback** simply *assumes the top-k retrieved documents are relevant* and harvests expansion terms from them. When that assumption holds, feedback bridges the vocabulary gap and recall jumps. When it fails — when an off-topic document sits near the top — its words pollute the query and the results drift away from the user's intent. The whole topic lives in the tension between those two outcomes.

<QueryExpansionFeedbackLab client:visible />

The laboratory above runs RM3 over a worked corpus for the query *rate guidance*. Two of the four relevant documents are synonym-only — they say *outlook* and *forecast*, never "guidance" — so the no-feedback ranking misses them and recall@4 is only $0.5$. Slide the feedback size to one or two documents and the relevance model adds *outlook* and *forecast*, the synonym filings surface, and recall reaches $1.0$. Keep sliding: off-topic feedback documents add *budget* and *costs*, the query drifts, and a relevant document drops back out.

## Rocchio: the centroid update

The vector-space answer, due to Rocchio, treats query repair as geometry: push the query vector toward the documents marked relevant and away from those marked non-relevant.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Rocchio update">

Given sets of relevant documents $D_r$ and non-relevant documents $D_{nr}$ (as tf-idf vectors), the updated query is

$$
q' = \alpha\, q + \beta\,\frac{1}{|D_r|}\sum_{d \in D_r} d - \gamma\,\frac{1}{|D_{nr}|}\sum_{d \in D_{nr}} d,
$$

with weights $\alpha, \beta, \gamma \ge 0$. In **pseudo-relevance feedback** we set $\gamma = 0$ and take $D_r$ to be the top retrieved documents, then re-rank by cosine similarity to $q'$.

</TheoremBlock>

The two averages are the **centroids** of the relevant and non-relevant sets, so Rocchio is a [nearest-centroid](https://formalml.com/topics/clustering) construction: it shifts the query toward the middle of what looks relevant. This is not an arbitrary recipe — it is the solution to an optimization.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Rocchio maximizes the relevant–non-relevant margin">

Among query vectors of fixed norm, the one that maximizes the difference between the average similarity to the relevant set and the average similarity to the non-relevant set,

$$
J(q') = \frac{1}{|D_r|}\sum_{d \in D_r} q'^\top d \;-\; \frac{1}{|D_{nr}|}\sum_{d \in D_{nr}} q'^\top d,
$$

points in the direction of the centroid difference $\bar{d}_r - \bar{d}_{nr}$. Rocchio's $\beta$ and $\gamma$ terms are exactly this direction; the $\alpha\,q$ term anchors the result to the original query.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

Write the objective as a single inner product. Pulling the sums inside,

$$
J(q') = q'^\top\!\left(\frac{1}{|D_r|}\sum_{d \in D_r} d - \frac{1}{|D_{nr}|}\sum_{d \in D_{nr}} d\right) = q'^\top\big(\bar{d}_r - \bar{d}_{nr}\big),
$$

where $\bar{d}_r$ and $\bar{d}_{nr}$ are the two centroids. Maximizing a linear function $q'^\top v$ over the sphere $\lVert q' \rVert = c$ is the textbook Cauchy–Schwarz problem: $q'^\top v \le \lVert q' \rVert\,\lVert v \rVert$ with equality iff $q'$ is parallel to $v$. So the optimal direction is $v = \bar{d}_r - \bar{d}_{nr}$ — the centroid difference. Rocchio realizes this direction with separate non-negative weights $\beta$ on $\bar{d}_r$ and $\gamma$ on $\bar{d}_{nr}$, and adds $\alpha\,q$ so the repaired query does not wander arbitrarily far from what the user actually asked. $\;\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

Because $q'$ now points toward the relevant centroid, its similarity to those documents — and to their unseen near-neighbors in the same vocabulary — rises. The companion notebook confirms that the Rocchio update strictly increases the query's mean cosine to the feedback documents and lifts recall@4 on the worked corpus.

## The relevance model RM1

The language-model analogue, due to Lavrenko and Croft, is more probabilistic. Instead of moving a vector, it estimates a distribution: a **relevance model** $P(w \mid R)$ that we imagine the relevant documents were generated from.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="RM1 relevance model">

Estimate the probability of a word under the relevance model by averaging the document models, weighted by how well each document explains the query:

$$
P(w \mid R) = \sum_{d} P(w \mid d)\, P(d \mid q), \qquad P(d \mid q) \propto P(q \mid d)\,P(d),
$$

where $P(q \mid d)$ is the [query likelihood](/topics/query-likelihood-language-models) and the document prior $P(d)$ is taken uniform over the feedback set.

</TheoremBlock>

The intuition is a sampling story: if we believe the query was drawn from the relevant class, then documents that generate the query well ($P(q \mid d)$ large) are our best proxies for that class, and the words they contain are the words the relevant class favors. Summing each feedback document's model weighted by its query likelihood produces a genuine probability distribution over the vocabulary — the notebook verifies $\sum_w P(w \mid R) = 1$ — whose highest-probability terms are the expansion terms. On the worked corpus with clean feedback, those top terms are exactly *forecast* and *outlook*, each at weight $0.243$: the relevance model has discovered the synonyms the literal query lacked. This is a [point estimate](https://formalstatistics.com/topics/point-estimation) of the expanded query from a small sample of documents, and — as we will see — its bias when that sample is contaminated is precisely query drift.

## RM3: interpolating with the original query

A pure relevance model can wander, so RM3 anchors it to the original query, exactly as Rocchio's $\alpha\,q$ term does.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="RM3">

Interpolate the relevance model with the original (maximum-likelihood) query model and re-score by cross-entropy:

$$
P_{\text{RM3}}(w) = (1 - \alpha)\,P_{\text{ml}}(w \mid q) + \alpha\,P_{\text{RM1}}(w \mid R), \qquad
\text{score}(d) = \sum_w P_{\text{RM3}}(w)\,\log P(w \mid d).
$$

</TheoremBlock>

The re-scoring is the [KL / cross-entropy view](https://formalml.com/topics/kl-divergence) from the query-likelihood topic, with the thin empirical query model swapped for the richer $P_{\text{RM3}}$. The mixing weight $\alpha$ interpolates between two familiar endpoints.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="RM3 limits">

At $\alpha = 0$, $P_{\text{RM3}} = P_{\text{ml}}(\cdot \mid q)$ and the ranking is exactly the original query-likelihood ranking — no expansion. At $\alpha = 1$, $P_{\text{RM3}} = P_{\text{RM1}}$, the pure relevance model. Intermediate $\alpha$ blends the two.

</TheoremBlock>

The proof is immediate from the definition — substitute $\alpha = 0$ and $\alpha = 1$ — but the content is real: $\alpha$ is the dial between trusting the user's words and trusting the feedback. The notebook confirms that at $\alpha = 0$ the RM3 ranking equals the unexpanded query-likelihood ranking (recall $0.5$), while a moderate $\alpha = 0.5$ with a little feedback bridges the vocabulary gap to recall $1.0$.

## Query drift: the honest failure

Everything above assumed the feedback documents are relevant. Pseudo-relevance feedback assumes it *by fiat* — it trusts the top-k of the initial ranking — and that is where it can fail.

<RigorFlag>

Pseudo-relevance feedback is effective on average but carries three honest qualifications:

1. **The relevance assumption is often false.** PRF treats the top-k retrieved documents as relevant. When one is not — a *tax rate* filing surfacing for an interest-*rate* query — its terms enter the relevance model and the query **drifts** toward them. The notebook makes this concrete: recall@4 climbs from $0.5$ to $1.0$ with one or two feedback documents, then falls back toward $0.5$ as the feedback set grows to include off-topic documents whose terms (*budget*, *costs*) crowd out the bridging ones.
2. **No per-query guarantee.** PRF improves *mean* effectiveness across queries while hurting some individual queries outright. There is no theorem that it never lowers recall for a given query — and the drift curve shows it plainly does.
3. **The parameters are tuned.** Rocchio's $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$, RM3's $\alpha$, the number of feedback documents, and the number of expansion terms are all set empirically; the smoothing used in the re-ranking must itself be scaled to document length. None of them are derived from first principles.

The drift is not a bug to be patched away — it is the structural cost of fabricating relevance judgments, and naming it is the point.

</RigorFlag>

## Finance case study

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text"]}>

Query expansion earns its keep in financial retrieval precisely because the domain is full of vocabulary mismatch. An analyst's *rate guidance* must match a filing's *outlook for the rate environment*; a query for *credit deterioration* must reach a disclosure of *rising provisions for loan losses*. Pseudo-relevance feedback over **10-K / 10-Q filings** and **earnings-call transcripts** bridges these gaps by harvesting the domain's own synonyms from the top retrieved documents.

But the finance domain is also where drift bites hardest, because of polysemy: *rate* means an interest rate, a tax rate, a dividend rate, or a growth rate depending on context. A pseudo-relevant document about the *effective tax rate* injects *tax* and *charge* into the relevance model and pulls an interest-rate query toward accounting disclosures. The practical discipline this topic teaches — use few feedback documents, interpolate with the original query (RM3's $\alpha$), and cap the expansion — is exactly the guardrail a production retriever needs.

In the production system, query expansion runs *before* fusion: the expanded lexical query feeds [reciprocal rank fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) alongside dense retrieval, so the dense leg's paraphrase-matching and the expanded lexical leg's synonym-bridging reinforce each other, while RM3's interpolation with the original query keeps the drift bounded.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Implementation

The companion notebook implements both relevance-feedback families from scratch over the worked finance corpus — Rocchio on tf-idf vectors and RM1/RM3 on language models, with the query likelihood $P(q \mid d)$ supplying the feedback weights — and turns each claim into an assertion. It verifies that the RM1 relevance model is a proper distribution, that the Rocchio update equals $\alpha\,q + \beta$ times the feedback centroid and raises the query's mean similarity to it, and that RM3 recovers the unexpanded query-likelihood ranking at $\alpha = 0$.

The two headline numbers are the gain and the drift, measured on the same curve. For the query *rate guidance*, whose four relevant documents include two synonym-only filings, recall@4 starts at $0.50$ with no feedback, rises to $1.00$ once one or two feedback documents inject *forecast* and *outlook* (each weighted $0.243$ in the relevance model), and falls back to $0.50$ as the feedback set grows to four and off-topic terms enter — the query-drift failure, reproduced exactly. The full recall-versus-feedback curve and the expansion terms at each feedback size are printed by the harness so the page, the notebook, and the laboratory read one set of numbers.
