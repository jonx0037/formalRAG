## The label we no longer have

The evaluation layer was built on a fixed answer key. [Set metrics](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr),
[NDCG](/topics/ndcg-discount-geometry), and [significance testing](/topics/significance-testing-calibration)
all measured a retrieval system against a *qrel* — a relevance label fixed in advance — and treated the
resulting number as the **sample mean** of a per-query score, with a standard error, a confidence interval,
and a paired test. Generation breaks that contract. A generated answer has no answer key: there is no fixed
label that says whether "Q3 revenue rose 12% to \$4.2B" is grounded in the retrieved filing. The production
response, formalized by RAGAS, is to **hire a large language model as a judge** and let its verdicts stand in
for the missing labels.

The judge, however, is not an oracle. It is a *measurement instrument*: it answers "is this claim supported?"
with imperfect sensitivity and specificity, it carries systematic biases toward longer or earlier-shown
candidates, and it adds its own noise on top of the noise we already had from sampling queries. Reading a
RAGAS number honestly means treating it as an estimate produced by that instrument, and correcting for the
instrument before believing it.

We introduce the notation once. A generated answer is decomposed into $n$ atomic **claims** $c_1, \dots, c_n$;
each claim has a latent truth $Y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ (is it genuinely supported by the context?) with prevalence
$\pi = \Pr(Y_i = 1)$, and the judge returns a verdict $\hat Y_i \in \{0, 1\}$. The judge's **sensitivity** is
$\mathrm{se} = \Pr(\hat Y_i = 1 \mid Y_i = 1)$ and its **specificity** is
$\mathrm{sp} = \Pr(\hat Y_i = 0 \mid Y_i = 0)$. RAGAS faithfulness is the sample mean of the verdicts,
$\hat F = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i \hat Y_i$, with observed positive rate $p_{\mathrm{obs}} = \Pr(\hat Y_i = 1)$.

> An LLM judge is a measurement instrument with its own bias and variance; every RAGAS metric is an estimator
> built from its verdicts, and reading it correctly means **correcting for the instrument** — debiasing the
> verdict, agreeing on the protocol, calibrating the confidence, and pricing the noise floor.

## The judge as a noisy instrument

Faithfulness inherits everything the prerequisite said about a metric — it is a mean of per-item scores — with
one change: the scores are now noisy verdicts rather than fixed labels.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Faithfulness as a sample mean of judge verdicts">
For an answer decomposed into $n$ claims with judge verdicts $\hat Y_i = \mathbb{1}[\text{the judge calls } c_i
\text{ supported}]$, the RAGAS faithfulness is the sample mean
$$
\hat F \;=\; \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\hat Y_i,
$$
an estimate of the latent grounding prevalence $\pi = \Pr(Y_i = 1)$ — the fraction of claims that are *truly*
supported.
</TheoremBlock>

Because the judge is imperfect, that mean does not estimate $\pi$; it estimates something shifted away from it.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Faithfulness is a biased estimator of prevalence">
The expected verdict relates to the latent prevalence through the judge's error rates,
$$
p_{\mathrm{obs}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}[\hat Y] \;=\; \mathrm{se}\cdot\pi \;+\; (1-\mathrm{sp})(1-\pi),
$$
so $\mathbb{E}[\hat F] = p_{\mathrm{obs}} \ne \pi$ unless $\mathrm{se} = \mathrm{sp} = 1$, with bias
$\mathbb{E}[\hat F] - \pi = (\mathrm{se} - 1)\pi + (1 - \mathrm{sp})(1-\pi)$.

*Proof.* Condition a single verdict on the latent truth:
$\Pr(\hat Y = 1) = \Pr(\hat Y = 1 \mid Y = 1)\Pr(Y = 1) + \Pr(\hat Y = 1 \mid Y = 0)\Pr(Y = 0) =
\mathrm{se}\,\pi + (1-\mathrm{sp})(1-\pi)$, the law of total probability. Linearity of expectation over the $n$
claims gives $\mathbb{E}[\hat F] = p_{\mathrm{obs}}$; subtracting $\pi$ and collecting terms gives the bias. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

A lenient judge — one that over-endorses, with high sensitivity but mediocre specificity — inflates faithfulness.
On the corpus, such a judge reports faithfulness $p_{\mathrm{obs}} = 0.703$ for the dense leg whose true grounded
fraction is $\pi = 0.648$. The fix is to invert the error model.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="The Rogan–Gladen corrected estimator">
Solving the prevalence identity for $\pi$ gives the **Rogan–Gladen** estimator
$$
\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}} \;=\; \frac{p_{\mathrm{obs}} + \mathrm{sp} - 1}{\mathrm{se} + \mathrm{sp} - 1},
$$
defined when the **Youden index** $J = \mathrm{se} + \mathrm{sp} - 1 > 0$ — when the judge is better than random.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Rogan–Gladen unbiasedness and its variance">
With known $\mathrm{se}, \mathrm{sp}$ and $J = \mathrm{se} + \mathrm{sp} - 1 > 0$, for a judge whose error rates
are constant across claims:
(a) $\mathbb{E}[\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}}] = \pi$ — the estimator is unbiased; and
(b) since $p_{\mathrm{obs}}$ is a mean of $n$ Bernoulli verdicts,
$$
\operatorname{Var}(\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}}) \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Var}(p_{\mathrm{obs}})}{J^2}
\;=\; \frac{p_{\mathrm{obs}}(1 - p_{\mathrm{obs}})}{n\,J^2},
$$
inflated relative to the raw verdict variance by $1/J^2$, which diverges as $J \to 0^+$: **a useless judge
cannot be debiased.**

*Proof.* The estimator is an affine function of $p_{\mathrm{obs}}$, namely $\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}} = g(p_{\mathrm{obs}})$
with $g(p) = (p + \mathrm{sp} - 1)/J$. By linearity,
$\mathbb{E}[\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}}] = (\mathbb{E}[p_{\mathrm{obs}}] + \mathrm{sp} - 1)/J =
(\mathrm{se}\,\pi + (1-\mathrm{sp})(1-\pi) + \mathrm{sp} - 1)/J$, and the numerator simplifies to
$\pi(\mathrm{se} + \mathrm{sp} - 1) = \pi J$, giving $\pi$ exactly — no approximation, because $g$ is affine.
For the variance, $g'(p) = 1/J$, so $\operatorname{Var}(g(p_{\mathrm{obs}})) = (g')^2 \operatorname{Var}(p_{\mathrm{obs}}) =
\operatorname{Var}(p_{\mathrm{obs}})/J^2$; substituting the binomial variance $p_{\mathrm{obs}}(1-p_{\mathrm{obs}})/n$
completes it. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

On the dense leg, the audited correction recovers $\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}} = 0.648$ — exactly the oracle prevalence —
at the cost of a variance inflated $2.6\times$. Panel A drives this with sensitivity and specificity sliders: the
truth $\pi$, the inflated observation $p_{\mathrm{obs}}$, and the corrected $\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}}$ sit on one
axis, and the $1/J^2$ curve climbs without bound as the sliders approach a coin flip.

<RigorFlag>
The unbiasedness is exact only for a *homogeneous* judge with *known* error rates, and only before the estimate
is clipped into $[0,1]$ — the raw $\hat\pi_{\mathrm{RG}}$ can fall outside the unit interval when
$p_{\mathrm{obs}} < 1 - \mathrm{sp}$ or $> \mathrm{se}$, and clipping reintroduces bias near the boundary. In
deployment $\mathrm{se}$ and $\mathrm{sp}$ are themselves estimated from a labeled audit set, adding two more
variance terms; and below the Youden line ($J \le 0$) the correction is simply undefined. A near-random judge is
not debiasable — its noise is amplified, not removed.
</RigorFlag>

The consequence is a decision, not just a number. Two answer-generation systems judged by the *same* lenient
judge can be **ranked in the wrong order** by raw faithfulness: a system whose documents the judge over-endorses
(low effective specificity) is inflated above a genuinely better one. Panel A's lower chart shows the constructed
case — raw faithfulness favors system A ($0.80$ over $0.74$), but Rogan–Gladen with each system's audited rates
restores the truth, favoring system B ($0.79$ over $0.70$), matching the oracle. The instrument was masking the
ranking; correcting for it flips the verdict back.

## Agreement is not accuracy: chance-corrected reliability and the variance floor

Before trusting a judge against the truth, we ask a weaker question we can actually check in production: does the
judge **agree** with a second rater — another model, or a human auditor on a sample? Raw agreement is the wrong
summary, because two raters agree by chance even when they share no signal.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Observed agreement, chance agreement, and Cohen's kappa">
For two raters on $N$ items forming a $2\times 2$ table, the **observed agreement** $p_o$ is the fraction of items
on which they agree, and the **chance agreement** $p_e = \sum_{k} \Pr_A(k)\Pr_B(k)$ is the sum over categories of
the product of the two raters' marginals. **Cohen's kappa** is
$$
\kappa \;=\; \frac{p_o - p_e}{1 - p_e}.
$$
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Kappa is the fraction of beyond-chance agreement achieved">
$\kappa = 1$ iff $p_o = 1$ (perfect agreement); $\kappa = 0$ iff $p_o = p_e$ (agreement at chance); $\kappa < 0$
iff the raters agree *less* than chance.

*Proof.* The map $p_o \mapsto (p_o - p_e)/(1 - p_e)$ is the unique affine map sending the chance level $p_e$ to $0$
and perfect agreement $1$ to $1$, so it rescales $p_o$ from the achievable-beyond-chance interval $[p_e, 1]$ onto
$[0,1]$. At $p_o = 1$ numerator and denominator both equal $1 - p_e$; at $p_o = p_e$ the numerator vanishes; for
$p_o < p_e$ the numerator is negative while the denominator stays positive. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The trouble is that $p_e$ depends on the marginals, and in a setting where almost every claim is supported, the
marginals are extreme and $p_e$ swells toward $1$.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The kappa paradox (Feinstein–Cicchetti)">
At a fixed observed agreement $p_o$, as the prevalence of one category approaches $0$ or $1$ the chance agreement
$p_e \to 1$ and $\kappa \to$ an indeterminate form that collapses toward $0$. Two tables with the **same** $p_o$
can therefore carry very different $\kappa$.

*Proof by example (RUN in the notebook).* Two $2\times2$ tables each have observed agreement $p_o = 0.85$. The
balanced table $(40, 10, 5, 45)$ has $p_e = 0.50$ and $\kappa = 0.70$; the skewed table $(80, 10, 5, 5)$ has
$p_e = 0.78$ and $\kappa = 0.32$. Identical agreement, kappa differing by $0.38$ — one "substantial," one "fair." $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The remedy is a chance term that does not balloon with skew.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Prevalence-robust coefficients: Gwet's AC1 and Krippendorff's alpha">
**Gwet's AC1** replaces the chance term with $p_e^{\gamma} = 2\,q(1-q)$, where $q$ is the overall propensity of
the positive category (the binary special case of $\frac{1}{Q-1}\sum_k \pi_k(1-\pi_k)$), giving
$\mathrm{AC1} = (p_o - p_e^{\gamma})/(1 - p_e^{\gamma})$, which is stable as the marginals skew.
**Krippendorff's alpha** $= 1 - D_o/D_e$, the ratio of observed to expected disagreement, generalizes the same
chance-correction to any number of raters, missing data, and any measurement level.
</TheoremBlock>

On the same two tables, Gwet's AC1 reads $0.70$ and $0.81$ — it does not collapse where kappa does. Panel B sweeps
the prevalence at fixed agreement and overlays the two curves: kappa plunges toward zero at the extremes while AC1
stays high. The lesson is not that AC1 is "correct" — none of the three is canonical — but that **kappa must never
be read without its marginals.**

Agreement also exposes a second, structural fact: the judge is a *source of variance* that more queries cannot
average away. Model each per-query faithfulness as $X_{q,j} = \mu + a_q + b_j + \varepsilon_{q,j}$ with a query
effect $a_q$, a judge effect $b_j$, and residual noise.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="The variance decomposition and the judge-variance floor">
The total variance splits into independent components, $\sigma^2 = \sigma_q^2 + \sigma_j^2 + \sigma_e^2$, and the
**intraclass correlation** $\mathrm{ICC} = \sigma_q^2 / (\sigma_q^2 + \sigma_j^2 + \sigma_e^2)$ is the fraction of
variance explained by true between-query differences. The standard error of a faithfulness averaged over $Q$
queries and $J$ judges is
$$
\operatorname{SE}^2 \;=\; \frac{\sigma_q^2}{Q} \;+\; \frac{\sigma_j^2}{J} \;+\; \frac{\sigma_e^2}{Q\,J}.
$$
As $Q \to \infty$ at fixed $J$, $\operatorname{SE}^2 \to \sigma_j^2/J$ — a positive **floor** that only more judges,
never more queries, can lower.

*Proof.* Write $\bar X = \mu + \frac{1}{Q}\sum_q a_q + \frac{1}{J}\sum_j b_j + \frac{1}{QJ}\sum_{q,j}\varepsilon_{q,j}$.
By independence of the effects, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar X) = \sigma_q^2/Q + \sigma_j^2/J + \sigma_e^2/(QJ)$; the
first and third terms vanish as $Q \to \infty$, leaving $\sigma_j^2/J$. The ICC identity follows from
$\operatorname{Var}(X_{q,j}) = \sigma_q^2 + \sigma_j^2 + \sigma_e^2$. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

On the five-judge panel the components are $\sigma_q^2 = 0.0102$, $\sigma_j^2 = 0.0048$, $\sigma_e^2 = 0.0134$,
giving $\mathrm{ICC}(2,1) = 0.36$. Panel D plots the total standard error descending toward the judge floor as
queries grow, and prices the lever: at a fixed budget of $200$ judge-calls, **five judges over forty queries**
($\operatorname{SE} = 0.036$) beat **one judge over two hundred queries** ($\operatorname{SE} = 0.070$). You cannot
buy confidence in a faithfulness score with queries alone — the judge noise is structural.

## Judge bias, calibration, and learning the error rates

Two threads remain. First, the systematic biases the judge brings — and which of them we can remove. Second, the
judge's stated *confidence*: is it a probability?

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={5} title="The paired swap test for position bias">
Present each claim to the judge once in the **first** slot and once in the **last** slot, holding its content
fixed, and form the paired difference of the judge's confidence, $\delta = \hat c_{\text{first}} - \hat c_{\text{last}}$.
Under no position bias, presentation order is exchangeable and $\mathbb{E}[\delta] = 0$; the position-bias estimate
is $\bar\delta$, tested by the prerequisite's paired and permutation tests.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="A symmetric position effect is removable; self-preference is not">
If the judge's verdict carries a slot effect $\beta$ symmetric across the two orders, then averaging the two
presentations cancels $\beta$ and recovers an unbiased preference. A self-preference effect that correlates with a
candidate's *provenance* rather than its slot survives order-averaging and is unidentifiable without external
ground truth.

*Proof.* For the removable part, $\frac{1}{2}(\hat c_{\text{first}} + \hat c_{\text{last}})$ adds $+\beta$ and
$-\beta$ in the two slots, which cancel, leaving the content term plus mean noise. The provenance term is constant
across slots, so it does not cancel under order-averaging. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

On the corpus the lenient judge's swap test returns a bias of $0.070$ at $p \approx 10^{-105}$ — a real, removable
position effect — while a bias-free control judge returns bias $0$ at $p = 1.0$. The swap test detects and removes
the slot effect; it cannot touch self-preference.

The judge does not only vote; it states a **confidence**. We feed those confidences straight into the
prerequisite's calibration suite.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={6} title="Judge-confidence calibration">
Pooling the judge's stated confidence and the binary truth across all $(q, \text{claim})$ pairs, the **reliability
diagram** plots empirical relevance against confidence per bin (the diagonal is perfect calibration), the
**expected calibration error** $\mathrm{ECE} = \sum_b \frac{n_b}{N}\,|\mathrm{acc}_b - \mathrm{conf}_b|$ is the area
off the diagonal, and Platt scaling and isotonic regression recalibrate it.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={4} title="Monotone recalibration preserves ranking (reused)">
A strictly increasing recalibration map leaves every order-based decision of the judge unchanged, so the AUC and
every ranking metric are invariant under it; only the probability reading changes. This is exactly the
monotone-recalibration theorem of the [calibration prerequisite](/topics/significance-testing-calibration),
applied to judge confidence — the proof is there.
</TheoremBlock>

The judge confidence ranks claims almost perfectly (AUC $\approx 0.998$ for the lenient judge) yet is wildly
**over-confident** as a probability (ECE $0.154$): Panel C shows the raw dots bowed far off the diagonal. Platt
scaling pulls the ECE to $0.003$ and isotonic to $0$, both leaving the AUC untouched at $0.998$ — calibration is
orthogonal to ranking, the same reason [rank fusion](/topics/rank-fusion-rrf) fuses ranks rather than scores. A
pipeline that thresholds or averages a judge's raw confidences inherits the miscalibration; one that ranks by them
does not.

### Learning the error rates with no gold set

The Rogan–Gladen correction needed the judge's sensitivity and specificity. In production there is often no gold
label to estimate them from — only the judges' verdicts. The latent-class model recovers them anyway.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={7} title="The Dawid–Skene latent-class model">
With latent class $z_i \in \{0,1\}$ per item, class prior $\pi_k$, and each judge $j$'s confusion matrix
$\theta^{(j)}_{kl} = \Pr(\text{judge } j \text{ says } l \mid z_i = k)$, the EM algorithm alternates an E-step —
the posterior $\Pr(z_i = k \mid \text{verdicts}) \propto \pi_k \prod_j \theta^{(j)}_{k, r_{ij}}$ — with a
closed-form M-step re-estimating $\pi_k$ and each $\theta^{(j)}$. It recovers each judge's sensitivity
$\theta^{(j)}_{11}$ and specificity $\theta^{(j)}_{00}$ from the **agreement structure alone**.
</TheoremBlock>

Panel E runs it: with no gold labels, EM recovers every planted per-judge sensitivity and specificity within
$\approx 0.06$ and labels items more accurately than majority vote ($0.975$ versus $0.972$). This closes the loop —
the error rates the instrument correction assumed are exactly what EM supplies. The result is identifiable only up
to a label permutation (we anchor to the majority vote), and the likelihood is non-convex, so EM finds a local
optimum from a sensible start; correlated judges, who violate the conditional-independence assumption, break the
guarantee. It is a powerful tool, not a free lunch.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="Auditing generated answers over financial filings">
A retrieval-augmented system answers an analyst's question about a 10-K with "Q3 revenue rose 12% to \$4.2B." An
LLM judge decomposes the answer into atomic claims and checks each against the retrieved filing context — exactly
the RAGAS faithfulness pipeline. A hallucinated figure the judge fails to flag is a **false negative with
regulatory and trading consequences**, so the quantity that matters is the judge's *specificity on numeric
claims*, and the high-confidence bin of its reliability diagram is precisely where a confident-but-wrong
"supported" verdict reaches the desk.

Every correction in this topic is load-bearing here. A raw faithfulness of $0.92$ from a judge with sensitivity
$0.85$ and specificity $0.80$ overstates grounding; the Rogan–Gladen number is the honest figure to report to
risk. Before trusting the judge at all, the desk audits it against a human reviewer on a labeled sample — and
because "most claims are supported" makes the marginals extreme, kappa misleads and Gwet's AC1 is the coefficient
to report. And a high-stakes sign-off cannot be bought with more evaluation queries: the judge-variance floor means
a confident verdict needs multiple judges or a sharper protocol. The corpus here is a synthetic claim-support
oracle standing in for those filings; every value illustrates the mechanism, not a benchmark.
</FinanceCaseStudy>

The thread that ran through the evaluation layer — a metric is an estimator — now reaches the generation layer with
a second source of noise stacked on the first. Faithfulness and groundedness will give that support a semantic
definition, and conformal factuality will turn a calibrated, debiased judge confidence into a distribution-free
correctness guarantee. Both begin from the instrument this topic learned to read.
