## From a fixed pool to a growing one

The previous topic ended on a sentence that pointed past itself. Having spent its length showing how to
choose the best $k$ passages from a *fixed* candidate pool — coverage over relevance, the greedy choice
provably within $1 - 1/e$ of optimal — it closed: "when one passage's answer raises a new question,
selection becomes a *search* over an evolving evidence space, which is where multi-hop and iterative
retrieval take over."

This is that search. Every topic in this layer so far has assumed one shot: retrieve a pool once, choose a
subset, answer. That assumption holds for a question whose evidence sits in one place. It breaks for a
*compositional* question — one whose answer is defined through an intermediate entity the query never names.
Ask for *the revenue of the company that acquired Company A's primary supplier*, and no single retrieval can
return the answer: the document that reports that revenue is a filing of some company $C$ that the query,
which only mentions $A$, has no reason to be near. You must first find $A$'s filing to learn the supplier
$B$, then find who acquired $B$, then retrieve $C$'s revenue. Retrieval becomes a loop — retrieve, read,
reformulate the query from what you read, retrieve again — and the object of study is the loop, not the
single step.

We will make four claims precise, on the same finance geometry the rest of the arc used. First, that the
compositional answer is genuinely *unreachable* in one hop — a statement about the embedding sphere.
Second, that the loop is a search over an evidence space, each hop the previous topic's retrieve-and-select
step. Third, that chaining is expensive: end-to-end recall multiplies across hops, so it decays. Fourth —
the climax — that greedy single-shot selection *provably cannot* shortcut the path, because the bridge and
the answer form a supermodular synergy, the exact failure mode of the diminishing-returns machinery the last
topic relied on.

## The compositional gap: an answer one hop cannot reach

Fix the geometry. Each company has a unit direction $u_X$ on the sphere; a *filing* of company $X$ that
mentions company $Y$ is a document whose embedding leans mostly toward $X$ but carries a component toward
$Y$ — the mention. We model it as

$$
f_{X\to Y} \;=\; \cos\alpha \, u_X + \sin\alpha\, u_Y, \qquad \alpha \in (0, \tfrac{\pi}{2}),
$$

so a query $q \approx u_X$ retrieves the filing ($\langle q, f_{X\to Y}\rangle \approx \cos\alpha$, large)
while the mentioned company $Y$, if it sits in a different sector, is near-orthogonal to $q$
($\langle q, u_Y\rangle \approx 0$). The terminal *answer* filing of the last company is just its own
direction. This is the whole mechanism, and it lets us define the structure the question lives on.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The retrieval graph and graph distance">

Let $V$ be the documents. Fix an edge threshold $\tau \in (0,1)$ and a reformulation map
$\rho(\cdot)$ that turns a read document into the next query. The **retrieval graph** is the directed graph
on $V$ with an edge $d_i \to d_j$ whenever $\langle \rho(d_i), d_j\rangle > \tau$ — "$d_j$ is retrievable
from a query formed by reading $d_i$." A query $q$ is a source: it reaches $\{d : \langle q, d\rangle > \tau\}$
in one step. The **graph distance** $d_G(q, d)$ of a document is the length of the shortest directed path
from the query's reachable set to $d$.

</TheoremBlock>

A single-hop retriever returns the source's one-step neighborhood. So a question is answerable in one hop
exactly when its answer document sits at graph distance one. The compositional question is engineered so it
does not.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The compositional gap">

Take a two-hop chain $q \approx u_A$, a bridge filing $f_{A\to B} = \cos\alpha\,u_A + \sin\alpha\,u_B$, and
an answer filing $\approx u_B$, with $A$ and $B$ in distinct sectors so $\langle u_A, u_B\rangle \approx 0$.
If $\cos\alpha > \tau > \langle u_A, u_B\rangle$, then:

1. the **bridge is reachable** from the query, $\langle q, f_{A\to B}\rangle \approx \cos\alpha > \tau$;
2. the **answer is not**, $\langle q, u_B\rangle \approx \langle u_A, u_B\rangle < \tau$ — it sits at graph
   distance two;
3. the **reformulated query reaches it**: the operator
   $\rho(d) = \dfrac{d - \langle d, q\rangle\, q}{\lVert d - \langle d, q\rangle\, q\rVert}$ applied to the
   bridge returns $\rho(f_{A\to B}) \approx u_B$, since removing the $u_A$ component of the bridge leaves the
   $\sin\alpha\,u_B$ mention; then $\langle \rho(f_{A\to B}), u_B\rangle \approx 1 > \tau$.

Consequently single-hop recall of the answer is $\approx 0$ and two-hop recall is $\approx 1$.

</TheoremBlock>

The reformulation operator is the heart of the construction and worth a sentence on its own. It is the
projection of the read document onto the subspace orthogonal to the current query — *the new direction the
filing adds, the entity it names that the query did not.* Reading $A$'s filing and subtracting what the
query already knew about $A$ leaves exactly the pointer to $B$. That is what converts a document into the
next query, and it is why the answer, invisible from $q$, becomes reachable after one step. The lab's first
panel is this proposition: on the "cosine to the current query" axis the bridge sits above $\tau$ and the
answer below it, until reformulation snaps the answer's cosine to $\approx 1$.

<MultiHopLaboratory client:visible />

On the constructed corpus the gap is total: averaged over the compositional chains, single-hop answer recall
is $0.00$ and multi-hop is $1.00$, while the one-hop control questions — whose answer *is* the query
company's own filing — are reachable in a single hop, recall $1.00$. The point is not the precise decimals,
which are exact for this synthetic answer model; it is that the gap is a property of the *geometry*, not of
any decoder. No amount of reading more of the wrong neighborhood reaches a document that is near-orthogonal
to the query.

## Multi-hop as search over an evidence space

Once the answer is a path rather than a point, retrieval is a sequential decision problem. We give it the
standard furniture.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="The evidence-space search problem">

A **state** is a belief $b \in \Delta(\mathcal{A})$ over the answer space — the posterior $p(a \mid q, D)$
given the documents $D$ read so far. An **action** is a reformulated query $q'$. The **transition** is the
previous topic's retrieve-and-select step: from the $\tau$-reachable pool of $q'$, select a context $S$ by
greedily maximizing the information gain $f_{\mathrm{IG}}(S) = H(b) - H(b \mid S)$ — the same monotone-greedy
maximizer context selection certified — read $S$, and update the belief. The **reward** of a hop is the
information it adds. A **trajectory** is a path through the retrieval graph; the **greedy policy** takes, at
each state, the action of maximal expected marginal information.

</TheoremBlock>

Two things deserve emphasis. The per-hop operator is *literally* the prerequisite's: retrieve a pool, then
select the covering subset under a budget, with greedy on a submodular oracle. Multi-hop does not replace
selection; it iterates it, feeding each step's reading into the next step's query. And the reward is
information, which ties the search to the bits the PMI and noisy-channel topics counted. What multi-hop adds
is the *control* problem on top — which query to issue next, and when to stop.

The optimal control problem has a clean form and an unfortunate cost.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="The optimal policy is a Bellman fixed point — and intractable">

The value of a belief under the optimal policy satisfies the Bellman recursion

$$
V(b) \;=\; \max_{a}\Big[\, r(b, a) + \mathbb{E}_{b' \sim T(b, a)}\, V(b') \,\Big],
$$

the fixed point of a contraction, reachable in principle by value iteration. But the state space is the
simplex over answers and the action space is every reformulation, so computing $V$ is intractable. Every
practical multi-hop method — the greedy max-information hop we analyze, and every system in the literature —
is a heuristic approximation of this policy, not the policy itself. We will see in the last section that the
greedy heuristic has no approximation guarantee here, for a reason specific to compositional structure.

</TheoremBlock>

## Compounding recall: why hops decay geometrically

A path is only as strong as its weakest link, and "strong" here is recall. If hop $i$ retrieves its target
with probability $r_i$, and the hops were independent, the whole chain succeeds with the product.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The chain recall law and its over-fetch">

For a $k$-hop chain with per-hop recalls $r_1, \dots, r_k$ and independent hops, the end-to-end recall is

$$
R_{\mathrm{chain}} \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^{k} r_i .
$$

To leave an end-to-end target $\rho$, each hop must reach $r_i = \rho^{1/k}$, which rises toward $1$ as $k$
grows; equivalently the front end of each hop must **over-retrieve** by $1/\prod_i r_i$ — one
negative-binomial scan law on the composite retention, exactly as the capstone composed cascade stages, now
composed over hops.

</TheoremBlock>

The consequences are stark and they are the lab's second panel. With a modest per-hop recall of $0.7$, a
three-hop chain succeeds barely a third of the time ($0.7^3 \approx 0.34$); to hold $\rho = 0.9$ end to end
over three hops, each hop must individually reach $0.9^{1/3} \approx 0.965$. Multi-hop is not free
reach — it is reach bought at a geometric price, which is why a system that hops carelessly is worse than one
that does not hop at all.

The independence assumption is, as ever, the soft spot — and as ever, it errs in the safe direction.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Positive dependence makes the product a lower bound (FKG)">

Hops share corpus geometry: a query that is hard at one hop tends to be hard at the next, so the per-hop
success events are positively associated. By the FKG/Harris inequality, the realized chain recall is at
least the product of the marginal recalls,

$$
\Pr\!\Big[\textstyle\bigcap_i \{\text{hop } i \text{ succeeds}\}\Big] \;\ge\; \prod_i r_i .
$$

The independent product is therefore a conservative lower bound; provisioning the over-fetch to $1/\prod_i r_i$
is safe, not exact.

</TheoremBlock>

We verify the direction rather than assume it, on the same bivariate-survival copula the capstone used: as
the hop dependence $\rho$ runs from negative to positive, the gap between realized and independent chain
recall runs from negative through zero to positive — the FKG sign, confirmed across both signs before being
written down. (The measured per-hop recalls on this clean corpus are $\approx 1$, which leaves no room for
the product law or FKG to bite, so the panel uses illustrative middling retentions — a high-retention chain
makes the law vacuous, the same caveat the capstone flagged.)

## When to stop: marginal information versus cost

A search needs a stopping rule. The information-theoretic instinct is right: stop when the next hop's
expected marginal information falls below its cost. The subtlety is *which* signal measures that
information — and the answer is not the one a naive reading suggests.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="The marginal information of a hop">

The information a hop adds is the entropy it removes from the answer belief,
$\Delta I_t = H(A \mid Q, D_{1:t-1}) - H(A \mid Q, D_{1:t})$; by the chain rule of mutual information these
telescope to the total $I(A; D_{1:k} \mid Q)$. Operationally, the *new direction* a read filing opens is its
reformulation residual $\lVert d - \langle d, q\rangle q\rVert$ — the magnitude of the entity it names that
the query did not. When the residual collapses, the filing names no one new and the next hop can only
re-retrieve known evidence, so its expected marginal information is $\approx 0$.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={1} title="Adaptive iterative retrieval with an information stopping rule">

Initialize $q \leftarrow q_0$ and the belief $b_0$. Repeat: rank the corpus by
$\langle q, \cdot\rangle$; read the top unread document $d$; update the belief; if the residual
$\lVert d - \langle d, q\rangle q\rVert < \varepsilon$ — the filing names no new entity — **stop** and
return the belief; otherwise set $q \leftarrow \rho(d)$ and continue. This is a heuristic, an SPRT /
optimal-stopping analogue, not the Bellman-optimal policy.

</TheoremBlock>

The rule terminates correctly: through every bridge hop the residual clears the threshold (a new entity to
chase), and at the terminal answer filing it collapses (it names no one), so the realized hop count equals
the chain depth — one, two, three hops for one-, two-, three-hop questions. The lab's third panel shows this
on a worked three-hop trajectory: two bridge residuals well above the threshold, then a collapse, and the
loop halts.

The panel also exposes a trap, and it is the bridge to the climax. The obvious stopping signal would be the
*belief movement* — stop when reading another document no longer changes your answer distribution. On a
compositional chain that signal is exactly backwards. The bridge hop barely moves the belief: reading $A$'s
filing only confirms what the query already implied about $A$, a KL movement near $0.08$. The decisive shift
comes *last*, at the answer hop, with a KL movement above $9$ — the belief snaps from $\approx 0$ mass on
the truth to $\approx 1$. A myopic "stop when the belief stops moving" rule would halt at the
worthless-looking bridge and never reach the answer. The decisive evidence arrives late, which is the
operational face of the structure we turn to now.

## Why greedy cannot shortcut: the supermodular synergy

The prerequisite leaned on submodularity — diminishing returns — to earn its $1 - 1/e$ guarantee, and
flagged the one objective that breaks it: information gain. Multi-hop is where that break becomes the whole
story.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Compositional information is supermodular">

For a compositional pair — a bridge document $d_{\mathrm{br}}$ and an answer document $d_{\mathrm{ans}}$
whose joint identifies the answer while neither does alone — the marginal information of the answer document
*increases* with conditioning:

$$
\Delta\big(d_{\mathrm{ans}} \mid \varnothing\big) \;=\; 0
\;<\; 1 \;=\; \Delta\big(d_{\mathrm{ans}} \mid \{d_{\mathrm{br}}\}\big).
$$

This is the XOR witness: with $A = D_{\mathrm{br}} \oplus D_{\mathrm{ans}}$, each observation alone carries
zero information about $A$ while the two together carry one bit. A submodular gain would have the marginal
*decrease* with conditioning; here it does the opposite — information gain is **supermodular** on the
compositional pair, and so not submodular, the same witness context selection used to disqualify it as a
greedy objective.

</TheoremBlock>

Submodularity was the hypothesis the Nemhauser–Wolsey–Fisher theorem needed. Without it, greedy carries no
$1 - 1/e$ guarantee — and worse, on this structure greedy single-shot selection cannot reach the answer at
all.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="Multi-hop is the synergy-exploiting move">

Because the answer document's marginal value is zero until the bridge is read, a single-shot selector
optimizing a one-pass objective will never buy it — and in fact cannot: the answer is below the edge
threshold from the query, so it is not even in the one-hop reachable pool. Only by reading the bridge and
*reformulating from its content* does the answer enter a reachable pool. Multi-hop is precisely the move that
converts the bridge's named entity into a query that reaches the answer, harvesting a synergy no single-shot
selection can.

</TheoremBlock>

The lab's fourth panel is this corollary as two bars and a toggle: the answer document alone contributes
zero bits, given the bridge it contributes one — the marginal arrow points *up* — and the answer is in the
single-hop reachable pool with frequency $0.00$, in the reformulated pool with frequency $1.00$. The
supermodular synergy is not a curiosity at the edge of the theory; it is the reason multi-hop retrieval has
to exist.

<RigorFlag>

Five honesty notes. First, greedy hopping has no $(1-1/e)$ guarantee: the per-hop objective is information
gain, which is supermodular on the compositional pair, exactly the hypothesis the theorem rules out — so the
prerequisite's machinery does not transfer across hops. Second, the optimal policy is the fixed point of a
Bellman recursion over the belief simplex and is intractable; the greedy max-information hop, and every cited
system, is a heuristic. Third, the compounding-recall product holds under hop independence; positive
dependence makes it a conservative lower bound (FKG/Harris), verified across both signs of a copula, so the
over-retrieve provisioning is the safe direction, not an equality. Fourth, the stopping rule — stop when the
read filing opens no new direction — is an optimal-stopping/SPRT analogue, not a derived optimum on this
corpus; the threshold is tuned and the realized hop count is seed-dependent. Fifth, the answer model is a
synthetic von Mises–Fisher softmax, exact for the model and illustrative of a real retriever, on one
constructed corpus engineered to have a near-orthogonal answer reachable through a bridge — it demonstrates
the mechanism, it does not claim every multi-hop question has this geometry.

</RigorFlag>

## What multi-hop retrieval trades, in one line

Multi-hop turns selection into search over an evolving evidence space: each hop is the certified
retrieve-and-select step, the reformulation operator extracts the new entity a filing names, and the answer
to a compositional question is a path, not a point. Recall compounds geometrically, so each hop must
over-retrieve; the loop stops when a filing opens no new direction; and greedy earns no $1 - 1/e$ here
because the compositional case is supermodular — which is the very reason iterative reformulation, not a
bigger single-shot selection, is what reaches the answer.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="The revenue of the company that acquired Company A's supplier" modality={["text", "pdf", "news"]}>

A supply-chain question is rarely answerable from one filing. Ask for the revenue of the company that
acquired Company A's primary chip supplier, and the query mentions only $A$. Hop one retrieves $A$'s 10-K —
the bridge — which discloses the supplier $B$; the reformulation strips away what the query already knew
about $A$ and leaves the pointer to $B$. Hop two, querying for $B$, retrieves the acquisition news and the
acquirer $C$; hop three retrieves $C$'s revenue, the answer. No single retrieval could have surfaced $C$'s
filing: it never mentions $A$, and on the embedding sphere it sits near-orthogonal to the original query.
The discipline is to read the filing that *names the next entity*, reformulate, and retrieve again — paying
the geometric recall cost and stopping once a hop names no one new. Once iterative retrieval has traversed
these edges enough times, the relation "retrievable-from" has drawn a graph over the whole corpus, and the
question shifts from finding a single path to understanding that graph's global structure — its dense
communities of mutually-retrievable filings. That global community structure is the object of GraphRAG and
community detection, the next step in this arc.

</FinanceCaseStudy>
