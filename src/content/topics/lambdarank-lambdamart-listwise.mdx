## Overview & motivation

The [previous topic](/topics/learning-to-rank-pairwise) learned a ranking with **RankNet**: model each
preference $i \succ j$ as a Bernoulli trial $P(i \succ j) = \sigma(s_i - s_j)$, minimize the pairwise
cross-entropy, and read off the gradient's structure — it factorizes into a per-document **lambda force**
$\lambda_i = \sum_j \lambda_{ij}$, the net pull each document feels from its preference pairs. That
factorization is the bridge we cross now. RankNet's force has one blind spot, and it is fatal for ranking
quality: it is **position-blind**. The force on a pair depends only on the score gap $s_i - s_j$, not on
*where in the list* the two documents sit. A swap at ranks 1 and 2 — which a user sees immediately —
contributes exactly as much loss as a swap at ranks 99 and 100, which no one ever reaches.

This topic removes that blindness three ways, and is honest about what each one *is*. **LambdaRank**
keeps the force structure but scales each $\lambda_{ij}$ by the change in NDCG that swapping the pair
would cause — a fix that works empirically but, we will prove, optimizes no actual loss. **LambdaMART**
boosts those lambdas into regression trees, buying a nonlinear scorer. And **listwise** objectives
abandon the pairwise heuristic for a proper probabilistic loss over the whole permutation. The arc is
pairwise (a loss, position-blind) $\to$ LambdaRank (position-aware, *not* a loss) $\to$ listwise (a
convex loss).

## From RankNet to LambdaRank: the ΔNDCG reweight

LambdaRank's idea is a single multiplication. Keep RankNet's pairwise force, but scale it by how much
swapping that pair would move the metric we actually care about.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="The LambdaRank force">
For an ordered preference pair $(i, j)$ with $g_i > g_j$, LambdaRank sets
$$
\lambda_{ij} \;=\; -\sigma\!\big(-(s_i - s_j)\big)\,\big|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}\big|,
\qquad
\lambda_i \;=\; \sum_{j} \lambda_{ij},
$$
where $|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}|$ is the absolute change in NDCG that would result from swapping the
ranks of documents $i$ and $j$ under the current scores, holding all other documents fixed. Setting every
$|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}| \equiv 1$ recovers RankNet's force exactly — LambdaRank is a *reweighting* of
RankNet, not a new object.
</TheoremBlock>

That last sentence is a load-bearing anchor: the companion notebook asserts that the LambdaRank force
with uniform weights equals the imported `lambda_forces` from the predecessor to machine precision. The
only new ingredient is the weight, and it has a closed form.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The swap sensitivity ΔNDCG has a closed form">
Let documents occupy 1-indexed ranks $p$ and $q$ under the current scores, with gains $G(g_p), G(g_q)$
and discounts $D(p), D(q)$ (here $G(g) = 2^g - 1$ and $D(r) = 1/\log_2(r+1)$). Swapping the two documents
exchanges their positions, so the discounted-cumulative-gain changes by
$$
\Delta\mathrm{DCG} \;=\; \big(G(g_p) - G(g_q)\big)\,\big(D(q) - D(p)\big),
$$
and since the ideal DCG depends only on the *multiset* of grades — which a permutation leaves unchanged —
the normalizer is swap-invariant, giving
$$
\big|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}\big| \;=\; \frac{\big|G(g_i) - G(g_j)\big|\;\big|D(r_i) - D(r_j)\big|}{\mathrm{IDCG}}.
$$

*Proof.* Before the swap the two ranks contribute $G(g_p)D(p) + G(g_q)D(q)$ to DCG; after, the documents
trade places and contribute $G(g_p)D(q) + G(g_q)D(p)$. Subtracting gives
$\big(G(g_p)-G(g_q)\big)\big(D(q)-D(p)\big)$. Dividing by the (constant) IDCG and taking absolute values
yields the weight. Using the *untruncated* DCG makes this exact for every pair, including ones that
straddle the top-$k$ cutoff. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The companion notebook checks this closed form against a physical-swap recomputation of the imported
NDCG to within $10^{-12}$, over many pairs and queries. The weight factors into a **change in gain** times
a **change in discount**. The gain factor decides which pairs matter at all; the discount factor decides
*where on the list* they matter — and because the discount marginal $D(r) - D(r+1)$ is steep at the head
and flat in the tail (the [marginal-value geometry](/topics/ndcg-discount-geometry) from NDCG), a swap of
a given grade gap near the top of the list carries far more weight than the same swap in the tail.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="LambdaRank concentrates gradient mass at the head">
For a fixed grade gap, the LambdaRank-to-RankNet weight ratio $|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}| / 1$ is
*strictly decreasing* in the ranks of the pair, because $|D(r_i) - D(r_j)|$ shrinks as both ranks grow.
The adjacent-rank weight at the head, $|D(1) - D(2)| = 0.369$, exceeds the tail weight
$|D(9) - D(10)| = 0.012$ by an order of magnitude. Consequently LambdaRank's gradient mass concentrates
at the top of the list, while RankNet's is governed only by pair counts.
</TheoremBlock>

Panel A makes this visible: on the worked query, RankNet's force is spread down the list (its top-3 ranks
hold only $16\%$ of the gradient mass) while LambdaRank's is front-loaded ($40\%$). Same documents, same
scores — only the weighting moves. This is the **seed-free, provable** statement of LambdaRank's
advantage; as we will see, its *aggregate* NDCG win over RankNet on this corpus is real but small enough
to sit inside the confidence interval.

## The catch: LambdaRank optimizes no scalar loss

There is a price for defining the gradient directly instead of deriving it from a loss, and it is the
honest core of the topic. We have written down a vector field $\boldsymbol{\lambda}(\mathbf{s})$ on score
space — a force for each document, as a function of the scores. The natural question: is this field the
gradient of some scalar loss $L(\mathbf{s})$ we could just as well have minimized? For RankNet the answer
is yes, by construction. For LambdaRank it is no — and the *way* it is no is subtle.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The LambdaRank field is locally a gradient, globally not">
A vector field is the gradient of a scalar potential if and only if its Jacobian
$\partial \lambda_i / \partial s_j$ is symmetric (Clairaut's equality of mixed partials). Then:

- **Within a ranking cell** — an open region of score space where no two scores are tied, so the sorted
  order and hence every $|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}|$ is *constant* — the LambdaRank field is exactly
  $-\nabla \Phi$ for the weighted-RankNet potential
  $\Phi(\mathbf{s}) = \sum_{i \succ j} |\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ij}|\,\zeta\!\big(-(s_i - s_j)\big)$. Its
  Jacobian is symmetric; locally, it *is* a gradient.
- **Across a ranking cell** — when two adjacent documents $a, b$ swap — the rank of $a$ changes, so the
  weight $|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}_{ak}|$ of a *spectator* pair $(a, k)$ jumps discontinuously (its discount
  factor $|D(r_a) - D(r_k)|$ jumps). The field $\lambda_a$ is therefore **discontinuous** across the swap
  hyperplane. A discontinuous field is the gradient of no $C^1$ potential, so **no global loss exists**.
</TheoremBlock>

Panel B demonstrates all three faces of this on a three-document toy. Within a cell, both fields have a
symmetric Jacobian (asymmetry $0$) — so LambdaRank is *not* "broken everywhere," a tempting misreading.
Drag the probe across the swap boundary: RankNet's force varies smoothly (its across-boundary difference
shrinks with the step size — a continuous field), while LambdaRank's $\lambda_0$ **jumps** by a fixed
$0.082$ (the step-stable signature of a genuine discontinuity, here a spectator pair whose weight flips
its discount). And a closed loop in score space integrates to $0$ for RankNet — a conservative field —
but to a nonzero $-0.044$ for LambdaRank, the classical witness that a field is not a gradient.

<RigorFlag>
LambdaRank optimizes nothing — it is a *heuristic gradient*, locally a weighted-RankNet potential but
globally non-integrable. That it works at all is an empirical fact: Donmez, Svore, and Burges showed the
LambdaRank updates locally ascend NDCG, reaching a local optimum, but this is a property of the iteration,
not of a loss it descends. We name this result and rely on it; we do not prove it. The reweighting is the
correction to RankNet's position-blindness, paid for with the loss itself.
</RigorFlag>

## Listwise objectives: a proper, convex loss

Listwise methods restore what LambdaRank gave up. Instead of scoring documents in pairs, they put a
probability distribution over *whole permutations* and minimize a likelihood — a genuine loss with a
genuine optimum.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Plackett–Luce, ListMLE, and ListNet">
The **Plackett–Luce** model turns scores into a distribution over permutations by sequential softmax: the
probability that $\pi$ is the true order is
$$
P(\pi \mid \mathbf{s}) \;=\; \prod_{r=1}^{M} \frac{e^{s_{\pi(r)}}}{\sum_{r' \geq r} e^{s_{\pi(r')}}},
$$
each factor a softmax over the documents not yet placed. **ListMLE** minimizes the negative log-likelihood
of the *ideal* permutation $\pi^*$ (grades descending, ties broken by oracle score):
$$
L_{\mathrm{ListMLE}}(\mathbf{s}) \;=\; -\log P(\pi^* \mid \mathbf{s})
   \;=\; \sum_{r}\Big[\operatorname{logsumexp}\big(s_{\geq r}\big) - s_{\pi^*(r)}\Big].
$$
**ListNet** minimizes the top-one cross-entropy between the target distribution
$\operatorname{softmax}(G(g))$ and the model's $\operatorname{softmax}(\mathbf{s})$.
</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="The listwise losses are convex in the linear scorer">
For a linear scorer $\mathbf{s} = X\mathbf{w}$, both $L_{\mathrm{ListMLE}}$ and the ListNet loss are
convex in $\mathbf{w}$.

*Proof.* Each ListMLE term is $\operatorname{logsumexp}(s_{\geq r}) - s_{\pi^*(r)}$: a log-sum-exp (convex)
minus a linear function, hence convex; a sum of convex functions composed with the affine map
$\mathbf{w} \mapsto X\mathbf{w}$ is convex. The Hessian is
$\sum_r X_{\geq r}^\top\big(\mathrm{diag}(p_r) - p_r p_r^\top\big) X_{\geq r}$, a sum of softmax
covariances, which are positive semidefinite. The ListNet loss is likewise $\operatorname{logsumexp} -$
linear. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

The notebook verifies the gradients by finite differences, confirms the Hessian is positive semidefinite,
and checks that two starting points converge to the same optimum — a genuine single optimum, the
principled contrast to LambdaRank's non-integrable field. Panel C draws the convex bowl: a single basin
whose loss decreases monotonically as the slider walks toward the optimum. Two cautions keep this honest.
The convexity is only PSD, not strict — ListMLE is invariant to a global shift of all scores, a null
direction — and ListMLE reaches zero only in the *limit* as a perfectly-ordered score is scaled up, not
at any finite optimum.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" number={1} title="The candidate set and the ideal permutation">
Running Plackett–Luce over all $120$ documents is meaningless — $110$ of them are grade-0 and the
likelihood would be dominated by ordering noise. We restrict each query's listwise loss to a **candidate
set**: the $10$ graded documents plus the $10$ hardest grade-0 negatives (highest oracle score). The
ideal permutation $\pi^*$ is grades descending, ties broken by oracle score then document id — a
deterministic total order, resolving the non-uniqueness that grade ties would otherwise leave.
</TheoremBlock>

That ListMLE is the maximum-likelihood estimate of the Plackett–Luce model connects it directly to
[maximum likelihood](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/maximum-likelihood): the ideal permutation is
the datum, the scores are the log-abilities, and the per-stage softmax choices are categorical
[exponential families](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/exponential-families), whose
negative-log-likelihood convexity is exactly the theorem above.

## LambdaMART: boosting the lambdas into trees

LambdaRank's force is a heuristic gradient; nothing requires the scorer it updates to be linear. **MART** —
multiple additive regression trees, Friedman's gradient boosting — fits a regression tree to the negative
gradient each round and takes a shrunken step. LambdaMART is the marriage: the per-document LambdaRank
lambda *is* the pseudo-residual the trees regress.

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={1} title="LambdaMART">
Initialize scores $F_0 \equiv 0$. For each round $t = 1, \dots, T$:

1. Score every training document with the current ensemble and rank within each query.
2. Compute the LambdaRank force $\lambda_d$ for each document (using the current ranking's
   $|\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}|$ weights).
3. Fit a regression tree $h_t$ to the pseudo-residuals $-\lambda_d$ (the ascent direction, since
   $\lambda_d = \partial L / \partial s_d$).
4. Update $F_t = F_{t-1} + \nu\, h_t$, with shrinkage $\nu$.

The trees are exact-greedy and seeded (`random_state=0`), so the ensemble is deterministic — no
stochastic gradient descent. An empty ensemble ($T = 0$) leaves the base scores unchanged.
</TheoremBlock>

The payoff is expressivity. A linear scorer can only produce rankings that are linear separations of the
feature cloud; if the optimal ranking requires a *nonlinear interaction* of the leg scores, no weight
vector can express it.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="LambdaMART escapes a rank ceiling no linear scorer can reach">
Consider a query whose relevance is an **XOR** of two features: documents are relevant exactly when the
two features *agree* in sign ($(+,+)$ and $(-,-)$ are relevant; $(+,-)$ and $(-,+)$ are not). No linear
scorer $w_1 a + w_2 b$ can rank both relevant archetypes above both irrelevant ones — that would require
$-|w_1 + w_2| > |w_1 - w_2|$, which is impossible — so every linear scorer is capped below NDCG $1$. A
depth-2 regression tree splits on $a$ then $b$ and realizes the partition exactly. On this instance
LambdaMART reaches NDCG $1.0$ against a best-linear ceiling of $0.89$.
</TheoremBlock>

<RigorFlag>
The XOR result is a *constructed* instance, where the gap is provable: the linear ceiling is below 1 by
an impossibility argument, and the trees reach 1 by realizing the partition. On the real three-leg
features — near-linear and standardized — the trees do *not* meaningfully beat the linear models; the
boosting curve plateaus near the linear NDCG and the gain is within sampling noise. The headline "trees
beat linear" holds where there is a nonlinear interaction to capture, which a synthetic standardized
leg-score cloud does not supply. We report the real-corpus result honestly and pin the escape to the
constructed witness.
</RigorFlag>

## The finance comparison

The climax puts all five learned methods — RankNet, LambdaRank, ListNet, ListMLE, LambdaMART — on the
held-out test queries against reciprocal-rank fusion and the best single leg.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" number={2} title="The methods cluster within the confidence interval">
On the $16$ held-out queries, every learned method clearly beats RRF (NDCG $\approx 0.77$ versus $0.733$)
and the best single leg ($0.689$). Among themselves they cluster tightly — RankNet $0.767$, LambdaRank
$0.770$, ListMLE $0.769$, LambdaMART $0.772$, ListNet $0.773$ — a spread of $0.006$ against a confidence
interval of half-width $\approx 0.075$. The aggregate NDCG differences are *within sampling noise*; the
provable separations are structural, not aggregate.
</TheoremBlock>

<FinanceCaseStudy>
In a production financial-retrieval reranker — fusing a lexical signal over filings, a dense signal over
earnings-call transcripts, and a late-interaction signal over news — LambdaMART is the default choice, and
for a real reason: feature interactions are common (a document strong on dense *and* late-interaction but
weak on lexical is often more relevant than the sum of its parts suggests), and trees capture them where a
linear weighting cannot. On this synthetic complementary-view corpus the three standardized leg scores are
near-linear, so the methods tie within noise — the honest caveat. The seed-free lesson that *does*
transfer is LambdaRank's head-concentration: weighting by $\Delta\mathrm{NDCG}$ spends the model's
gradient where the user actually looks, which matters far more on a real corpus with a long, noisy tail
than on this forgiving one.
</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Where this points

LambdaMART and the listwise losses are the workhorses of classical learning-to-rank, but they share an
assumption: the model scores each document from a fixed feature vector, blind to the *other* documents in
the list except through the loss. The next step drops that assumption. **LLM listwise rerankers** — RankGPT
and its successors — feed the whole candidate list into a language model and ask it to emit a permutation
directly, scoring documents *in each other's context*. That is a listwise objective of a different kind,
and the subject of the next topic in this track. The thread that carries forward is the one this topic
made rigorous: a ranking objective is only as honest as the loss it descends, and when there is no loss —
as with LambdaRank, and as with an autoregressive permutation model — we owe the reader the caveat, not a
laundered claim.

<RigorFlag>
Three honesty checks close the topic. LambdaRank optimizes no scalar loss — it is locally a
weighted-RankNet gradient, globally a discontinuous field, and its empirical success rests on a
local-optimality result we cite but do not prove. The listwise convexity is genuine but conditional on the
linear scorer; a tree-boosted or deep listwise model is non-convex. And the method comparison's headline
deltas sit inside the confidence interval, so the reported wins — LambdaRank's head-concentration,
LambdaMART's escape of the linear ceiling — are the structural, seed-free ones, not the aggregate scores.
</RigorFlag>
