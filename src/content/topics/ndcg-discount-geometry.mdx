## From sets to grades

[Set metrics](/topics/set-metrics-precision-recall-map-mrr) scored a ranking against a *set* of
relevant documents: a document is in $R_q$ or it is not. That binary judgment throws away something
real. Asked for a company's interest-rate exposure, a retriever might surface the exact hedging
paragraph from a 10-K, a sentence that merely mentions rates in passing, and an off-topic disclosure.
The first is *perfectly* on point, the second *marginally* so, the third irrelevant — yet precision
and recall see only "relevant, relevant, not." **Graded relevance** keeps the distinction: each
document carries a grade $g \in \{0, 1, 2, 3\}$, from irrelevant to perfect.

To keep every number a measurement rather than an illustration, we reuse the prerequisite's apparatus
wholesale — the same shared corpus of $N = 120$ documents and $Q = 40$ queries, the same three real
legs (BM25, a pooled dense dual encoder, and late interaction), and the same **exact-MaxSim oracle**
as the neutral ground truth. The one new ingredient is the grade itself.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Graded relevance from the oracle">
For each query, the oracle's top-$K$ documents (with $K = 10$, the same set the prerequisite called
$R_q$) are assigned a grade by **global tertiles** of their exact-MaxSim score: the top third of those
scores grade $3$, the middle third grade $2$, the bottom third grade $1$; every other document grades
$0$. Writing $g_q(d)$ for the grade of document $d$ on query $q$, the relevant set is recovered exactly
as $\{\,d : g_q(d) \ge 1\,\} = R_q$.
</TheoremBlock>

That last equality is deliberate: the graded judgments *nest* the binary ones, so this topic measures
the same retrievers against the same documents the set-metric topic did — only now with a degree
attached. Across the forty queries the grades come out in balanced thirds (134 at grade 3, 133 at
grade 2, 133 at grade 1), so no single grade dominates the signal.

## Gain and discount: the two design choices

A hit is worth something for *being relevant* and something for *being near the top*. NDCG multiplies
the two: a document of grade $g$ at rank $i$ contributes $\mathrm{gain}(g)\cdot\mathrm{discount}(i)$.
Both factors are design choices, and naming them honestly is half the topic.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Gain and discount functions">
A **gain function** maps a grade to a reward. The two standard choices are
$$
\mathrm{gain}_{\text{lin}}(g) = g, \qquad \mathrm{gain}_{\text{exp}}(g) = 2^{g} - 1.
$$
Linear gain treats grade $2$ as worth twice grade $1$; exponential gain (the common modern default)
makes grade $3$ worth $7$ and grade $1$ worth $1$, sharply rewarding the surfacing of a single perfect
document. A **discount function** down-weights later ranks. The celebrated choice is the logarithmic
discount, alongside two comparisons,
$$
\mathrm{disc}_{\log}(i) = \frac{1}{\log_2(i+1)}, \qquad
\mathrm{disc}_{\text{geo}}(i) = p^{\,i-1}, \qquad
\mathrm{disc}_{\text{rec}}(i) = \frac{1}{i},
$$
each evaluated at the $1$-indexed rank $i$, so rank $1$ is undiscounted under $\mathrm{disc}_{\log}$.
</TheoremBlock>

Neither gain nor discount is forced by any axiom — they are conventions, and the last section shows
each one reversing a verdict. The logarithmic discount is the most heuristic of the lot: it is chosen
for its slow, heavy-tailed decay, not derived from a model of how anyone reads a result list. The
geometric discount, by contrast, comes with a story, which we take up in the discount-geometry panel.

## DCG, IDCG, NDCG

With a gain and a discount fixed, the rest is bookkeeping. Let $\pi$ be the ranking, so $\pi(i)$ is the
document at rank $i$, with grade $g_{\pi(i)}$.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Discounted, ideal, and normalized cumulative gain">
The **discounted cumulative gain** at cutoff $k$ is
$$
\mathrm{DCG@}k \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k} \mathrm{gain}(g_{\pi(i)})\,\mathrm{disc}(i).
$$
The **ideal DCG** is the largest DCG attainable by any ordering of the documents,
$\mathrm{IDCG@}k = \max_{\pi'} \mathrm{DCG@}k(\pi')$, obtained by sorting the grades in descending order
and discounting in place. The **normalized DCG** is their ratio,
$$
\mathrm{NDCG@}k \;=\; \frac{\mathrm{DCG@}k}{\mathrm{IDCG@}k} \;\in\; [0, 1],
$$
equal to $1$ exactly when the top $k$ is an ideal ordering.
</TheoremBlock>

Normalization is what makes NDCG comparable across queries: a query with six perfect documents and a
query with one have very different DCG ceilings, and dividing by each query's own IDCG puts both on
$[0,1]$. Panel A shows the worked query (which has an ideal grade profile $[3,2,2,2,2,2,1,1,1,1]$):
the bars are each leg's gains in the order it ranked them, the dashed outline is the ideal, and the
shaded fraction is NDCG. That a maximum over orderings is *achieved by sorting* is the claim the next
section proves — it is not a definition we may simply assert.

## The ideal ranking is optimal: the rearrangement inequality

Why is the normalizer the *sorted* ranking? Read DCG as an inner product. Fix the multiset of gains a
query's documents carry and stack them in ranked order as a vector $\mathbf{g}_\pi$; stack the
discounts as $\mathbf{d} = (\mathrm{disc}(1), \mathrm{disc}(2), \dots)$, a strictly decreasing
sequence. Then $\mathrm{DCG} = \langle \mathbf{g}_\pi, \mathbf{d}\rangle$, and the question "which
ranking maximizes DCG" becomes "which pairing of gains with a fixed decreasing weight vector maximizes
the inner product."

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The ideal ranking maximizes DCG (rearrangement inequality)">
Let $\mathbf{d}$ be a strictly decreasing discount vector and let the documents carry gains
$a_1, \dots, a_n$. Over all orderings, $\langle \mathbf{g}_\pi, \mathbf{d}\rangle$ is **maximized** by
placing the gains in descending order (largest gain at rank $1$) and **minimized** by ascending order.
Hence $\mathrm{IDCG}$ is exactly the descending-gain ranking, and $\mathrm{NDCG} \in [0,1]$ with
equality iff the ranking is ideal on the top $k$.

*Proof.* Suppose an ordering places gains out of order: some rank $i < j$ has $a_{\pi(i)} < a_{\pi(j)}$
while $\mathrm{disc}(i) > \mathrm{disc}(j)$. Swapping the two documents changes the inner product by
$$
\big(a_{\pi(j)} - a_{\pi(i)}\big)\big(\mathrm{disc}(i) - \mathrm{disc}(j)\big) \;>\; 0,
$$
a product of two positive factors, so the swap strictly increases DCG. Any non-descending arrangement
therefore admits an improving swap; only the descending arrangement admits none, so it is the unique
maximizer (up to ties among equal gains). The same argument with the inequality reversed shows the
ascending arrangement is the minimizer. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

This is the rigorous spine of the metric: the normalizer is not a convention but a theorem, and it is
the elementary rearrangement inequality — pair large with large. The notebook checks it directly,
confirming that across the corpus no random permutation of a query's gains beats the descending order
and the ascending order is strictly worst whenever the grades are not all equal.

The construction also pins the implementation. Under **linear gain and the logarithmic discount**,
$\mathrm{NDCG@}k$ here is identical — to better than $10^{-12}$ on every leg, query, and cutoff — to
the `ndcg_at_k` the [BM25 notebook](/topics/bm25-binary-independence-model) computed long before this
topic existed. The dependent's metric reduces exactly to the prerequisite's, which is the byte-for-byte
twin that anchors the code.

## Discount geometry

The discount is a fixed *measure* on rank positions, and its **shape** — the topic's namesake — decides
what the metric cares about. Two readings make the geometry concrete: how much total weight sits in the
head, and how steeply the value of a position falls.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Head mass and marginal value">
For a discount over $n$ positions, the **head mass** at cutoff $k$ is the fraction of total discount
weight in the top $k$,
$$
M_k \;=\; \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{k}\mathrm{disc}(i)}{\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathrm{disc}(i)},
$$
and the **marginal value** of rank $i$ is $\mathrm{disc}(i) - \mathrm{disc}(i+1)$, the worth lost in a
one-rank demotion.
</TheoremBlock>

Over the full $N = 120$ positions, the three discounts concentrate their weight very differently in the
top ten: the geometric discount ($p = 0.85$) puts $M_{10} = 0.80$ of its mass there, the reciprocal
discount $0.55$, and the logarithmic discount only $0.19$. The log discount has a **heavy tail** — it
keeps caring about documents deep in the list — where the geometric discount is **light-tailed**,
spending almost everything at the top. The marginal value tells the same story locally: every discount
loses the most between ranks $1$ and $2$ and flattens with depth, so a demotion off the top is the most
expensive move and a demotion deep down barely registers. That single factor,
$\mathrm{disc}(i) - \mathrm{disc}(i+1)$, is one half of the *swap sensitivity*
$\Delta = \Delta_{\text{gain}}\cdot\Delta_{\text{disc}}$ that pairwise learning-to-rank methods such as
LambdaRank weight their gradients by — the gain side of which we will meet in a later topic.

The geometric discount earns its shape from a model the logarithmic one cannot claim.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The rank-biased user model">
Under the geometric discount, model a reader who examines rank $i$ and then continues to rank $i+1$
with probability $p$, stopping with probability $1-p$. The probability of reaching rank $i$ is
$p^{\,i-1}$ — exactly $\mathrm{disc}_{\text{geo}}(i)$ — and the expected number of documents examined is
$$
\mathbb{E}[\text{documents examined}] \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} p^{\,i-1} \;=\; \frac{1}{1-p}.
$$
At $p = 0.85$ that is $6.67$ documents.
</TheoremBlock>

This is the honest contrast the rigor flag insists on: rank-biased precision's discount *is* a user
model, with a tunable patience $p$ and a closed expectation; the logarithmic discount is picked for its
heavy tail and its convenient algebra, with no such interpretation. Panel B lets you slide $p$ and
watch the geometric curve sweep from steep (impatient reader, weight pinned to rank 1) to nearly flat
(patient reader), against the fixed logarithmic and reciprocal curves.

## NDCG as an estimator

Here the prerequisite's central lesson carries over without a change of substance. A leg's mean NDCG is
not a property of the retriever; it is the **sample mean** of forty per-query NDCG values, and a
different forty queries would give a different number. Under exponential gain and the log discount the
three legs again form a quality ladder — lexical $0.72$, dense $0.77$, late interaction $0.88$ — but
those are estimates, and estimates have standard errors.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={5} title="Mean NDCG as an estimator with standard error">
Treat the per-query scores $\mathrm{NDCG}_1, \dots, \mathrm{NDCG}_Q$ as a sample with mean
$\widehat{\mathrm{NDCG}}$ and sample standard deviation $\hat\sigma$. The **standard error** is
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}} = \frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{Q}}, \qquad
\text{95\% CI} = \widehat{\mathrm{NDCG}} \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}},
$$
and it shrinks like $1/\sqrt{Q}$: quadrupling the query count halves the interval.
</TheoremBlock>

We reuse the prerequisite's machinery verbatim — the per-query scores are fed to the same
`metric_summary`, and the $1/\sqrt{n}$ rate is the same
[concentration inequality](https://www.formalml.com/topics/concentration-inequalities), verified
empirically by resampling. Panel D is the payoff, and it tells two stories. The **clearest** pair —
lexical versus late interaction, a $0.16$ gap — separates by $n = 15$ queries: you can confidently
report a winner. The **closest** pair — lexical versus dense, a mere $0.05$ gap — has intervals that
**overlap across all forty queries** and would need roughly $185$ to separate. Below that, the honest
report is "indistinguishable on this query set," not a leaderboard delta.

<RigorFlag>
The normal-approximation interval is itself an approximation: the per-query NDCG are bounded in $[0,1]$
and skewed, not Gaussian, and across legs they are *paired* (the same queries), which the
independent-interval overlap test ignores — so it is conservative. We cross-check the standard error
with the [bootstrap](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/bootstrap), which resamples the per-query
scores without the normality assumption and agrees to within roughly a sixth. Overlapping intervals are
a *crude* read; the proper instrument is a paired
[significance test](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/hypothesis-testing) on the per-query
differences, the subject of the next topic. This one only establishes that the variance is there.
</RigorFlag>

## Convention sensitivity and consistency

Because the gain and the discount are choices, the verdict can move with them — and we build and run
the flip before stating it. On this corpus the three legs are a quality ladder under *every*
convention, so there is no aggregate reversal; but the convention reverses the per-query verdict on
five leg-pair-and-query instances, and two minimal constructed examples flip it starkly.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="The convention can reverse the verdict (demonstrated)">
**Gain flip.** One query with one perfect document ($g = 3$) and three marginal ones ($g = 1$). A
"headline" ranking puts the perfect document at rank 1 and buries the rest; a "broad" ranking puts the
three marginal documents in the top three and the perfect one at rank 4. Under exponential gain the
headline ranking wins, $\mathrm{NDCG} = 0.82$ versus $0.60$; under linear gain the broad ranking wins,
$0.75$ versus $0.66$.

**Discount flip.** One query with three equally relevant documents. A "top-heavy" ranking lands one at
rank 1 and misses the rest within the cutoff; a "deep" ranking lands all three at ranks 2–4. Under a
steep geometric discount ($p = 0.5$) the top-heavy ranking wins, $0.57$ versus $0.50$; under the
heavy-tailed logarithmic discount the deep ranking wins, $0.73$ versus $0.47$.

In each case neither ranking is "better" in any absolute sense — the metric's convention decides, and a
reported NDCG that does not state its gain and discount is underspecified.
</TheoremBlock>

The mechanism is exactly the geometry above. Exponential gain steepens the *grade* axis, so planting
the one perfect document at the top outweighs several marginal ones; a steep discount steepens the
*rank* axis, so a single hit at rank 1 outweighs three hits just below. Choose the convention to match
the task — a known-item search wants steep everything, a recall-oriented sweep wants the heavy tail —
and report which you chose.

<RigorFlag>
A last caveat on the cutoff. NDCG over the *full* list, with a smooth gain, is **consistent**:
optimizing it drives toward a Bayes-optimal ranking. Truncated $\mathrm{NDCG@}k$ need not be — Wang et
al. (COLT 2013) exhibit gain-and-discount-and-cutoff combinations where minimizing NDCG@k regret does
not minimize ranking error. The truncation that makes NDCG@10 practical is therefore not free, and the
choice of gain and discount is load-bearing for more than the leaderboard.
</RigorFlag>

The production system this series tracks retrieves over 10-K and 10-Q filing text and earnings-call
transcripts, and graded relevance is the natural fit: for "interest-rate exposure," the dedicated
hedging disclosure is a grade-3 answer, a passage that mentions rates among other risks is a grade-2,
a one-line aside is a grade-1, and the rest is grade-0. The choice of gain then encodes what the desk
wants. An analyst who needs the *single* authoritative disclosure is served by exponential gain, which
rewards a retriever for planting that grade-3 document at the very top even if it misses some marginal
context; a research sweep assembling everything on a theme is better matched by linear gain and the
log discount's heavy tail, which keeps crediting relevant material deep in the list. Picking the wrong
convention rewards the wrong retriever — and even the right comparison is provisional: with only forty
evaluation queries, a five-point NDCG gap can sit entirely inside the sampling error, so the
disciplined report is a confidence interval, and a decision to switch retrievers waits on a paired
significance test, not a single number. The corpus here is a synthetic vMF token cloud standing in for
those documents; every value illustrates the mechanism, not a benchmark.
</FinanceCaseStudy>
