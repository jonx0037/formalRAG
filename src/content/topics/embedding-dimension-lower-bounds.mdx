## Overview & motivation

[Dense retrieval](/topics/dense-retrieval-dual-encoders) left us with a clean upper bound. Stack the scores of every query against every document into a relevance matrix; a $d$-dimensional dual encoder can realize any such matrix of rank at most $d$, and below that rank the truncated SVD — Eckart–Young — is the best it can do. That theorem answers *how many dimensions suffice to reproduce the scores*. It is not the question a retrieval engineer actually has, which is sharper and more practical: how many dimensions does **relevance** need — not to match the real-valued scores, but to get the *order* right, to put every relevant document above every irrelevant one?

These are different questions, and the gap between them is the whole subject of this topic. Relevance is a **sign pattern**: for each query, a partition of the corpus into relevant and not. What a single-vector retriever must reproduce is that pattern of signs, and the smallest dimension in which a pattern of signs is linearly realizable is its **sign-rank**, not its rank. The two can be wildly different. We will see a relevance pattern of full rank $n$ whose sign-rank is $3$ — most of the rank is wasted on magnitudes the ranking never uses — and, going the other way, patterns whose sign-rank is forced upward without bound, so that no fixed embedding dimension can represent them at all. The second kind is not a pathology: it is what *combinatorial* relevance looks like, the queries a real analyst asks, and it is the reason a single-vector embedding has a ceiling that no amount of training, data, or scale removes.

The second is a closed-form wall — Forster's spectral lower bound forcing a Hadamard relevance pattern to need at least $\sqrt{N}$ dimensions. The third is the retrieval wall: the largest corpus whose all-pairs relevance pattern a $d$-dimensional model can realize, even with perfect embeddings, growing only polynomially in $d$.

## Notation: relevance as a sign pattern

Fix $m$ queries and $n$ documents. A **qrel matrix** $A \in \{0,1\}^{m \times n}$ records relevance: $A_{ij} = 1$ when document $j$ is relevant to query $i$. Its signed form is $M = 2A - \mathbf{1} \in \{-1, +1\}^{m \times n}$, with $+1$ for relevant and $-1$ for not. A single-vector retriever realizes this pattern if it can place query embeddings $x_i$ and document embeddings $y_j$ in some $\mathbb{R}^d$ so that, within each query's row, every relevant document outscores every irrelevant one — equivalently, so that the score matrix $B = X Y^\top$ has the right signs against a per-row threshold. Absorbing the threshold into an extra constant coordinate, the clean object is the **sign-rank**.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Sign-rank (dimension complexity)">

For a sign matrix $M \in \{-1, +1\}^{m \times n}$, the **sign-rank** is

$$
\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \;=\; \min\big\{ \operatorname{rank}(B) \;:\; B \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n},\ \operatorname{sign}(B_{ij}) = M_{ij}\ \text{for all } i,j \big\}.
$$

It is the smallest inner dimension $d$ in which the pattern $M$ is linearly realizable as $\operatorname{sign}(X Y^\top)$ with $X \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times d}$, $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ — the dimension the relevance *pattern* needs, with the score *magnitudes* free.

</TheoremBlock>

Throughout, $\|M\|_2$ is the spectral norm (largest singular value) and $\operatorname{rank}(M)$ the ordinary rank. The contrast in one line: rank constrains the matrix of scores; sign-rank constrains only their signs, so it is never larger than the rank — a matrix realizes its own signs at $d = \operatorname{rank}(M)$ — and is usually strictly smaller.

## Movement 1 — rank is the wrong complexity ⇒ sign-rank

The cleanest way to see that rank and sign-rank are different measures is a single pattern on which they disagree maximally.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="The signed identity: full rank, sign-rank three">

Let $M = 2 I_n - J_n \in \{-1,+1\}^{n \times n}$ be the **signed identity**: $+1$ on the diagonal, $-1$ off it. Then for every $n \ge 3$, $\operatorname{rank}(M) = n$ while $\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \le 3$. The relevance pattern "each query's own document, and no other" therefore needs only three dimensions, however many documents there are.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

*Rank.* $J_n$, the all-ones matrix, has eigenvalues $n$ (once) and $0$ (with multiplicity $n-1$), so $M = 2 I_n - J_n$ has eigenvalues $2 - n$ (once) and $2$ (with multiplicity $n-1$). For $n \ge 3$ both values are nonzero, so all $n$ eigenvalues are nonzero and $\operatorname{rank}(M) = n$.

*Sign-rank.* We exhibit a rank-$3$ realization geometrically. Place the $n$ queries at points $p_1, \dots, p_n \in \mathbb{R}^2$ in strictly convex position — the vertices of a regular $n$-gon. Each $p_j$ is then a vertex of the convex hull, so there is a line $\ell_j$ with $p_j$ strictly on its positive side and every other $p_i$ strictly on its negative side; write $\ell_j$ as $\{z : \langle a_j, z\rangle + b_j = 0\}$ with $\langle a_j, p_i\rangle + b_j > 0 \iff i = j$. Lift each query to $x_i = (p_i, 1) \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and each document to $y_j = (a_j, b_j) \in \mathbb{R}^3$. Then

$$
(X Y^\top)_{ij} \;=\; \langle x_i, y_j\rangle \;=\; \langle a_j, p_i\rangle + b_j,
$$

which is positive exactly when $i = j$ and negative otherwise — precisely $\operatorname{sign}(X Y^\top) = M$. Since $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 3}$ and $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times 3}$, the realizing matrix has rank at most $3$, so $\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \le 3$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The proof is the whole intuition. Rank counts independent directions in the *scores*; sign-rank counts the dimensions the *geometry* needs to separate each point from the rest, and $n$ points in convex position separate with a two-dimensional picture plus one affine coordinate. The notebook confirms it numerically: for the $4 \times 4$ signed identity it finds an explicit rank-$3$ matrix matching all sixteen signs and fails to find any rank-$2$ one (the sign-rank is in fact exactly $3$, a classical value we cite rather than belabor). The laboratory's first panel shows the same gap as a margin: the pattern is unrealizable below three dimensions — the achievable margin is zero — and at $d = 3$ a positive margin appears, widening as $d$ grows.

This already overturns the reflex that "more relevance structure means more dimensions." A relevance matrix can be full rank and yet, as a pattern, demand only a constant dimension. The sign-rank, not the rank, is the honest answer to *how many dimensions relevance needs* — and the rest of the topic is about when that answer is small, when it is provably large, and what it means for retrieval.

## Movement 2 — a closed-form lower bound: Forster on Hadamard

The signed identity is the easy direction: a pattern that *looks* expensive but is cheap. The hard and more consequential direction is a pattern that is genuinely expensive — one whose sign-rank we can lower-bound by a closed form, with no optimization and no guessing. The tool is a spectral inequality of Forster.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Forster's spectral lower bound (cited)">

For every sign matrix $M \in \{-1, +1\}^{m \times n}$,

$$
\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \;\ge\; \frac{\sqrt{m\,n}}{\|M\|_2}.
$$

A small spectral norm forces a large sign-rank: a pattern whose energy is spread evenly across all singular directions cannot be realized in few dimensions.

</TheoremBlock>

Forster's theorem is proved by a balancing argument — every sign matrix admits a realization whose query and document points are in near-isotropic position, and isotropy plus the unit-modulus entries bound the spectral norm from below in terms of the dimension. The argument is delicate and we cite it. What we *do* prove is its sharpest consequence, on the matrix that saturates it.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="Hadamard patterns need √N dimensions">

Let $H \in \{-1, +1\}^{N \times N}$ be a Hadamard matrix (orthogonal rows, $H H^\top = N I$). Then $\|H\|_2 = \sqrt{N}$ and therefore

$$
\operatorname{rank}_\pm(H) \;\ge\; \frac{\sqrt{N \cdot N}}{\sqrt{N}} \;=\; \sqrt{N}.
$$

A relevance pattern shaped like $H$ provably needs at least $\sqrt{N}$ dimensions, with no appeal to optimization.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof" title="Proof">

The rows of $H$ are orthogonal and each has squared norm $N$, so $H H^\top = N I_N$. Hence every eigenvalue of $H H^\top$ equals $N$, every singular value of $H$ equals $\sqrt{N}$, and in particular $\|H\|_2 = \sqrt{N}$. Substituting $m = n = N$ and $\|H\|_2 = \sqrt{N}$ into Forster's bound gives $\operatorname{rank}_\pm(H) \ge N / \sqrt{N} = \sqrt{N}$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is the first genuinely *negative* result of the curriculum's expressivity track: an explicit family of relevance patterns whose required dimension grows without bound, $\sqrt{N}$ at $N$ documents, provably, in closed form. The laboratory's second panel walks the Sylvester–Hadamard matrices $N = 2, 4, 8, 16$ and shows the bound $\sqrt{N}$ rising; the notebook corroborates it from the other side, failing to realize $H_{16}$ in three dimensions when the bound says four are required. Where the signed identity showed sign-rank far *below* the rank, Hadamard shows it can be a hard floor — and crucially, a floor we can certify without solving an intractable problem.

### Eckart–Young is the wrong norm

It is worth saying precisely why DPR's rank story does not already answer this. The truncated SVD is optimal for the **Frobenius** norm: $M_d = \arg\min_{\operatorname{rank} B \le d} \|M - B\|_F$. But Frobenius error weights large-magnitude entries, and a relevance pattern cares only about signs against a per-row threshold. A rank-$d$ approximation can have small Frobenius error and still flip the signs of the small entries that decide a close ranking; conversely the signs can be entirely correct at a rank where the Frobenius error is still large. The notebook makes this concrete on the signed identity: the Frobenius-optimal rank-$3$ truncation does *not* reproduce every sign, while a rank-$3$ realization built for the signs does. Approximating in rank and realizing a sign pattern are different objectives, and the SVD solves the first. This is the load-bearing reason sign-rank, not rank, is the relevant complexity — and it is `rigorFlag` territory, because the "rank $\le d$" framing we inherited is exposition, not the tight measure.

## Movement 3 — margin complexity: the dimension for a usable gap

Sign-rank is honest but austere: it asks only that the signs come out right, allowing scores to sit arbitrarily close to the threshold. A retriever trained by a contrastive (soft-margin) loss does not get to live on that knife edge — it needs a *gap*, a margin by which relevant beats irrelevant, or noise and finite samples will flip the order. The capacity measure that respects this is the **margin complexity**.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Margin complexity">

For a realization $\operatorname{sign}(X Y^\top) = M$, its margin is $\min_{ij} M_{ij}\langle x_i, y_j\rangle / (\|x_i\|\,\|y_j\|)$, the smallest normalized gap by which any entry has the correct sign. Writing $\mathrm{m}(M)$ for the largest margin over all realizations in any dimension, the **margin complexity** is $\mathrm{mc}(M) = 1/\mathrm{m}(M)$.

</TheoremBlock>

Margin complexity and sign-rank are close relatives but not the same number. Linial, Mendelson, Schechtman, and Shraibman tie them together through the $\gamma_2$ norm — a quantity with a semidefinite-programming characterization, hence *computable*, unlike sign-rank itself — by the inequalities $\mathrm{mc}(M) \le \gamma_2(M)$ and $\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \le O(\mathrm{mc}(M)^2 \log N)$. The direction that matters for retrieval is the second read backward: a *large* margin (small $\mathrm{mc}$) forces a *small* sign-rank, but a small sign-rank does **not** promise a usable margin — a pattern can be realizable in few dimensions only by hugging the threshold. For a model that must generalize from finite data, margin complexity is therefore the more faithful measure of how many dimensions relevance needs, and it is never smaller than the sign-rank story suggests.

The laboratory's first panel shows exactly this shape on the signed identity: zero margin below the sign-rank (no correct realization exists), then a positive margin at $d = 3$ that *grows* as $d$ increases. More dimensions do not just make the pattern realizable; they make it robustly so. We report the achievable margins found by projected gradient — an honest lower bound on the best margin at each dimension — and leave the exact $\gamma_2$ value as the semidefinite program it is, described but not solved.

## Movement 4 — the retrieval theorem and the free-embedding wall

The abstract story becomes a statement about retrieval through a recent result of Weller, Boratko, Naim, and Lee — the *LIMIT* paper — which pins the embedding dimension a qrel matrix demands directly to its sign-rank.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={4} title="The qrel dimension is the sign-rank (cited)">

For a binary qrel matrix $A \in \{0,1\}^{m \times n}$, let $d^\star(A)$ be the smallest embedding dimension in which a single-vector model can rank, within every query's row, all relevant documents above all irrelevant ones. Then

$$
\operatorname{rank}_\pm(2A - \mathbf{1}) - 1 \;\le\; d^\star(A) \;\le\; \operatorname{rank}_\pm(2A - \mathbf{1}).
$$

The minimum embedding dimension of a relevance pattern is its sign-rank, up to one.

</TheoremBlock>

The sandwich turns every sign-rank fact into a retrieval fact. Forster's Hadamard bound becomes: a qrel whose signed form is Hadamard cannot be realized below $\sqrt{N}$ dimensions, period. And it lets us *measure* the wall on a concrete construction. Take the **all-pairs** qrel: $n$ documents, one query for every unordered pair $\{i,j\}$, relevant to exactly documents $i$ and $j$. This is the densest top-$2$ pattern, and the experiment that isolates the architecture from every confound is to optimize the embeddings **freely** — drop the encoder, drop the training data, let $X$ and $Y$ be unconstrained parameters fit directly to the qrel — and find the largest document count a given dimension can realize.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="The free-embedding wall (measured)">

Let the **critical $n$** at dimension $d$ be the largest $n$ whose all-pairs qrel is realizable by free embeddings in $\mathbb{R}^d$. The notebook measures it growing slowly with $d$ — on its grid, $4, 4, 6, 12, 16$ at $d = 2, 3, 4, 5, 6$ — and confirms that beyond the critical $n$ the best achievable row-order accuracy drops below one. Even with *perfect*, freely optimized embeddings, each dimension admits only a bounded combinatorial corpus.

</TheoremBlock>

This is the practical face of the sign-rank ceiling. The all-pairs pattern over many documents has a sign-rank that climbs faster than any fixed $d$ can follow, so at a chosen embedding dimension there is always a corpus size past which some combination of relevant documents is simply unrepresentable — not poorly trained, *unrepresentable*. The LIMIT authors extrapolate the same growth to the dimensions production systems use and find the critical corpus, even for an idealized embedder, lands well below web scale. The wall is real, it is best-case, and it is the property of a single vector per document; we return to the escape below.

## The communication-complexity bridge

One last reframing explains why the limit is fundamental rather than an artifact of how we fit embeddings. Sign-rank is not only a geometric quantity — it is an *information* quantity. Paturi and Simon proved that the unbounded-error randomized communication complexity of a sign matrix $M$ — the number of bits two parties, holding the row and the column respectively, must exchange to decide $M_{ij}$ with *any* advantage over a coin flip — equals $\log_2 \operatorname{rank}_\pm(M)$ up to an additive constant. The embedding dimension a relevance pattern needs is, up to a logarithm, the number of bits its relevance decision contains. A pattern that needs many dimensions needs them because its relevance is genuinely information-rich, not because the optimizer was weak — which is why no amount of training removes the wall.

## Finance case study

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text", "pdf"]}>

A production retrieval system over financial filings, earnings calls, and analyst notes embeds every passage as one vector and answers a query by inner-product search — and its embedding dimension is a representational budget the sign-rank theory prices exactly. The previous topic's task is the benign case. "Find this company's filings" is a low-sign-rank pattern: queries cluster by issuer and sector, each relevant set is small and *aligned* with the geometry, and a modest dimension separates it cleanly. On DPR's synthetic finance corpus — four sectors, two companies each, one document per company — single-company retrieval recovers full recall@1 at embedding dimension $d = 6$, comfortably below the rank-$8$ ceiling. So far the dimension budget is generous.

Now ask the questions analysts actually ask. "Which issuers are exposed to *both* rising rates *and* supply-chain disruption?" "Find filings that discuss *both* the pension shortfall *and* the buyback." These are **combinatorial** relevance patterns: each query is relevant to a *combination* of documents, and as the combinations multiply the qrel's signed form approaches the all-pairs construction whose sign-rank climbs without bound. The flip is stark and the notebook measures it: build the all-pairs combinatorial qrel over a larger but comparable corpus of $24$ companies, and the embedding dimension that *perfectly* solved single-company retrieval — $d = 6$ — can no longer realize it. Row-order accuracy stalls below one; the pattern is realized only at a strictly larger dimension. The same budget that was generous for simple relevance is *insufficient* for combinatorial relevance, on the same kind of corpus.

The engineering lesson is that the embedding dimension is not a free knob to be raised at will — the wall climbs faster than you can pay for it — so the architecture must change. A multi-vector late-interaction retriever escapes the single-vector sign-rank bound by scoring many vectors per document; a sparse lexical leg ([BM25](/topics/bm25-binary-independence-model)) answers the conjunctive "both X and Y" queries that single-vector dense retrieval is provably bad at; and a reranker can fix the residual order on a short candidate list. The production system is multi-architecture not by fashion but because a single embedding has a ceiling this topic proves, and the combinatorial queries that matter most in finance are exactly the ones that hit it.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

## Honest accounting

<RigorFlag>

Two results here are proved in full and the rest is cited or measured. The signed-identity gap is proved both directions — the rank by an eigenvalue computation, the sign-rank $\le 3$ by a convex-position construction — and Forster's bound applied to a Hadamard matrix is proved from the orthogonality of its rows; Forster's *general* theorem, the balancing argument behind $\operatorname{rank}_\pm(M) \ge \sqrt{mn}/\|M\|_2$, is cited, not reproved, as is the LIMIT sandwich pinning the qrel dimension to the sign-rank. The "rank $\le d$" framing inherited from DPR is our exposition: Eckart–Young governs approximation in rank, the wrong norm for a sign pattern, which is exactly the mismatch Movement 2 exhibits. Sign-rank is the right measure but it is intractable to compute — deciding $\operatorname{rank}_\pm \le 3$ is complete for the existential theory of the reals, and the strict $\pm 1$ case is NP-hard — so we compute it exactly only for tiny matrices and otherwise rely on Forster lower bounds; many sign-rank lower bounds are existential, proving that hard patterns exist without exhibiting one, with Razborov–Sherstov's $\mathrm{AC}^0$ bound the explicit-construction frontier. Margin complexity and sign-rank are polynomially related with a $\log N$ slack, *not* equal, and the exact margin complexity is a semidefinite program we describe but do not solve — the laboratory reports achievable margins found by projected gradient, an honest lower bound on the best margin at each dimension. The free-embedding critical $n$ is an empirical best-case optimization, not a closed-form theorem; it shows the wall by demonstration where Forster-on-Hadamard proves a wall in closed form. The ceiling is specific to single-vector dot-product retrieval: multi-vector late interaction, cross-encoders, and sparse lexical retrieval are not bound by it. And every finance number is measured on the synthetic von Mises–Fisher cloud reused from the InfoNCE and DPR topics, not a trained encoder.

</RigorFlag>

The dimension lower bound is the deepest impossibility result in the dense-retrieval track, and it points directly at the architectures that route around it. **Late interaction** replaces a single pooled vector with a token-level comparison, lifting the relevance matrix out of the sign-rank bound proved here — the explicit escape, and the next topic in this track. **Cross-encoders**, which DPR kept invoking as the rank-free counterpoint, pay a quadratic cost to fuse query and document and so are not factorizations at all. And the [evaluation track](/topics/the-retrieval-problem) measures *where* the wall bites: the metrics that follow are how you detect, on real qrels, that a single embedding has run out of dimensions.
