## Overview & motivation

[The retrieval problem](/topics/the-retrieval-problem) ended with a ranking: a query $q$, a score
$\mathrm{rel}(q, d)$ on every document $d$, and the top-$k$ returned as a set. Everything the
curriculum built afterward — BM25, dense dual encoders, IVF and product quantization, late
interaction served by PLAID, reciprocal-rank fusion — was judged by a single recurring number,
*recall@k*. The capstone reported it a dozen times. Not once did we say what it means.

That is the gap this topic closes. An **evaluation metric** is a functional that takes a ranking and
a set of *relevance judgments* and returns a number. Write the judgments as a **qrel set** $R_q
\subseteq \mathcal{D}$: the documents a competent analyst would mark relevant to query $q$. Because we
want every number on this page to be a measurement rather than an illustration, we do not invent
rankings — we score three *real* retrievers over a shared finance corpus. The lexical leg is BM25,
the dense leg is a pooled dual encoder, and the strongest leg is late interaction, each producing a
full ranking of $N = 120$ documents for each of $Q = 40$ queries. The relevance judgments are the
top-$k$ documents under *exact MaxSim*, a **neutral oracle**: no single leg *is* the ground truth, so
the three genuinely differ in quality, and "relevance" is a fixed target they all approximate.

queries — and then admit that every resulting number is a noisy estimate.

## Set metrics at a cutoff: precision and recall

Fix a query and a cutoff $k$. Of the top $k$ documents the system returned, write
$\mathrm{TP}_k = |\,\mathrm{top}_k(q) \cap R_q\,|$ for the number that are relevant (true positives).
The two foundational metrics divide this count two ways.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Precision and recall at a cutoff">
For a ranking with qrel set $R$ (with $|R| > 0$) and a cutoff $k \ge 1$,
$$
P@k \;=\; \frac{|\,\mathrm{top}_k \cap R\,|}{k}, \qquad
R@k \;=\; \frac{|\,\mathrm{top}_k \cap R\,|}{|R|}.
$$
Precision is the **purity** of the cutoff — what fraction of what you returned is relevant — and
recall is the **coverage** of the relevant set — what fraction of what you should have returned you
did. Their harmonic mean is the $F_1$ score, $F_1@k = 2\,P@k\,R@k / (P@k + R@k)$.
</TheoremBlock>

The denominator of recall is $|R|$, the size of the relevant set. This is the textbook convention,
and it is worth a word because it differs from the one the capstone used: there, recall divided by
$\min(k, |R|)$, a cascade-retention reading ("of the $k$ slots, how many true neighbors survived").
The two agree exactly at $k = |R|$, the capstone's only operating point, so this topic defines
precisely the recall@10 the capstone reported; the $|R|$ form is what generalizes the curve, because
it makes the next proposition true.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Recall climbs, precision wobbles">
As the cutoff $k$ increases, $R@k$ is non-decreasing, and $R@N = 1$. Precision $P@k$ is **not**
monotone.

*Proof.* The set $\mathrm{top}_{k+1}$ contains $\mathrm{top}_k$, so $\mathrm{TP}_k$ is
non-decreasing in $k$; dividing by the constant $|R|$ preserves this. At $k = N$ every document is
returned, so $\mathrm{TP}_N = |R|$ and $R@N = 1$. For precision, $\mathrm{TP}_k$ rises by one exactly
when document $k{+}1$ is relevant, while the denominator $k$ rises by one every step; so $P@(k{+}1)$
exceeds $P@k$ when the new document is relevant and falls when it is not. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

Panel A makes the asymmetry tactile. Drag the cutoff across the worked query's ranking: each slot is
a true positive (relevant and retrieved), a false positive (retrieved, not relevant), or — beyond the
cutoff — a false negative (relevant, missed). Recall only ever rises as the line moves right;
precision jumps up on a relevant slot and decays on an irrelevant one. A single $(P@k, R@k)$ pair is a
point on a plane; the whole topic is what happens when we let $k$ run.

## The precision–recall curve and Average Precision

Sweeping $k$ from $1$ to $N$ traces a path in the precision–recall plane. Because recall only changes
when a relevant document is hit, the informative points are exactly those hits. Let the relevant
documents sit at ranks $\mathrm{pos}_1 < \mathrm{pos}_2 < \dots < \mathrm{pos}_m$ (with $m = |R|$); at
the $i$-th hit, recall is $R_i = i/m$ and precision is $P_i = i/\mathrm{pos}_i$. Connecting these
corners gives the familiar sawtooth — and the single most useful summary of a ranking is the area
beneath it.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Average Precision is the area under the PR curve">
Define **Average Precision** as the mean of precision at the relevant ranks,
$$
\mathrm{AP} \;=\; \frac{1}{|R|}\sum_{i=1}^{m} P_i \;=\; \frac{1}{|R|}\sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{i}{\mathrm{pos}_i}.
$$
Then $\mathrm{AP}$ equals the area under the (raw) precision–recall curve,
$$
\mathrm{AP} \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{m} (R_i - R_{i-1})\,P_i, \qquad R_0 = 0.
$$

*Proof.* At rank $\mathrm{pos}_i$ exactly $i$ relevant documents have been retrieved, so recall steps
from $(i{-}1)/m$ to $i/m$ — an increment $R_i - R_{i-1} = 1/m$ — and precision there is $P_i$. Between
consecutive relevant ranks recall is constant, so those stretches add nothing to a recall-integrated
sum. Hence $\sum_i (R_i - R_{i-1}) P_i = \sum_i \tfrac{1}{m} P_i = \tfrac{1}{m}\sum_i P_i = \mathrm{AP}$.
$\square$
</TheoremBlock>

This is a Riemann sum over the recall axis: the relevant documents partition $[0,1]$ into $m$ equal
increments of width $1/m$, and AP is the sum of precision times those widths — the
[area-as-a-limit-of-sums](https://www.formalcalculus.com/topics/riemann-integral) construction applied to
a step function. The proof also explains the denominator $|R|$ rather than "number of documents
retrieved": a relevant document that never surfaces contributes a *missing* $1/m$ recall step, i.e. a
term of zero, which correctly drags AP down. Dividing by the count actually found would give a
ranking that retrieves one of ten relevant documents (at rank 1) an AP of $1.0$ — exactly wrong.

Panel B draws this. The shaded bars are the recall steps; their total area is AP. Toggle the
interpolated envelope and a second, larger area appears.

<RigorFlag>
"Average Precision" is ambiguous until you name a convention. The **raw** AP above — the area under
the sawtooth, equal to the mean of precision at the relevant ranks — is the form MAP averages and the
one we report. The **interpolated** AP replaces each precision with the best precision attainable at
any equal-or-higher recall, $p_{\text{interp}}(r) = \max\{P_j : R_j \ge r\}$, a monotone
non-increasing envelope. Its area is *always at least* the raw AP, because the envelope dominates the
raw precision pointwise — interpolation **inflates** the number, it does not correct it. The classic
eleven-point interpolated average precision (TREC) samples that envelope at recall $0, 0.1, \dots, 1$.
None of these is "the" AP; they are conventions, and a reported AP that does not state which one was
used is underspecified.
</RigorFlag>

## Mean Average Precision

A single query's AP is a property of one ranking. To score a *system* we average over a query set.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Mean Average Precision">
For a set of $Q$ queries, $\displaystyle \mathrm{MAP} = \frac{1}{Q}\sum_{q=1}^{Q} \mathrm{AP}_q$, the
sample mean of per-query Average Precision.
</TheoremBlock>

Over our three legs the MAP values are a clean quality ladder: lexical $0.619$, dense $0.735$, late
interaction $0.874$. Panel C shows why an average is the right summary — the per-query AP scatter
widely (the late-interaction leg ranges from below $0.6$ to a perfect $1.0$), and only their mean is
stable. That scatter is not noise to be ignored; it is the metric's variance, and we return to it.

## Reciprocal rank and known-item retrieval

Some queries have one right answer — the filing that announced a merger, the slide that stated a
guidance number. There, what matters is not coverage of a set but how high the *one* answer ranks.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Reciprocal rank and MRR">
The **reciprocal rank** of a query is $\mathrm{RR} = 1/\mathrm{rank}_1$, where $\mathrm{rank}_1$ is the
rank of the first relevant document (and $\mathrm{RR} = 0$ if none appears). The **Mean Reciprocal
Rank** is $\displaystyle \mathrm{MRR} = \frac{1}{Q}\sum_{q=1}^{Q}\mathrm{RR}_q$.
</TheoremBlock>

MRR is built for the **known-item** regime, where each query has exactly one relevant document — and
there it is not a different animal from AP at all.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="MAP equals MRR in the known-item regime">
If every query has exactly one relevant document ($|R_q| = 1$ for all $q$), then $\mathrm{AP}_q =
\mathrm{RR}_q$ for every query, and hence $\mathrm{MAP} = \mathrm{MRR}$.

*Proof.* With $m = 1$, the single relevant document sits at some rank $r_q$. Average Precision has one
term: $\mathrm{AP}_q = \tfrac{1}{1}\cdot\tfrac{1}{r_q} = 1/r_q$. The first (and only) relevant
document is at rank $r_q$, so $\mathrm{RR}_q = 1/r_q$ too. The per-query metrics coincide, and so do
their means. $\square$
</TheoremBlock>

This is the topic's collapse anchor: at $|R| = 1$ the whole apparatus of precision, recall, and the PR
curve degenerates to a single reciprocal rank. Panel C makes it visible — switch to the known-item
regime and the per-query AP strip lands exactly on the reciprocal-rank strip, mean line and all.
Because MRR is degenerate when relevant sets are large (with ten relevant documents some leg almost
always lands one near the top, so reciprocal rank says little), we score it in the regime it was built
for: relevance is the single top-1 oracle document.

Holding both metrics in view exposes something the quality ladder hid. The MAP ranking is
lexical $<$ dense $<$ late interaction; the MRR ranking is dense $<$ lexical $<$ late interaction.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={3} title="Metric choice can reverse the verdict (demonstrated)">
On this corpus, **dense beats lexical on MAP** ($0.735$ vs $0.619$) but **lexical beats dense on
MRR** ($0.522$ vs $0.472$). The verdict between two systems reverses depending on the metric.

The mechanism is structural: MAP rewards ranking the *whole* relevant set high, and the dense leg does
that better on average; MRR rewards getting *one* relevant document to the very top, and the lexical
leg's exact-match behavior more often plants a relevant document at rank 1. This is a demonstrated
proposition on one corpus, not a theorem — but it is built and run before it is stated, and it makes
the lesson concrete: "system A is better" is meaningless without naming the metric.
</TheoremBlock>

## Metrics as estimators

Here is the move the rest of the evaluation layer rests on. A MAP of $0.735$ is not a property of the
dense retriever; it is the **sample mean** of forty per-query AP values, and a different forty queries
would give a different number. Every metric on this page is an *estimator* of an unknown population
quantity, and estimators have variance.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="MAP as an estimator with standard error">
Treat the per-query Average Precisions $\mathrm{AP}_1, \dots, \mathrm{AP}_Q$ as a sample with mean
$\widehat{\mathrm{MAP}}$ and sample standard deviation $\hat\sigma$. The **standard error** of the MAP
estimate is
$$
\widehat{\mathrm{SE}} = \frac{\hat\sigma}{\sqrt{Q}}, \qquad
\text{95\% CI} = \widehat{\mathrm{MAP}} \pm 1.96\,\widehat{\mathrm{SE}},
$$
and the standard error shrinks like $1/\sqrt{Q}$: quadrupling the query count halves the interval.
</TheoremBlock>

That $1/\sqrt{n}$ rate is not a heuristic; it is a
[concentration inequality](https://www.formalml.com/topics/concentration-inequalities). The per-query AP
are bounded in $[0,1]$, so Chebyshev's inequality already gives a confidence interval from the variance
alone, and the sub-Gaussian (Hoeffding) bound sharpens it — the sample mean concentrates around its
expectation at a rate controlled by the query count. The companion notebook verifies the rate
empirically: resampling the per-query AP at growing $n$, the measured standard error tracks
$\hat\sigma/\sqrt{n}$, and $\widehat{\mathrm{SE}}\cdot\sqrt{n}$ holds essentially constant.

Panel D is the payoff. It plots two legs — dense and late interaction — with their 95% intervals, and
a slider for the query count $n$. The gap in MAP is real ($0.139$), but at few queries the intervals
**overlap**: you cannot tell the systems apart. They separate only at $n = 12$. Below that, the
honest report is "indistinguishable on this query set," not "late interaction wins."

<RigorFlag>
The normal-approximation interval is itself an approximation: the per-query AP are bounded and skewed,
not Gaussian, and across legs they are *paired* (the same queries), which the independent-interval
overlap test ignores — so it is conservative. We cross-check the standard error with the
[bootstrap](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/bootstrap), which resamples the per-query scores
without the normality assumption and agrees to within roughly ten percent. Crucially, overlapping
intervals are a *crude* read; the proper instrument is a paired
[significance test](https://www.formalstatistics.com/topics/hypothesis-testing) on the per-query
differences, which is tighter and is the subject of the next topic. This one only establishes that the
variance is there and must be reckoned with.
</RigorFlag>
