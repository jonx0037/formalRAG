## From "how much to read" to "what to choose"

The previous topic ended with a verdict and an unfinished sentence. The verdict: a bigger context window
does not relax the retrieval problem, it sharpens it — answer quality peaks at the smallest context that
carries the answer and declines as you stuff the window, because the extra passages are redundant at best
and same-sector distractors at worst. The unfinished sentence: the right response is not a larger window
but a *better-chosen* one, and "the discipline of choosing that handful is what the rest of the layer is
about."

This is that discipline. Retrieval hands us a ranked list of candidate passages, more than the budget can
hold; we must pick a subset $S$ of size $k$. The naive rule is top-$k$ by relevance, and we already know
its failure mode: the highest-scoring passages of a confusable company are near-copies of one another, so
top-$k$ spends its budget re-reading the same fact. We want instead a subset that *covers* what the query
needs — relevant, but also spread out, with little redundancy. The good news is that this is not a vague
design preference. It is a clean optimization problem with a real approximation theorem, and the honest
accounting of which popular methods earn that theorem and which do not is most of what this topic is.

We will formalize three answers — submodular **coverage**, the **MMR** heuristic, and **determinantal**
diversity — on the same finance geometry the rest of the arc used, and read each through the imported
answer model so the numbers chain. The thread that ties them together is the property we have been circling
since PMI measured it in bits: diminishing returns.

## Diminishing returns, formalized

Let $V = \{1, \dots, n\}$ be the candidate pool returned by retrieval, with unit-vector embeddings
$d_1, \dots, d_n$, and let $f : 2^V \to \mathbb{R}$ score any subset by how well it serves the query. Two
properties make $f$ tractable.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Monotone submodular set functions">

The **marginal gain** of adding an element $e \notin A$ to a set $A$ is

$$
\Delta_f(e \mid A) \;=\; f(A \cup \{e\}) - f(A).
$$

$f$ is **normalized** if $f(\varnothing) = 0$, **monotone** (nondecreasing) if $A \subseteq B \Rightarrow
f(A) \le f(B)$, and **submodular** if for all $A \subseteq B \subseteq V$ and all $e \in V \setminus B$,

$$
\Delta_f(e \mid A) \;\ge\; \Delta_f(e \mid B).
$$

Submodularity is exactly *diminishing returns*: adding $e$ to a smaller set helps at least as much as
adding it to a larger one. (Equivalently, $f(S) + f(T) \ge f(S \cup T) + f(S \cap T)$ for all $S, T$.)

</TheoremBlock>

This is not a new phenomenon for us; it is a name for one we measured. The PMI topic showed that a second,
*identical* filing moves the answer belief almost not at all — far less than the first filing, and far less
than a genuinely novel one. Read that as a set function: the marginal value of the redundant passage,
added to a set that already contains its twin, is tiny. Submodularity is that inequality holding for every
pair of nested sets at once. Panel A of the lab plots the marginal gains of greedy selection and watches
them shrink — the discrete signature of a submodular objective.

## Coverage as a covering: the facility-location objective

We need a concrete $f$ that rewards covering the pool rather than re-reading one corner of it.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={2} title="Facility-location coverage">

With a nonnegative similarity $\operatorname{sim}(i, j) \ge 0$ between candidates (we use the clipped
cosine $\max(0, \langle d_i, d_j\rangle)$) and nonnegative item weights $w_i \ge 0$, the
**facility-location coverage** of a selection $S \subseteq V$ is

$$
f(S) \;=\; \sum_{i \in V} w_i \, \max_{j \in S} \operatorname{sim}(i, j),
\qquad f(\varnothing) = 0 \ \ \big(\textstyle\max_{\varnothing} := 0\big).
$$

Each candidate $i$ in the pool is "covered" by its single closest representative in $S$; the objective
rewards a set that has, for *every* candidate the query weights, *some* close member. It is the Lin–Bilmes
summarization objective and the classical facility-location function.

</TheoremBlock>

The geometric reading is the whole point: $f(S)$ asks how well the chosen handful *stands in for* the whole
pool. Two near-duplicates both close to one region cover it about as well as either alone — so the second
adds little, which is precisely the diminishing returns we are after. The $\operatorname{sim} \ge 0$
convention is load-bearing, and we will see in the proof exactly where.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Facility-location coverage is monotone submodular">

With $\operatorname{sim} \ge 0$ and $w_i \ge 0$, $f$ is normalized, monotone nondecreasing, and submodular.

**Proof.** Normalization is the convention $\max_\varnothing = 0$. For monotonicity, fix a candidate $i$;
if $A \subseteq B$ then $\max_{j \in A} \operatorname{sim}(i, j) \le \max_{j \in B} \operatorname{sim}(i,
j)$ because the maximum is taken over a superset. Multiplying by $w_i \ge 0$ and summing over $i$ gives
$f(A) \le f(B)$.

For submodularity it suffices to prove each summand $g_i(S) := \max_{j \in S} \operatorname{sim}(i, j)$ is
submodular, since a nonnegative combination of submodular functions is submodular. Fix $i$ and write
$s_j := \operatorname{sim}(i, j) \ge 0$. Let $A \subseteq B$ and $e \notin B$, and set $a = \max_{j \in A}
s_j$ and $b = \max_{j \in B} s_j$, so $a \le b$. The marginal gain of adding $e$ to a set whose current
best similarity is $m$ is

$$
\max(m, s_e) - m \;=\; (s_e - m)^{+},
\qquad (x)^{+} := \max(x, 0).
$$

Hence $\Delta g_i(e \mid A) = (s_e - a)^{+}$ and $\Delta g_i(e \mid B) = (s_e - b)^{+}$. Because
$t \mapsto (s_e - t)^{+}$ is nonincreasing in $t$ and $a \le b$, we get $(s_e - a)^{+} \ge (s_e - b)^{+}$,
i.e. $\Delta g_i(e \mid A) \ge \Delta g_i(e \mid B)$. Summing the submodular $w_i g_i$ over $i$ proves $f$
submodular. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

The engine is the hinge $t \mapsto (s_e - t)^{+}$: once a candidate is well covered (large incumbent best
match $b$), there is less room for a new near-duplicate to improve its coverage. That is diminishing
returns made geometric. (The nonnegativity of $\operatorname{sim}$ is what makes $\max_\varnothing = 0$
consistent with monotonicity and keeps the hinge argument clean; a signed cosine would break both.)

## The greedy guarantee

Maximizing $f(S)$ subject to $|S| \le k$ is NP-hard. But a one-line algorithm does remarkably well.

<TheoremBlock type="algorithm" number={1} title="Greedy selection under a budget">

Start with $S_0 = \varnothing$. For $t = 0, 1, \dots, k-1$, add the element of largest marginal gain,

$$
e_{t+1} \in \arg\max_{e \in V \setminus S_t} \Delta_f(e \mid S_t),
\qquad S_{t+1} = S_t \cup \{e_{t+1}\},
$$

and return $S_{\text{greedy}} = S_k$. (Minoux's lazy-greedy keeps a priority queue of stale marginal gains;
since submodularity guarantees gains only shrink, most are never recomputed — the same output, far faster.
The notebook checks the lazy and standard selections are identical.)

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Nemhauser–Wolsey–Fisher: greedy is a (1−1/e)-approximation">

Let $f$ be monotone, submodular, and normalized, and let $\mathrm{OPT} = \max_{|S| \le k} f(S)$ with
maximizer $S^\star$. Then

$$
f(S_{\text{greedy}}) \;\ge\; \Big(1 - \big(1 - \tfrac{1}{k}\big)^{k}\Big)\,\mathrm{OPT}
\;\ge\; \big(1 - \tfrac{1}{e}\big)\,\mathrm{OPT} \;\approx\; 0.632\,\mathrm{OPT}.
$$

**Proof.** Fix iteration $t$ with current set $S_t$, and order the optimum as $S^\star = \{o_1, \dots,
o_m\}$ with $m \le k$. By monotonicity and the exact telescoping identity for marginal gains,

$$
f(S^\star) \;\le\; f(S^\star \cup S_t)
\;=\; f(S_t) + \sum_{j=1}^{m} \Delta_f\!\big(o_j \,\big|\, S_t \cup \{o_1, \dots, o_{j-1}\}\big).
$$

Each conditioning set contains $S_t$, so submodularity bounds every term by its value at $S_t$:
$\Delta_f(o_j \mid S_t \cup \{o_1,\dots,o_{j-1}\}) \le \Delta_f(o_j \mid S_t)$. And greedy takes the best
available marginal, so $\Delta_f(o_j \mid S_t) \le f(S_{t+1}) - f(S_t)$ for every $j$. With $m \le k$ and
nonnegative marginals,

$$
f(S^\star) \;\le\; f(S_t) + k\,\big(f(S_{t+1}) - f(S_t)\big),
\quad\text{so}\quad
f(S_{t+1}) - f(S_t) \;\ge\; \tfrac{1}{k}\big(\mathrm{OPT} - f(S_t)\big).
$$

Write the residual gap $\delta_t = \mathrm{OPT} - f(S_t)$. The inequality says $\delta_{t+1} \le (1 -
\tfrac1k)\,\delta_t$, so by induction from $\delta_0 = \mathrm{OPT}$,

$$
\delta_k \;\le\; \big(1 - \tfrac1k\big)^{k}\,\mathrm{OPT},
\qquad
f(S_{\text{greedy}}) = \mathrm{OPT} - \delta_k \;\ge\; \Big(1 - \big(1 - \tfrac1k\big)^{k}\Big)\,\mathrm{OPT}.
$$

Finally $(1 - \tfrac1k)^k$ increases to $e^{-1}$ from below, so $1 - (1-\tfrac1k)^k \ge 1 - e^{-1}$.
$\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

Three remarks on what the proof uses and what it promises. Monotonicity enters twice — to write
$f(S^\star) \le f(S^\star \cup S_t)$ and to let the marginals stay nonnegative; submodularity enters in the
single inequality bounding each optimum-element's marginal by its value at $S_t$; normalization sets
$\delta_0 = \mathrm{OPT}$. Remove monotonicity and the recurrence fails — this is exactly the door MMR
walks through below. And the constant is not slack in the analysis: Feige (1998) showed that no polynomial
algorithm beats $1 - 1/e$ for max-coverage unless $\mathrm{P} = \mathrm{NP}$, and Nemhauser–Wolsey (1978)
proved the same in the value-oracle model. So $0.632$ is the best a tractable method can promise.

<ContextSelectionLaboratory client:visible />

Panel B shows the guarantee with teeth. On a small constructed instance, greedy grabs the biggest set
first and reaches $9$ of the optimal $12$ — a real gap, the realized ratio $0.75$ — yet it sits comfortably
above the $0.632\,\mathrm{OPT}$ floor. (On the smooth finance pool greedy happens to *reach* the optimum;
the bound is a worst-case promise, and the worst case is where it earns its keep.)

## MMR: the heuristic that does not earn the theorem

The field's standard diversity tool predates the submodular framing and is worth stating precisely,
because its relationship to the theorem is the topic's central honesty note.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={3} title="Maximal Marginal Relevance">

Maximal Marginal Relevance (Carbonell–Goldstein, 1998) selects sequentially: with $S$ the set chosen so
far, it adds

$$
d^{\text{next}} \in \arg\max_{d \notin S}\;
\Big[\, \lambda \, \operatorname{rel}(d, q) \;-\; (1 - \lambda) \max_{d' \in S} \operatorname{sim}(d, d') \,\Big],
$$

trading the candidate's relevance against its redundancy with the chosen set. At $\lambda = 1$ it is exactly
top-$k$; at $\lambda = 0$ it is pure dispersion.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="MMR carries no (1−1/e) guarantee">

MMR is not covered by Theorem 2, for two distinct reasons that should both be stated plainly.

1. **It is not greedy on a fixed set function.** The penalty $\max_{d' \in S} \operatorname{sim}(d, d')$
   is computed against the *evolving* chosen set $S$, not as the marginal gain $\Delta_f(d \mid S)$ of any
   single fixed $f$. There is no objective whose greedy argmax equals MMR's at every step, so a theorem
   *about greedy on one monotone submodular $f$* simply does not apply.
2. **The closest fixed objective is submodular but non-monotone.** The natural MMR-flavored set function,
   $f_{\text{MMR}}(S) = \lambda \sum_{d \in S} \operatorname{rel}(d, q) - (1 - \lambda) \sum_{\{d, d'\}
   \subseteq S} \operatorname{sim}(d, d')$, is submodular (a modular relevance term plus a negated
   supermodular pairwise term) but **not monotone**: adding a redundant passage to a large set can
   *decrease* it, as the new pairwise penalties outweigh its relevance. Monotonicity is exactly the
   hypothesis Theorem 2 requires.

Non-monotone submodular maximization has its own theory — double-greedy achieves $1/2$ unconstrained
(Buchbinder et al.) — but plain MMR is not that algorithm and inherits none of its bounds.

</TheoremBlock>

None of this makes MMR bad. It is cheap, effective, and the historical origin of diversity-aware
selection; in the lab it lands between top-$k$ and the submodular methods. The point is only that its
"$1-1/e$" is folklore, not theorem. The clean monotone-submodular backbone for a *provable* guarantee is
facility-location coverage — and, as we will now see, the log-determinant.

## Diversity as volume: determinantal point processes

There is a second, probabilistic route to diversity, and its geometry is beautiful.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={4} title="Determinantal point processes and the volume geometry">

An L-ensemble DPP over $V$ with a positive semidefinite kernel $L \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ assigns to
each subset $S$

$$
\mathcal{P}(S) \;=\; \frac{\det(L_S)}{\det(I + L)},
\qquad L_S = [L_{ij}]_{i, j \in S}.
$$

If $L_{ij} = \langle \phi_i, \phi_j \rangle$ is a Gram matrix of feature vectors, then

$$
\det(L_S) \;=\; \operatorname{Vol}^2\big(\{\phi_i\}_{i \in S}\big),
$$

the **squared volume** of the parallelepiped the selected feature vectors span. Diversity is geometric:
near-duplicate passages give near-collinear $\phi$'s, a degenerate near-zero-volume solid, hence near-zero
probability. A DPP structurally suppresses redundant subsets.

</TheoremBlock>

The kernel factors the trade-off cleanly. Write $\phi_i = q_i \, \psi_i$ with a relevance scalar $q_i > 0$
and a unit diversity feature $\psi_i$, so $L = \operatorname{diag}(q)\, S\, \operatorname{diag}(q)$ with
$S_{ij} = \langle \psi_i, \psi_j\rangle$. Then, by multilinearity of the determinant in the rows and
columns of $\operatorname{diag}(q_S)$,

$$
\det(L_S) \;=\; \Big(\prod_{i \in S} q_i^2\Big)\,\det(S_S)
\;=\; \underbrace{\textstyle\prod_{i \in S} q_i^2}_{\text{quality}} \;\times\;
\underbrace{\operatorname{Vol}^2(\{\psi_i\}_{i \in S})}_{\text{diversity}}.
$$

This is the Kulesza–Taskar quality–diversity decomposition: the probability of a set rises with the
relevance of its members and with how *spread out* they are. Panel C of the lab shows the geometry directly
— the candidate pool in two dimensions, with each method's selection highlighted, and the factorization
$\det(L_S) = q^2 \times \operatorname{Vol}^2$ read off a worked triple.

## log det is submodular, so DPP MAP inherits the guarantee

Finding the most probable set — the DPP MAP — is the natural selection rule, and it connects straight back
to Theorem 2.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={3} title="log det(I + L_S) is monotone submodular">

For positive semidefinite $L$, the function $F(S) = \log \det(I + L_S)$ is normalized, monotone
nondecreasing, and submodular.

**Proof.** Write the marginal gain of adding $e$ to $S$. Let $M = I + L_S$ (positive definite), let
$b = (L_{ie})_{i \in S}$ be the cross-column and $c = 1 + L_{ee}$. The block-determinant (Schur-complement)
identity gives

$$
\det(I + L_{S \cup \{e\}}) = \det\!\begin{pmatrix} M & b \\ b^\top & c \end{pmatrix}
= \det(M)\,\big(c - b^\top M^{-1} b\big),
\quad\text{so}\quad
\Delta_F(e \mid S) = \log\big(c - b^\top M^{-1} b\big).
$$

The quantity $c - b^\top M^{-1} b$ is the Schur complement of $M$ — in the Gaussian-process reading where
$L$ is a covariance, it is the *posterior variance* of feature $e$ after conditioning on the features in
$S$. The $+I$ shift makes $M \succeq I$, so $b^\top M^{-1} b \le b^\top b \le L_{ee} = c - 1$ and the
Schur complement is $\ge 1$; hence $\Delta_F(e \mid S) \ge 0$ and $F$ is monotone. For submodularity, the
Schur complement is *nonincreasing* under additional conditioning — conditioning on more elements can only
reduce posterior variance (the "information never hurts" fact for Gaussians, the Loewner-order monotonicity
of the Schur complement of a larger principal block). So $A \subseteq B$ gives $\operatorname{Var}(e \mid
A) \ge \operatorname{Var}(e \mid B)$, and since $\log$ is increasing, $\Delta_F(e \mid A) \ge \Delta_F(e
\mid B)$. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

Because $F$ is monotone submodular and normalized, greedy maximization of $\log \det(I + L_S)$ under
$|S| \le k$ inherits the $(1 - 1/e)$ guarantee of Theorem 2 — and that greedy is precisely fast greedy DPP
MAP (Chen et al., 2018), each step a Schur-complement (Cholesky rank-one) update. The notebook reuses the
*same* greedy maximizer for facility location and for $\log\det(I + L_S)$, differing only in the value
oracle.

<TheoremBlock type="corollary" number={1} title="Certified diversity, with an honest boundary">

Greedy on $\log\det(I + L_S)$ returns a set within $1 - 1/e$ of the optimum of *that* surrogate. The exact
$k$-DPP MAP — $\arg\max_{|S| = k} \det(L_S)$ — is **NP-hard**, so the certified object is the monotone
surrogate, not the raw determinant. The $+I$ regularization is also what keeps the objective finite when a
near-duplicate makes $L_S$ singular (raw $\log\det(L_S) \to -\infty$). The notebook handles that singular
case with a small ridge.

</TheoremBlock>

## The objective we actually want, and why it is not submodular

Coverage and $\log\det$ are the clean backbones. But the objective most faithful to the rest of this layer
is neither — it is the answer-information gain, and it does not behave.

<TheoremBlock type="remark" title="Information gain is the honest hinge">

The objective we would *like* to maximize is

$$
f_{\text{IG}}(S) \;=\; I(A; D_S \mid Q) \;=\; H(A \mid Q) - H(A \mid Q, D_S),
$$

the multi-document generalization of the bits PMI measured. It is monotone and normalized. But mutual
information of a *set* of observations with a target is **not submodular in general**. The cleanest
counterexample is a synergistic pair: let $A = D_1 \oplus D_2$ with $D_1, D_2$ independent fair bits. Each
observation alone is useless — $I(A; D_1) = I(A; D_2) = 0$ — yet together they determine $A$, so
$I(A; D_1, D_2) = 1$ bit. The marginal gain of $D_2$ therefore *increases* with conditioning,

$$
\Delta(D_2 \mid \varnothing) = 0 \;<\; \Delta(D_2 \mid \{D_1\}) = 1,
$$

the opposite of diminishing returns. This is not only an abstract example: on our finance corpus the
numerical submodularity witness for $f_{\text{IG}}$ is clearly negative ($\approx -0.70$), while
facility-location coverage's is $\approx 0$. Submodularity of information gain holds only under structural
assumptions, the cleanest being conditional independence of the observations given the target
(Krause–Guestrin, 2005). So we optimize coverage — which *is* submodular and earns Theorem 2 — and treat
information gain as the motivating-but-caveated quantity it is.

</TheoremBlock>

It is worth keeping the ledger explicit, because the differentiator of this topic is the honesty about it:

| Object | Status |
| --- | --- |
| Facility-location coverage is monotone submodular | **Theorem** (proved, Theorem 1) |
| Greedy gets $1-1/e$ on monotone submodular | **Theorem** (proved, Theorem 2) |
| $\log\det(I + L_S)$ is monotone submodular, so greedy DPP MAP gets $1-1/e$ | **Theorem** (proved, Theorem 3) |
| Exact $k$-DPP MAP | **NP-hard** (cited) |
| MMR has a $1-1/e$ guarantee | **False / folklore** (Proposition 1) |
| Information gain $I(A; D_S \mid Q)$ is submodular | **Conditional** — only under conditional independence |
| Diverse selection beats top-$k$ at fixed $k$ on a redundant pool | **Demonstrated** (run, pinned) |

That last row is the payoff, and it is the reason any of this matters for an answer.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Diversity-aware selection beats top-k at a fixed budget (demonstrated)">

We build, for each query, a candidate pool with the redundancy structure that makes selection bite: a tight
cluster of sector-generic near-duplicates that support the gold company *and* its confusable peer about
equally — and carry the *highest* query relevance — plus a single, less-relevant **disambiguating** passage
near the gold prototype, plus low-relevance distractors. Reading each method's selection through the
imported `answer_posterior_topk` under an equal attention budget, at budget $k = 3$:

$$
Q_{\text{top-}k} \approx 0.38 \;<\; Q_{\text{MMR}} \approx 0.44 \;<\;
Q_{\text{facility}} \approx 0.54 \;\approx\; Q_{\text{DPP}} \approx 0.55,
$$

where $Q = \mathbb{E}_q\big[p(a^\star \mid q, S)\big]$ is the posterior mass on the true company.
Top-$k$ spends all three slots inside the redundant cluster, where the generics support gold and peer
equally, and leaves the answer split; coverage and DPP reach the disambiguator and resolve it. The notebook
asserts the observed ordering and the seed-independent coverage contrast.

</TheoremBlock>

This is a *demonstrated* proposition, pinned to the run, not a universal law. The mechanism is the honest
one: diversity helps not for its own sake but because the disambiguating evidence is *less* query-similar
than the redundant cluster, so only a coverage- or quality-aware selector reaches it. On a pool with no
redundancy, coverage and relevance coincide and the gap vanishes — the correct boundary condition. One
caveat the lab makes visible: diversity raises the mass on the truth while *spreading* the remaining mass
across more companies, so the answer entropy actually rises; quality here is mass-on-truth, not low entropy,
and we do not claim the latter.

<RigorFlag>

Four honesty notes. First, MMR has no $(1-1/e)$ guarantee — its penalty is taken against the evolving
chosen set, and the closest fixed objective is submodular but non-monotone, exactly the hypothesis the
theorem needs. Second, information gain is the objective we actually want, but it is not submodular: the
witness goes clearly negative on this corpus, and a synergistic XOR pair makes the marginal gain increase
with conditioning; it is submodular only under conditional independence (Krause–Guestrin), so coverage is
the backbone that earns the theorem. Third, exact $k$-DPP MAP is NP-hard; the certified object is greedy on
the monotone surrogate $\log\det(I + L_S)$, not the raw determinant, and the $+I$ also tames the
near-duplicate singularity. Fourth, the payoff $Q(k)$ is exact for a synthetic von Mises–Fisher softmax
answer model (the same stand-in the arc has used throughout), illustrative of a real generator; the
headline is a comparison *across methods at a fixed budget*, robust across seeds — not a claim that some
interior $k^\star$ is optimal, which is seed-fragile and not asserted.

</RigorFlag>

## What context selection trades, in one line

At a fixed budget, choose for coverage and diversity, not relevance alone: the greedy choice is provably
within $1 - 1/e$ of optimal for the right (monotone submodular) objective, MMR is the cheap heuristic that
does not earn that bound, a DPP makes diversity the squared volume of the selected vectors, and on a
redundant pool every diversity-aware rule beats top-$k$ on the answer it produces — because top-$k$ re-reads
the same fact while a covering set buys the disambiguating evidence.

<FinanceCaseStudy title="Which 10-K passages should enter the window?" modality={["text", "pdf"]}>

A multi-part analyst question about a company touches several distinct sub-claims — revenue recognition, a
specific risk disclosure, forward guidance. Retrieval returns a thick candidate set: many near-duplicate
passages of the most query-typical section (the ones a dual encoder scores highest, precisely because they
are sector-boilerplate that several comparable companies share), plus the one paragraph that actually
distinguishes *this* company's treatment, ranked lower because it is idiosyncratic and less query-similar.
Top-$k$ by relevance fills the window with the boilerplate and never reaches the distinguishing paragraph;
the generator answers with the sector consensus, not the company's actual position. Facility-location
coverage spends each slot on a passage that covers a *different* sub-claim, the DPP's volume term suppresses
the near-duplicate filings, and the disambiguating paragraph makes the cut. The discipline is not to read
more of the filing but to choose the few passages that, together, cover the question — and to leave the
duplicates out. When the candidate pool itself grows — when one passage's answer raises a new question —
selection becomes a *search* over an evolving evidence space, which is where multi-hop and iterative
retrieval take over.

</FinanceCaseStudy>
