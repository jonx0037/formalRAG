## Overview & motivation

The [previous topic](/topics/vector-quantization-lloyd-max) left us at a wall. A single flat codebook of $k = 256$ codewords compresses a $256$-dimensional embedding to a single byte, but on the synthetic finance cloud it retained only about $53\%$ of the top-$10$ nearest-neighbor recall — and a flat codebook cannot do much better cheaply. To halve its distortion it must roughly square its codebook size, and a codebook of $k$ codewords needs $k$ trainable centroids: bounded above by the number of training points and, well before that, intractable to store or search. A flat codebook simply cannot reach the bit budgets that fine compression requires.

Product quantization gives up on the single codebook. Split each vector into $m$ disjoint subvectors, quantize each subspace *independently* with its own small codebook, and represent the vector by the tuple of $m$ sub-centroid indices. Each sub-codebook is trained by exactly the Lloyd's k-means of the previous topic — product quantization is that algorithm run $m$ times, in parallel, on the coordinate blocks. The reward is a codebook that is the *product* of the sub-codebooks: with $m = 8$ subspaces and $k^\* = 256$ codewords each, an effective $256^8 = 2^{64}$ codewords represented by only $8 \times 256 = 2048$ stored centroids. The whole topic is the mathematics of that trade — why the independent training is correct, what the product buys, how retrieval stays fast, and, honestly, what the factorization costs.

## The product quantizer

Fix a source $X \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and a number of subspaces $m$ dividing $d$, with subspace dimension $d^\* = d/m$.

<TheoremBlock type="definition" number={1} title="Product quantizer">

Write $x = (x^1, \dots, x^m)$ with each **subvector** $x^j \in \mathbb{R}^{d^\*}$ the $j$-th block of coordinates. Each subspace has its own **sub-codebook** $\mathcal{C}^j = \{c^j_1, \dots, c^j_{k^\*}\} \subset \mathbb{R}^{d^\*}$ and sub-quantizer $q_j(x^j) = \arg\min_i \lVert x^j - c^j_i\rVert^2$. The **product quantizer** encodes $x$ as the tuple of indices $(q_1(x^1), \dots, q_m(x^m))$ — a **code** of $m \log_2 k^\*$ bits — and decodes by concatenation,

$$
Q(x) = \bigl(c^1_{q_1(x^1)}, \; \dots, \; c^m_{q_m(x^m)}\bigr).
$$

Its reproduction values form the **effective codebook** $\mathcal{C} = \mathcal{C}^1 \times \cdots \times \mathcal{C}^m$, the Cartesian product of the sub-codebooks, with $(k^\*)^m$ elements.

</TheoremBlock>

The single structural choice — quantize the blocks *separately* — is what we must justify. It is not obvious that independent sub-quantizers minimize the joint distortion, nor that a product codebook is a good codebook. The next theorem settles the first question completely.

## The additive distortion decomposition

The distortion of $Q$ is $D = \mathbb{E}\lVert X - Q(X)\rVert^2$, the same squared-error objective as before. Its special structure under a product quantizer is the whole reason PQ works.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={1} title="Additive distortion decomposition">

For a product quantizer, the squared reconstruction error splits over subspaces,

$$
\lVert x - Q(x)\rVert^2 = \sum_{j=1}^{m} \bigl\lVert x^j - c^j_{q_j(x^j)}\bigr\rVert^2,
$$

so the total distortion is the sum of the per-subspace distortions, $D = \sum_{j=1}^m D_j$ with $D_j = \mathbb{E}\lVert X^j - c^j_{q_j(X^j)}\rVert^2$. Consequently the codebook minimizing $D$ is obtained by minimizing each $D_j$ **independently**, and each sub-problem is exactly the Lloyd–Max quantizer of the previous topic on the $j$-th block.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

The squared Euclidean norm is a sum over coordinates, and the coordinates partition into the $m$ disjoint blocks. Since $Q(x)$ restricted to block $j$ is $c^j_{q_j(x^j)}$ and depends only on the coordinates of $x$ in block $j$,

$$
\lVert x - Q(x)\rVert^2 = \sum_{i=1}^{d}\bigl(x_i - Q(x)_i\bigr)^2 = \sum_{j=1}^m \sum_{i \in \text{block } j}\bigl(x_i - c^j_{q_j,i}\bigr)^2 = \sum_{j=1}^m \bigl\lVert x^j - c^j_{q_j(x^j)}\bigr\rVert^2.
$$

Taking expectations gives $D = \sum_j D_j$. The decision variables are disjoint — the sub-codebook $\mathcal{C}^j$ and the encoder $q_j$ affect only the term $D_j$ and appear in no other — so the sum is minimized by minimizing each term over its own variables, with no coupling. Minimizing $D_j = \mathbb{E}\lVert X^j - c^j_{q_j(X^j)}\rVert^2$ over a $k^\*$-codeword quantizer of the $d^\*$-dimensional subvector is precisely the vector-quantization problem of the previous topic, solved by Lloyd's two conditions. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is the theorem the laboratory's first panel makes visible: the two per-subspace bars sum to the total, exactly, for every point. It is also the practical recipe — train $m$ separate k-means, one per block — and the reason the companion code *imports* the previous topic's Lloyd implementation rather than writing a new one. Product quantization adds no new optimization; it only factors the old one.

## What the product buys

The decomposition explains correctness; the product explains the win.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={1} title="Effective codebook versus storage">

A product quantizer with $m$ subspaces and $k^\*$ codewords each represents $(k^\*)^m$ distinct reproduction vectors — the size of the product codebook — while storing only $m k^\*$ centroids and encoding each vector in $m \log_2 k^\*$ bits. The per-vector distance to a query costs $O(m)$ table lookups (next section), against $O(d)$ for an exact distance.

</TheoremBlock>

The numbers are stark. At $m = 8$, $k^\* = 256$, a product quantizer encodes each vector in $8 \times 8 = 64$ bits — eight bytes — and its effective codebook holds $256^8 = 2^{64}$ codewords. A flat codebook with $2^{64}$ codewords is a fantasy: it would need $2^{64}$ trained centroids, impossible to store, train, or search, and unreachable in principle from any finite training set since a codebook can have at most as many codewords as training points. The product quantizer reaches the same representational resolution from $2048$ stored centroids. That gap — exponential codebook from linear storage — is the entire value proposition, and we will price it honestly in two sections.

## Asymmetric distance computation

A compressed index is useful only if we can search it. Given a query $q$ and database vectors stored as PQ codes, we need the distance $\lVert q - Q(x)\rVert^2$ to each. The block structure turns it into a table lookup.

<TheoremBlock type="theorem" number={2} title="Asymmetric distance is exact and a table lookup">

For an un-quantized query $q$ and a database vector with code $(q_1, \dots, q_m)$,

$$
\lVert q - Q(x)\rVert^2 = \sum_{j=1}^m \bigl\lVert q^j - c^j_{q_j(x^j)}\bigr\rVert^2 = \sum_{j=1}^m \mathrm{LUT}[j]\bigl[q_j(x^j)\bigr],
$$

where $\mathrm{LUT}[j][i] = \lVert q^j - c^j_i\rVert^2$ is an $m \times k^\*$ table of squared sub-distances built once per query. Each database distance is then $m$ table lookups and adds — $O(m)$, independent of $d$ beyond the one-time table build.

</TheoremBlock>

<TheoremBlock type="proof">

$Q(x)$ is a genuine vector in $\mathbb{R}^d$, and $q$ is untouched, so the additive decomposition of Theorem 1 applies verbatim with $q$ in place of $x$: the squared norm splits over the blocks, and on block $j$ the reproduction is $c^j_{q_j(x^j)}$, giving $\sum_j \lVert q^j - c^j_{q_j}\rVert^2$. Each term depends on the database vector only through its index $q_j$ in subspace $j$, so once $\mathrm{LUT}[j][i] = \lVert q^j - c^j_i\rVert^2$ is precomputed for all $k^\*$ sub-centroids, the term is the single entry $\mathrm{LUT}[j][q_j]$. The sum is exact — the table memoizes, it does not approximate. $\blacksquare$

</TheoremBlock>

This is **asymmetric** distance: the query stays in full precision while the database is quantized. The third panel of the laboratory shows it directly — a database vector's code highlights $m$ cells of the table whose sum *is* the exact distance to its reconstruction. The companion harness checks this equality to machine precision on the finance cloud.

The alternative is **symmetric** distance (SDC), which quantizes the query too and reads from a precomputed $k^\* \times k^\*$ table of inter-centroid distances. SDC is cheaper to precompute but adds the query's own quantization error, and that costs accuracy.

<TheoremBlock type="proposition" number={2} title="Asymmetric beats symmetric in the mean">

Averaged over queries and database vectors, the asymmetric estimate has smaller squared distance-estimation error than the symmetric one, $\mathbb{E}[(\hat d_{\mathrm{ADC}} - d_{\mathrm{true}})^2] < \mathbb{E}[(\hat d_{\mathrm{SDC}} - d_{\mathrm{true}})^2]$, and yields recall at least as high. The inequality is in expectation, not per pair: individual query–database pairs can reverse.

</TheoremBlock>

On the finance cloud the gap is large — the companion code measures a mean squared distance-estimation error of about $13$ for ADC against $455$ for SDC, and recall@10 of $0.75$ against $0.62$ — because keeping the query exact removes an entire source of error. We state the inequality honestly as a statement about the mean, since SDC can accidentally land closer on a given pair; what is guaranteed is the average and the recall.

## Scalability, honestly

It is tempting to say product quantization "beats" a flat codebook. It does not, and the honest version is more interesting.

<TheoremBlock type="remark">

At any bit budget $B$ where a flat codebook of $k = 2^B$ codewords is *trainable*, the flat codebook is unconstrained while the product quantizer's reconstruction is confined to the product set $\mathcal{C}^1 \times \cdots \times \mathcal{C}^m$ — a strict subset of all $2^B$-codeword codebooks. So at equal trainable bits, flat-VQ distortion is at most PQ's; PQ pays a product-separability constraint. The companion code confirms this at $4$, $6$, and $8$ bits on the finance cloud. PQ's advantage is *reachability*: a flat codebook needs $2^B$ centroids — capped at $k \le n$ training points and intractable past roughly sixteen to twenty bits — while PQ reaches rate $B = m \log_2 k^\*$ at $m k^\*$ stored centroids. It attains distortion and recall a flat codebook cannot reach at any feasible storage.

</TheoremBlock>

The scalability panel of the laboratory plots this exactly. The flat ceiling is the $m = 1$ point: a single $256$-codeword codebook at $8$ bits, recall $0.53$ — the same number the previous topic baked, on the same cloud. As $m$ grows the rate grows and recall climbs to $0.56$ at $16$ bits, $0.64$ at $32$ bits, $0.75$ at $64$ bits — each a budget a flat codebook cannot train. The per-subspace Zador exponent of the previous topic is why each subspace needs only modest $k^\*$, and the product is why the budgets compose. The gain is real, but it is a gain in *reach*, not in quality per bit, and the laboratory labels it as such.

## Finance case study

<FinanceCaseStudy modality={["text"]}>

We return to the exact $256$-dimensional synthetic finance cloud of the [previous topic](/topics/vector-quantization-lloyd-max) — six topical groups on a low-rank-plus-noise manifold — so the comparison is like-for-like. There, a flat $256$-codeword codebook compressed each vector to one byte and retained $52.8\%$ recall@10, the best a flat codebook can do at that storage. Now we product-quantize the same cloud and measure the rate–distortion frontier (the companion code re-derives the flat ceiling rather than hardcoding it, to prove the cloud is identical):

- a raw vector is $256 \times 32 = 8192$ bits; at $m = 8$, $k^\* = 256$ the PQ code is **$64$ bits — eight bytes, a $128\times$ smaller code** — from $2048$ stored centroids, an effective $2^{64}$ codewords;
- **recall@10 climbs from $52.8\%$ (the flat $8$-bit ceiling) to $\mathbf{75.2\%}$ at $64$ bits**, with the intermediate rates $55.6\%$ at $16$ bits and $63.7\%$ at $32$ bits;
- the asymmetric distance to each database vector is $\mathbf{8}$ table lookups rather than a $256$-dimensional dot product, the one-time $8 \times 256$ table amortized over the whole database.

The shape is the message. PQ does not magically retrieve perfectly — the cloud has a genuine noise floor, and $75\%$ at eight bytes reflects it honestly — but it reaches a recall that a flat codebook, frozen at its $8$-bit ceiling, simply cannot. This is the structure a production index exploits: the inverted-file index (IVFADC) adds a coarse Voronoi partition on top of these codes, and *optimized product quantization* — the next topic — rotates the space first to spend the bits better. Both are built on the codes this topic defines.

</FinanceCaseStudy>

A candid note, in the spirit of the series: the cloud is a deterministic synthetic low-rank-plus-noise construction, CPU-only and reproducible to the decimal, and the distortion and recall are measured on it; it is not a trained encoder. Because the previous topic used the same generator, the flat ceiling and the PQ numbers are measured on one cloud, which is what makes the comparison fair.

## The honest catch: the product constraint and the rotation that fixes it

The product-separability constraint has a name and a cure. Product quantization treats the subspaces as independent — it allocates the same $k^\*$ to each and quantizes each in ignorance of the others. When the subspaces are genuinely independent and carry equal variance, that loses almost nothing. When they are *correlated*, or when their variances are uneven, PQ wastes bits: a high-variance subspace is under-quantized while a low-variance one is over-quantized, and correlation that a joint codebook would exploit is thrown away. The residual distortion PQ pays over a full joint codebook is governed by the mutual information across subspaces.

The fix is a rotation. Apply an orthogonal $R$ to every vector before splitting; since $R$ preserves distances, the retrieval problem is unchanged, but the *axes* along which we split are now ours to choose. The companion code makes the effect concrete on a deliberately variance-imbalanced cloud, where the coordinate variances decay so the contiguous subspaces carry very unequal variance. Raw product quantization there has distortion about $85.6$. A *naive* rotation that only [aligns to the principal axes](https://formalcalculus.com/topics/linear-algebra) barely helps — distortion $83.3$ — because it concentrates all the variance into the first subspace and starves the rest. But a rotation that *balances* the variance across subspaces, spreading the high-variance principal directions evenly, cuts the distortion to about $15.4$, a several-fold reduction. Choosing the rotation that minimizes PQ distortion is exactly **optimized product quantization** (Ge et al., 2014), and it is the next topic. We show the direction of the effect, not a universal number — on the finance cloud, whose random basis already spreads variance fairly evenly, the gain is smaller — but the mechanism is the one OPQ formalizes.

## Honest caveats

<RigorFlag>

Five qualifications keep the accounting honest:

1. **PQ does not beat a trainable flat codebook at equal bits.** The product reconstruction is a strict subset of an unconstrained codebook's, so flat-VQ distortion is at most PQ's at any rate the flat codebook can train (verified at $4$, $6$, $8$ bits). PQ's win is reaching budgets a flat codebook cannot ($k = 2^B \le n$, intractable past ~$16$–$20$ bits) — a gain in reach, not in quality per bit. We never flip the inequality.
2. **ADC beats SDC in the mean, not per pair.** Keeping the query exact removes a query-quantization term from the *expected* squared error and raises recall; individual pairs can reverse. We assert the mean error and recall ordering, not a pairwise guarantee.
3. **Independence is an assumption.** PQ ignores cross-subspace correlation (= mutual information across subspaces) and unequal per-subspace variance under equal bits; a variance-balancing rotation lowers distortion, which is what optimized product quantization learns. We show the direction on an imbalanced cloud, not a magic number.
4. **Equal bit allocation is optimal only for equal-variance subspaces.** Uneven variance calls for unequal allocation (reverse water-filling); standard PQ's equal split is a convention we flag, not a theorem.
5. **The per-subspace quantizer inherits Lloyd's caveats, and the data is synthetic.** Each sub-codebook is a local optimum of k-means; the finance embeddings are a low-rank-plus-noise stand-in, not a trained encoder, with all numbers measured on the same cloud as the previous topic.

</RigorFlag>
